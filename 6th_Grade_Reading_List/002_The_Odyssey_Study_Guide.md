# The Odyssey Study Guide

Text: *The Children's Homer: The Adventures of Odysseus and the Tale of Troy*  
by Padraic Colum (Author), Willy Pogany (Illustrator)

## How to Use This Guide

This guide is for students reading Padraic Colum's retelling of the Greek stories about Troy, Odysseus, and the long journey home. Use it as a reading companion: pause after each section, answer a few questions in writing, and be ready to discuss the larger questions aloud.

Keep a reading notebook with four sections:

1. **People and Places**: important characters, gods, monsters, and lands.
2. **Plot Notes**: what happened in each episode.
3. **Big Ideas**: courage, pride, loyalty, cleverness, temptation, home, and fate.
4. **Questions**: anything confusing, surprising, or worth discussing.

## Before You Read

### What Kind of Story Is This?

*The Odyssey* is an epic: a long adventure story about heroic deeds, dangerous journeys, gods, monsters, honor, and homecoming. The original poem was told by Homer in ancient Greece. Colum's version retells the story for younger readers and also includes material from the Trojan War.

### Important Background

- **Troy** was a famous city in ancient stories. Greek armies fought there for ten years.
- **Odysseus** was one of the Greek leaders in the Trojan War. He is famous less for brute strength than for cleverness.
- After the war, Odysseus tries to sail home to **Ithaca**, but the journey takes ten more years.
- The Greek gods often help or hinder human beings. Their favor matters, but human choices still matter too.

### Essential Question

What makes Odysseus a hero: his courage, his intelligence, his endurance, his love of home, or something else?

## Main Characters

| Character | Who They Are | What to Watch For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Odysseus / Ulysses** | King of Ithaca; Greek hero; husband of Penelope; father of Telemachus | Cleverness, pride, patience, leadership, longing for home |
| **Penelope** | Odysseus's wife | Loyalty, patience, intelligence, strength under pressure |
| **Telemachus** | Odysseus's son | Growth, courage, learning how to act like a prince |
| **Athena** | Goddess of wisdom and war strategy | Guidance, protection, wisdom, disguises |
| **Poseidon** | God of the sea | Anger, revenge, obstacles on the journey |
| **Zeus** | King of the gods | Justice, fate, divine order |
| **Circe** | Enchantress who changes men into animals | Temptation, magic, danger, hospitality |
| **Calypso** | Nymph who keeps Odysseus on her island | Comfort versus duty, delayed homecoming |
| **Cyclops / Polyphemus** | One-eyed giant, son of Poseidon | Savagery, clever escape, consequences of pride |
| **The Sirens** | Creatures whose song lures sailors to death | Temptation, self-control, curiosity |
| **Scylla and Charybdis** | Sea monsters / dangers Odysseus must pass | Hard choices, leadership under danger |
| **The Suitors** | Men trying to marry Penelope and take Odysseus's place | Greed, disrespect, disorder in the household |

## Important Places

- **Ithaca**: Odysseus's home kingdom.
- **Troy**: the city where the Greeks fought a ten-year war.
- **The Sea**: the place of wandering, danger, testing, and uncertainty.
- **The Cyclops's Cave**: where Odysseus's cleverness saves his men, but his pride creates a new enemy.
- **Circe's Island**: a place of enchantment and delay.
- **The Underworld**: where Odysseus learns from the dead.
- **Calypso's Island**: a beautiful prison that tests Odysseus's desire for home.

## Major Themes

### Homecoming

Odysseus is not simply looking for safety. He wants to return to his wife, son, kingdom, and proper place in the world. Ask: why is home worth so much suffering?

### Cleverness and Wisdom

Odysseus often wins by thinking clearly under pressure. But cleverness can become pride if it is not joined with self-control.

### Pride and Consequences

When Odysseus reveals his name to the Cyclops, he wins fame but brings Poseidon's anger on himself and his men. Ask: when does confidence become dangerous pride?

### Hospitality

In the ancient Greek world, hosts and guests had serious duties. Good hosts feed and protect strangers; good guests show respect. Many episodes test this rule.

### Loyalty

Penelope remains faithful. Telemachus seeks news of his father. Odysseus struggles toward home. The story asks what loyalty costs and why it matters.

### Temptation

Odysseus and his men face many temptations: food, sleep, comfort, curiosity, forgetfulness, and glory. Some temptations look harmless at first.

### Fate, the Gods, and Human Choice

The gods influence events, but people still make choices. A good discussion question is whether Odysseus's troubles are mostly caused by fate, by the gods, or by his own decisions.

## Reading Roadmap

Use these sections as checkpoints. Depending on the edition, chapter titles may differ, so follow the episode names.

### 1. The Trojan War Background

**What happens:** The story begins with the world of Greek heroes and the war at Troy. Students meet the heroic code: honor, courage, loyalty, and fame.

**Notice:**

- Why the Greeks and Trojans fight.
- How heroes are praised.
- How war affects families and kingdoms.

**Study Questions:**

1. What qualities make someone seem heroic in the Trojan War stories?
2. Which matters more to the warriors: survival, honor, loyalty, or fame?
3. How does the war prepare us to understand Odysseus?
4. What does the story suggest about the cost of glory?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Is a great warrior automatically a great man?
- Can a person win fame and still lose something important?

### 2. The Wooden Horse and the Fall of Troy

**What happens:** Odysseus helps win the war through strategy rather than strength alone.

**Notice:**

- The difference between courage and cleverness.
- The danger of deception.
- The Greek admiration for cunning plans.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why is the wooden horse such an effective plan?
2. What does this episode reveal about Odysseus's mind?
3. Is trickery honorable in war? Explain your answer.
4. How might the Trojans have avoided disaster?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Is it better to be strong or clever?
- When, if ever, is deception acceptable?

### 3. Odysseus Begins His Journey Home

**What happens:** After Troy falls, Odysseus sails for Ithaca, but the journey becomes long and dangerous.

**Notice:**

- How quickly victory turns into trouble.
- How Odysseus leads his men.
- How the sea becomes a place of testing.

**Study Questions:**

1. What does Odysseus want most after the war?
2. What makes leading a crew difficult?
3. How do Odysseus's men help him? How do they hurt the journey?
4. What early signs show that the trip home will not be easy?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Why might going home after a great struggle be harder than expected?
- What makes a good leader when people are tired, afraid, or hungry?

### 4. The Lotus-Eaters

**What happens:** Odysseus's men meet people who eat the lotus, a plant that makes them forget home.

**Notice:**

- The danger is not violence, but forgetfulness.
- Odysseus must force his men away for their own good.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why is the lotus dangerous?
2. Why does Odysseus refuse to let his men stay?
3. What does this episode teach about comfort and duty?
4. What modern distractions can make people forget their goals?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Can something pleasant still be dangerous?
- Is Odysseus right to drag his men back to the ships?

### 5. The Cyclops

**What happens:** Odysseus and his men are trapped by Polyphemus, a one-eyed giant. Odysseus escapes through courage and cleverness, but then foolishly reveals his name.

**Notice:**

- Polyphemus violates hospitality.
- Odysseus uses the false name "Nobody."
- Odysseus's pride creates lasting trouble.

**Study Questions:**

1. How does Polyphemus behave as a bad host?
2. Why does Odysseus say his name is "Nobody"?
3. What parts of the escape plan show Odysseus's intelligence?
4. Why is it a mistake for Odysseus to reveal his real name?
5. What does this episode teach about pride?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Was Odysseus brave, reckless, or both?
- Should a leader risk danger for fame?

### 6. Aeolus and the Bag of Winds

**What happens:** Aeolus gives Odysseus a bag containing the winds. Odysseus's men open it out of suspicion or greed, blowing the ships away from home.

**Notice:**

- The crew's lack of trust.
- How close they come to Ithaca.
- How one foolish action can undo great progress.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why do the men open the bag?
2. What does this reveal about their trust in Odysseus?
3. How does Odysseus react to failure?
4. What lesson does this episode teach about self-control?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Is Odysseus responsible for his men's mistake?
- Why is trust important between a leader and followers?

### 7. Circe's Island

**What happens:** Circe turns Odysseus's men into swine. With help from Hermes, Odysseus resists her magic and eventually wins her aid.

**Notice:**

- Magic as a test of wisdom.
- The men reduced to animal form.
- The danger of staying too long in comfort.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why is it fitting that the men are changed into swine?
2. How does Odysseus overcome Circe's power?
3. What does Circe eventually teach or provide?
4. Why is it dangerous for Odysseus and his men to remain on the island?

**Conversation Starters:**

- What does it mean for a person to act like an animal?
- Can comfort be as dangerous as hardship?

### 8. The Underworld

**What happens:** Odysseus visits the land of the dead to seek wisdom for the journey ahead.

**Notice:**

- Odysseus must face sorrow and mortality.
- The dead reveal truth.
- Heroism looks different when seen from the underworld.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why must Odysseus visit the dead?
2. What does he learn there that he could not learn elsewhere?
3. How does meeting the dead change the mood of the story?
4. What does this episode teach about life, death, and wisdom?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Why do stories often send heroes into dark or frightening places?
- Does wisdom usually come easily or painfully?

### 9. The Sirens

**What happens:** Odysseus wants to hear the Sirens' song, but he must be tied to the mast so he cannot follow it to destruction.

**Notice:**

- Odysseus is curious, but he knows his own weakness.
- He survives by planning ahead.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why is the Sirens' song dangerous?
2. Why does Odysseus want to hear it anyway?
3. How does Odysseus protect himself and his men?
4. What does this episode teach about self-knowledge?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Is curiosity always good?
- Why is it wise to make a plan before temptation arrives?

### 10. Scylla and Charybdis

**What happens:** Odysseus must steer between two deadly dangers. He cannot avoid all loss.

**Notice:**

- Some choices are not between good and bad, but between bad and worse.
- Leadership sometimes means choosing the least terrible option.

**Study Questions:**

1. What makes this choice so difficult?
2. Why can Odysseus not save everyone?
3. How should a leader act when every option has a cost?
4. What does this episode show about the limits of heroism?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Can a choice be right even if it leads to suffering?
- What should courage look like when victory is impossible?

### 11. The Cattle of the Sun

**What happens:** Odysseus's men disobey a warning and kill the sacred cattle of the Sun. Their disobedience brings disaster.

**Notice:**

- Hunger and exhaustion weaken judgment.
- Warnings are ignored.
- The crew's choices have final consequences.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why are the men tempted to kill the cattle?
2. What warnings do they ignore?
3. Is their choice understandable, unforgivable, or both?
4. Why does Odysseus survive when the others do not?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Does suffering excuse disobedience?
- Why do people ignore warnings they know are serious?

### 12. Calypso's Island

**What happens:** Calypso keeps Odysseus on her island and offers comfort, beauty, and even immortality, but Odysseus still longs for home.

**Notice:**

- Odysseus chooses a mortal life with Penelope over endless ease.
- Home is shown as more valuable than pleasure.

**Study Questions:**

1. What does Calypso offer Odysseus?
2. Why does Odysseus still want to leave?
3. What does this choice reveal about his character?
4. Why might an ordinary home be better than a perfect island?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Would immortality be a gift or a trap?
- What makes a place truly home?

### 13. Telemachus and Penelope in Ithaca

**What happens:** While Odysseus is away, Penelope resists the suitors and Telemachus begins to grow into his responsibilities.

**Notice:**

- Penelope's intelligence and patience.
- Telemachus's growth from boy to young man.
- The disorder caused by the suitors.

**Study Questions:**

1. What problems do the suitors create in Odysseus's house?
2. How does Penelope delay them?
3. How does Telemachus change during the story?
4. Why is Ithaca not truly at peace until Odysseus returns?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Is Penelope heroic? Why or why not?
- What does it mean for Telemachus to become worthy of his father?

### 14. Odysseus Returns in Disguise

**What happens:** Odysseus returns to Ithaca disguised as a beggar. He tests the people in his household before revealing himself.

**Notice:**

- Odysseus practices patience.
- Disguise reveals people's true character.
- The hero must master his anger before acting.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why does Odysseus return in disguise?
2. What does he learn by watching people secretly?
3. Why is patience important in this part of the story?
4. How does the disguise change the way others treat him?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Do people reveal their character by how they treat the weak or poor?
- Why might a hero need humility as much as strength?

### 15. The Bow and the Defeat of the Suitors

**What happens:** Penelope sets a test with Odysseus's bow. Odysseus reveals himself and punishes the suitors.

**Notice:**

- The bow represents Odysseus's rightful place.
- The suitors fail because they do not deserve what they seek.
- Justice arrives after long patience.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why is the bow a good test?
2. Why can Odysseus string the bow when the suitors cannot?
3. Is the punishment of the suitors just? Explain carefully.
4. How does this scene restore order to the household?

**Conversation Starters:**

- What is the difference between revenge and justice?
- Why do stories often end by restoring a rightful king, father, or ruler?

### 16. Reunion and Restoration

**What happens:** Odysseus is reunited with Penelope and Telemachus. His homecoming is complete when trust, identity, and order are restored.

**Notice:**

- Penelope does not accept Odysseus too quickly.
- Their marriage is based on memory, loyalty, and shared knowledge.
- The journey ends not only with arrival, but with recognition.

**Study Questions:**

1. Why does Penelope test Odysseus?
2. What proves Odysseus's identity?
3. How has Odysseus changed by the end?
4. How have Penelope and Telemachus changed?
5. What kind of peace is restored at the end?

**Conversation Starters:**

- Why is recognition important in a homecoming story?
- Does Odysseus return as the same man who left Troy?

## Character Study Questions

### Odysseus

1. What are Odysseus's greatest strengths?
2. What are his greatest weaknesses?
3. When is he most admirable?
4. When is he most foolish?
5. Does he become wiser during the journey?

### Penelope

1. How does Penelope show intelligence?
2. How does she show loyalty?
3. Why should she not be seen as merely waiting passively?
4. What makes her a match for Odysseus?

### Telemachus

1. What does Telemachus lack at the beginning?
2. Who helps him grow?
3. What does he learn about courage?
4. How does he prove himself?

### The Gods

1. Which gods help Odysseus?
2. Which gods oppose him?
3. Do the gods act fairly?
4. How do divine actions and human choices work together?

## Vocabulary to Know

| Word | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Epic** | A long heroic story, often involving gods, monsters, and great journeys |
| **Hero** | A person admired for courage, noble qualities, or great deeds |
| **Cunning** | Skill in achieving goals through cleverness or strategy |
| **Hospitality** | Generous treatment of guests or strangers |
| **Fate** | A power or plan that seems to determine what will happen |
| **Immortal** | Unable to die |
| **Mortal** | Human; able to die |
| **Disguise** | A changed appearance meant to hide identity |
| **Temptation** | Something attractive that may lead a person away from what is right |
| **Vengeance** | Punishment or revenge for a wrong |
| **Restoration** | The act of bringing order back |
| **Loyalty** | Faithfulness to a person, duty, or promise |

## Symbols and Motifs

- **The Sea**: danger, wandering, uncertainty, and testing.
- **The Bow**: rightful identity, strength, kingship, and marriage.
- **The Bed**: Penelope and Odysseus's private bond and true home.
- **Disguises**: hidden identity and the testing of character.
- **Food and Feasting**: hospitality when used rightly; greed and disorder when abused.
- **Names**: identity, fame, pride, and recognition.

## Big Discussion Questions

Use these for Socratic discussion, family conversation, or class debate.

1. Is Odysseus a good man, a great hero, both, or neither?
2. Does Odysseus suffer mostly because of bad luck, angry gods, his men's failures, or his own mistakes?
3. Which is more important in the story: courage or cleverness?
4. What does the story teach about self-control?
5. Why is home worth so much to Odysseus?
6. Is Penelope as heroic as Odysseus?
7. What does Telemachus learn about becoming a man?
8. Are the gods in the story just?
9. What makes the suitors so wicked?
10. What is the difference between hospitality and indulgence?
11. When does curiosity become dangerous?
12. Does Odysseus deserve his happy ending?

## Short Writing Prompts

Answer in one well-developed paragraph. Use at least one specific example from the story.

1. Explain how Odysseus uses cleverness to survive one dangerous episode.
2. Describe one moment when Odysseus's pride hurts him or his men.
3. Explain why Penelope should be considered heroic.
4. Compare the Lotus-Eaters and the Sirens as two different kinds of temptation.
5. Describe how Telemachus changes from the beginning to the end.
6. Explain what the Cyclops episode teaches about hospitality.
7. Choose one monster or magical figure and explain what human weakness it represents.
8. Explain why Odysseus chooses home over comfort or immortality.

## Longer Essay Prompts

Write a complete essay with an introduction, body paragraphs, and conclusion.

1. **The Hero's Mind:** Odysseus is famous for cleverness. Is intelligence his greatest heroic quality? Use three episodes to support your answer.
2. **The Cost of Pride:** How does pride create danger in *The Odyssey*? Discuss Odysseus, the suitors, or another character.
3. **The Meaning of Home:** What does home mean in the story? Explain how Ithaca, Penelope, Telemachus, and kingship all shape Odysseus's desire to return.
4. **Women of the Odyssey:** Compare Penelope, Circe, Calypso, and Athena. How does each woman test, help, delay, or guide Odysseus?
5. **Leadership Under Trial:** Is Odysseus a good leader? Consider both his successes and his failures.

## Memory Work and Quotable Ideas

Students do not need to memorize exact lines from Homer, but they should remember these central ideas:

- A hero may be tested more by temptation than by battle.
- Cleverness without humility can become dangerous.
- Home is not merely a place; it is a web of duties, loves, and memories.
- Hospitality reveals civilization; cruelty to guests reveals savagery.
- A long journey can teach a person who he really is.

## Final Review Checklist

Before finishing the unit, students should be able to:

- Identify Odysseus, Penelope, Telemachus, Athena, Poseidon, Circe, Calypso, Polyphemus, and the suitors.
- Retell the major episodes of Odysseus's journey in order.
- Explain why the Cyclops episode is important.
- Describe at least three temptations Odysseus or his men face.
- Explain how Penelope shows wisdom and loyalty.
- Discuss whether Odysseus is a hero despite his flaws.
- Define epic, hospitality, fate, immortal, mortal, cunning, and restoration.
- Write a paragraph using evidence from the story.
