# Gravity Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Gravity is the force of attraction between objects that have mass.

2. Earth has a much stronger gravitational pull because it has far more mass than a pencil, book, or other everyday object.

3. Near Earth, gravity pulls objects toward Earth's center. From our point of view on the surface, that direction feels like down.

4. Mass is the amount of matter in an object. Weight is the measure of how strongly gravity pulls on that mass.

5. A person would weigh less on the Moon because the Moon has less mass than Earth, so its gravitational pull is weaker. The person's mass would stay the same.

6. For a falling object to accelerate means that it speeds up as it falls. Near Earth's surface, gravity makes objects accelerate downward if air resistance is not too great.

7. A feather falls more slowly than a stone on Earth because air resistance slows the feather much more. The feather has a large surface area compared with its weight.

8. A hammer and a feather dropped on the Moon would hit the ground at the same time because the Moon has almost no atmosphere, so there is almost no air resistance to slow the feather.

9. The Moon is moving forward through space, while Earth's gravity pulls it inward. These two motions combine to keep the Moon traveling in an orbit around Earth.

10. Isaac Newton realized that the same force that pulls objects, such as apples, toward Earth also helps keep the Moon in orbit. He understood that gravity acts throughout the universe.

11. The two main factors are the masses of the objects and the distance between them. More mass makes gravity stronger, while greater distance makes gravity weaker.

12. Answers may vary. Good examples include keeping our feet on the ground, causing rain to fall, making rivers flow downhill, giving objects weight, shaping the path of a thrown ball, and helping planets stay in orbit.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Drop Test: Air Resistance in Action**  
   Have students drop a flat sheet of paper and a crumpled sheet of paper from the same height. Ask them to explain why the crumpled paper lands first even though both pieces have the same mass.

2. **Hammer and Feather Demonstration With Substitutes**  
   Use a small toy or eraser as the "hammer" and a feather or lightweight paper shape as the "feather." Drop them together, then discuss how the result would change on the Moon.

3. **Ball Toss Orbit Model**  
   Have students gently toss a ball forward and observe its curved path. Explain that the ball moves forward while gravity pulls it downward, then connect this idea to how an orbit is also a curved path caused by forward motion and gravity.

4. **Weight on Other Worlds Calculation**  
   Give students simple multipliers for the Moon and Mars, then let them calculate what a 90-pound student or backpack would weigh there. Use the activity to reinforce the difference between mass and weight.

5. **Gravity Scavenger Hunt**  
   Ask students to list or sketch examples of gravity at work around the classroom, schoolyard, or home. Examples might include books resting on desks, water flowing downhill, balls falling after being thrown, or bags hanging from hooks.
