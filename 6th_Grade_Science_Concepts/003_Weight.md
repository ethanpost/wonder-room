# Weight

Suppose you step onto a bathroom scale in the morning. The number appears, and you may say, "That is how much I weigh." Now imagine taking the same scale to the Moon. You would still be you. Your bones, muscles, brain, and school shoes would contain the same amount of matter. Yet the scale would show a much smaller number.

How can that be? The answer is that weight is not simply "how much stuff" is in an object. Weight is the measure of how strongly gravity pulls on that object. To understand weight, we must understand the connection between matter, gravity, force, and measurement.

## What Is Weight?

Weight is the force of gravity pulling on an object. When Earth pulls downward on your body, that pull is your weight. When Earth pulls downward on a book, a baseball, or a sack of flour, that pull is the object's weight.

Because weight is a force, scientists usually measure it in newtons. In everyday life in the United States, people often use pounds to describe weight. A bathroom scale might say that a student weighs 90 pounds. In science class, it is useful to remember that this number describes a gravitational pull, not the amount of matter in the student.

Weight always depends on two things:

1. The mass of the object.
2. The strength of gravity where the object is located.

If either one changes, the weight changes.

## Mass and Weight Are Not the Same

Mass is the amount of matter in an object. It tells how much "stuff" the object contains. A bowling ball has more mass than a tennis ball. A full backpack has more mass than an empty backpack.

Weight is different. Weight tells how strongly gravity pulls on that mass. On Earth, a larger mass usually has a larger weight because Earth pulls on more matter. That is why a full backpack feels heavier than an empty one.

But mass can stay the same while weight changes. If you could travel to the Moon, your mass would not disappear. You would not become thinner, smaller, or less real. The Moon simply has weaker gravity than Earth, so it would pull on you with less force. Your weight on the Moon would be about one-sixth of your weight on Earth.

If you weighed 90 pounds on Earth, you would weigh only about 15 pounds on the Moon. Your mass would be unchanged, but your weight would be much less.

## Why Earth Gives Us Weight

Earth is enormous. Because it has so much mass, it has strong gravity near its surface. This gravity pulls objects toward Earth's center. Your weight is the result of Earth pulling you downward.

You feel this pull all day. It is why your feet press against the floor, why your chair supports you, and why your muscles must work when you climb stairs. If Earth did not pull on you, you would not press down on the ground. You would float away unless another force held you in place.

The ground also pushes upward on you. When you stand still, Earth's gravity pulls you down, and the floor pushes up with an equal force. That is why you do not fall through the floor. A scale measures how hard you press down on it, which tells us how strongly gravity is pulling on you.

## Weight as a Force

A force is a push or a pull. Since weight is gravity's pull on an object, weight is a force.

In science, force is measured in newtons, named after Sir Isaac Newton. On Earth, an object with a mass of 1 kilogram weighs about 9.8 newtons. You do not need to memorize complicated formulas yet, but you should know the basic idea:

Weight equals mass multiplied by the strength of gravity.

In symbols, older students often write:

**weight = mass x gravitational field strength**

Near Earth's surface, gravitational field strength is about 9.8 newtons per kilogram. This means Earth pulls on each kilogram of mass with a force of about 9.8 newtons.

## Why Heavy Things Feel Heavy

When you lift a heavy suitcase, you are not lifting "heaviness" itself. You are using your muscles to pull the suitcase upward against gravity's downward pull. The more mass the suitcase has, the more strongly Earth pulls on it, and the more force your muscles must provide.

This is why a backpack becomes harder to lift when you add books. The books add mass. Earth pulls on the added mass. The backpack's weight increases.

It is also why lifting the same object can feel different in different places. On the Moon, the suitcase would have the same mass, but it would weigh less. You could lift it more easily. However, if you tried to push it sideways or stop it after it was moving, it would still resist changes in motion because its mass had not changed.

This point is important: lower weight does not mean lower mass. An astronaut on the Moon can jump higher because the Moon pulls downward less strongly, but the astronaut still has the same body and the same amount of matter.

## Weightlessness

You may have seen pictures of astronauts floating inside a spacecraft. People often say they are "weightless." That word can be a little misleading.

Astronauts in orbit are still being pulled by Earth's gravity. In fact, Earth's gravity at the height of many spacecraft is only somewhat weaker than it is on the ground. The astronauts float because they and their spacecraft are falling around Earth together.

Imagine an elevator cable suddenly broke. During the fall, a person inside would feel weightless for a short time because the person, the elevator, and the scale inside it would all be falling together. The scale would not have a firm surface pushing up against the person, so it would not register the usual weight.

Orbit is similar, except the spacecraft is moving forward so fast that as it falls toward Earth, Earth's surface curves away beneath it. The astronauts are not beyond gravity. They are in continuous free fall.

## Apparent Weight

Your apparent weight is how heavy you feel or how much a scale says you weigh at a particular moment. It can change even when your true weight, the pull of gravity on you, has not changed much.

Think about riding in an elevator. When the elevator begins moving upward, you may feel a little heavier. The floor has to push upward harder to speed you upward, so a scale would briefly show a larger number. When the elevator begins moving downward, you may feel a little lighter because the floor pushes upward less strongly.

Roller coasters use this same idea. At the bottom of a dip, you may feel pressed into your seat. Near the top of a hill, you may feel light for a moment. Gravity is still pulling on you, but your apparent weight changes because your motion and the forces from the seat are changing.

## Measuring Weight

A scale measures weight by measuring force. A spring scale stretches when an object pulls down on it. The greater the weight, the farther the spring stretches. Many classroom spring scales are marked in newtons.

A bathroom scale works differently inside, but the idea is similar. It measures the force with which your body presses down. Since you are standing on Earth, that force is closely related to Earth's gravitational pull on you.

A balance, however, is usually used to compare mass. If two objects balance each other, they have equal mass. A balance would still compare masses correctly on the Moon because both sides would be pulled by the Moon's gravity in the same way. A spring scale, though, would show a smaller weight on the Moon.

## Weight on Other Worlds

Different planets and moons have different gravitational pulls. The strength of gravity depends largely on a body's mass and size. A very massive planet can pull strongly. A smaller moon usually pulls more weakly.

Here are approximate examples:

| Place | Compared with Earth Weight |
| --- | --- |
| Moon | about 1/6 as much |
| Mars | about 2/5 as much |
| Earth | normal Earth weight |
| Jupiter | more than 2 times as much |

If a student weighed 90 pounds on Earth, he would weigh about 15 pounds on the Moon, about 36 pounds on Mars, and more than 180 pounds on Jupiter. The student would not gain or lose mass by traveling. Only the gravitational pull would change.

This is why scientists and engineers must think carefully about weight when planning space missions. A machine that is easy to lift on the Moon may still have a great deal of mass. It may be hard to push, stop, or steer even though it weighs less there.

## Weight in Everyday Life

Weight matters in many ordinary situations. Builders must know how much weight a bridge, floor, or roof can support. Pilots and engineers must know the weight of an airplane before takeoff. Doctors may track a person's body weight as one clue about health. Athletes think about weight when they train, jump, climb, or throw.

Weight also helps explain why objects press on surfaces. A stack of books presses down on a desk because Earth pulls on the books. A person standing in snow may sink because his weight is concentrated on the area of his boots. Snowshoes help because they spread the same weight over a larger area, reducing the pressure on the snow.

Even play depends on weight. A kite must be light enough for the wind to lift. A sled must be heavy enough to stay steady but not so heavy that it is difficult to pull. A soccer ball's weight affects how it moves when kicked.

## Why Weight Matters

Weight is one of the first scientific ideas we meet in daily life, even before we know its name. Every time you lift a box, stand on a scale, jump from a step, pack a suitcase, or watch an astronaut float, weight is part of the story.

The key lesson is simple but powerful: weight is not the same as mass. Mass is the amount of matter in an object. Weight is the force of gravity pulling on that matter. Since gravity can be stronger or weaker in different places, weight can change even when mass stays the same.

Once you understand weight, you can better understand falling objects, planets, rockets, sports, buildings, machines, and your own body. Weight is gravity made personal: it is the universe pulling on you.
