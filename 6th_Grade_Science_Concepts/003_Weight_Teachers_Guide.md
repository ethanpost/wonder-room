# Weight Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Weight is the force of gravity pulling on an object.

2. Weight is considered a force because it is a pull: gravity pulls an object toward Earth or another body.

3. Mass is the amount of matter in an object. Weight is the measure of how strongly gravity pulls on that matter.

4. An object's weight depends on its mass and the strength of gravity where the object is located.

5. A person would weigh less on the Moon because the Moon has weaker gravity than Earth. The person's mass would stay the same.

6. A student who weighs 90 pounds on Earth would weigh about 15 pounds on the Moon, because Moon weight is about one-sixth of Earth weight.

7. A full backpack weighs more because the added books or supplies increase its mass. Earth pulls on that added mass, increasing the backpack's weight.

8. A scale measures force. In ordinary use on Earth, it measures how strongly a person or object presses down because of gravity.

9. Astronauts in orbit seem weightless because they and their spacecraft are falling around Earth together. Gravity is still acting on them, but they do not feel the usual support force from a floor or scale.

10. Apparent weight is how heavy a person feels, or what a scale reads, at a particular moment. It can change when motion changes even if the person's mass stays the same.

11. When an elevator begins moving upward, the floor pushes harder on the person, so the person feels heavier. When it begins moving downward, the floor pushes less, so the person feels lighter.

12. A balance compares mass by comparing two objects against each other. On the Moon, both sides are pulled by the Moon's gravity in the same way, so equal masses would still balance.

13. Engineers must think about weight because structures and machines must support, lift, move, or withstand forces caused by gravity. Bridges, airplanes, and spacecraft can fail if their weight and loads are not understood.

14. Answers may vary. Good examples include standing on a scale, lifting a backpack, a bridge supporting cars, an airplane preparing for takeoff, snowshoes spreading a person's weight, a kite needing to be light enough to fly, or a ball's weight affecting how it moves.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Mass vs. Weight Sorting Discussion**  
   Give students examples such as a backpack, astronaut, bowling ball, Moon rock, and bathroom scale. Ask them to identify whether each example mainly relates to mass, weight, or both, then explain their reasoning.

2. **Spring Scale Measurements**  
   Use a classroom spring scale to measure several objects in newtons. Have students compare the readings and connect larger readings with stronger gravitational pull on greater mass.

3. **Earth and Moon Weight Calculations**  
   Provide simple Earth weights, such as 60, 90, and 120 pounds. Have students divide by 6 to estimate Moon weight, then discuss why mass has not changed.

4. **Backpack Weight Investigation**  
   Weigh or compare an empty backpack and the same backpack with books added. Ask students to explain why the weight changes using the words mass, gravity, and force.

5. **Elevator or Roller Coaster Thought Experiment**  
   If an elevator is available, discuss how students feel when it starts and stops. If not, use a roller coaster diagram. Connect the sensation of feeling heavier or lighter to apparent weight.

6. **Balance vs. Scale Demonstration**  
   Compare a balance with a spring scale if both are available. Explain that the spring scale measures weight, while the balance compares mass. Ask students to predict what would happen to each tool on the Moon.

7. **Design for Weight Challenge**  
   Give students index cards, tape, and small weights or coins. Challenge them to build a small paper bridge that supports as much weight as possible, then discuss why engineers must understand loads and support forces.
