# Mass Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Mass is the amount of matter in an object.

2. Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space.

3. Mass is not always the same as size because a large object may contain little matter, while a smaller object may contain matter packed more tightly. For example, a baseball can have more mass than a beach ball.

4. Mass is the amount of matter in an object. Weight is the force of gravity pulling on that object.

5. A person's mass would stay the same on the Moon because the person still contains the same amount of matter. The person's weight would change because the Moon's gravity is weaker than Earth's.

6. Scientists usually measure mass in grams and kilograms.

7. A balance is commonly used to compare the mass of objects.

8. A balance would still compare masses correctly on the Moon because both sides of the balance would be pulled by the Moon's gravity in the same way.

9. Inertia is an object's resistance to changes in motion.

10. An object with more mass has more inertia, so it is harder to start moving, stop, or turn.

11. A full shopping cart has more mass than an empty cart, so it has more inertia. More force is needed to start it moving and to stop it once it is moving.

12. The light cart will accelerate more quickly because it has less mass. With the same force, an object with less mass changes its motion more easily.

13. Mass is connected to gravity because every object with mass attracts other objects with mass. Earth's large mass gives it strong gravity, and an object's mass is the matter that gravity pulls on.

14. Conservation of mass means that, in ordinary physical and chemical changes, mass is not created or destroyed. Matter may change form or be rearranged, but the total mass stays the same if nothing enters or leaves the system.

15. Matter might seem to disappear because it has become a gas, dissolved into a liquid, broken into pieces, or moved somewhere else. Careful measurement can show that the mass is still present.

16. Answers may vary. Good examples include a full backpack being harder to carry, a bowling ball being harder to throw than a tennis ball, a packed suitcase being harder to move, cooks measuring ingredients by mass, engineers designing low-mass bicycles or rockets, and astronauts handling massive equipment in space.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Mass and Size Comparison**  
   Show students objects such as a sponge, rock, beach ball, baseball, empty box, and full box. Ask them to predict which objects have more mass, then discuss why size alone does not determine mass.

2. **Balance Investigation**  
   Use a classroom balance to compare common objects. Have students record which object has greater mass and explain how the balance shows this.

3. **Mass vs. Weight Sort**  
   Give students statements such as "measured in kilograms," "changes on the Moon," "amount of matter," and "force of gravity." Have them sort each statement under mass, weight, or both.

4. **Shopping Cart Inertia Model**  
   Use a small cart or tray, first empty and then loaded with books. Have students gently push and stop it, then connect the difference to mass and inertia.

5. **Conservation of Mass: Paper Tear**  
   Have students measure the mass of a sheet of paper, tear it into pieces, and measure all pieces together. Discuss why the shape changed but the total mass stayed the same.

6. **Melting Ice Mass Check**  
   Place an ice cube in a sealed bag or container and measure its mass before and after melting. Students should observe that the mass remains nearly the same if no water escapes.

7. **Space Equipment Scenario**  
   Ask students to imagine moving a large tool chest in a spacecraft. Have them explain why it may feel weightless but still be hard to stop once moving.

8. **Recipe by Mass**  
   Compare measuring a dry ingredient by volume and by mass. Discuss why scientists and careful cooks often prefer mass for accuracy.
