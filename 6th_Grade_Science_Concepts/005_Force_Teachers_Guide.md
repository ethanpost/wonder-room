# Force Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. A force is a push or a pull acting on an object.

2. A force can change an object's motion or change its shape. For example, it can start, stop, speed up, slow down, turn, stretch, squeeze, or bend an object.

3. A contact force is a force that acts when objects touch each other.

4. Answers may vary. Good examples include a foot kicking a ball, a hand pulling a drawer, friction between shoes and the floor, air resistance pushing on a falling leaf, or a bat hitting a baseball.

5. A non-contact force is a force that can act even when objects are not touching.

6. Answers may vary. Good examples include gravity, magnetism, and electric force.

7. Scientists measure force in newtons.

8. A spring scale can be used in class to measure force.

9. Force has size and direction because a force can be stronger or weaker and can act in a particular direction, such as upward, downward, forward, backward, or sideways.

10. Balanced forces are forces that are equal in size and opposite in direction, so they cancel each other.

11. When forces are balanced, an object's motion does not change. A still object remains still, and a moving object continues moving at a steady speed in a straight line.

12. Unbalanced forces are forces that do not cancel each other. They cause an object's motion to change.

13. In science, acceleration means a change in velocity. An object accelerates when it speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.

14. Force, mass, and acceleration are connected because more force causes more acceleration, while more mass makes acceleration harder. The relationship is often written as force = mass x acceleration.

15. Weight is considered a force because it is the pull of gravity on an object.

16. Friction can be helpful because it allows shoes to grip the ground, tires to grip roads, brakes to slow vehicles, and hands to hold objects.

17. Friction can be troublesome because it slows motion, creates heat, wears down parts, and makes machines less efficient.

18. Answers may vary. Good examples include stretching a rubber band, squeezing clay, bending a paper clip, compressing a spring, twisting a towel, or denting a can.

19. Newton's third law says that forces come in pairs. When one object pushes or pulls on another, the second object pushes or pulls back on the first.

20. Answers may vary. Good examples include walking, kicking a ball, opening a door, riding a bicycle, braking a car, a bridge supporting traffic, wind pushing on a sail, muscles moving bones, or gravity pulling objects downward.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Push and Pull Hunt**  
   Have students list or sketch examples of pushes and pulls around the classroom. Discuss which forces change motion and which forces are balanced.

2. **Spring Scale Force Lab**  
   Use spring scales to pull different objects across a desk or floor. Students record force readings in newtons and compare how surface texture or object mass changes the reading.

3. **Balanced Tug-of-War Model**  
   Use a rope or string with students pulling gently from opposite sides. Show balanced forces when the rope stays still and unbalanced forces when one side pulls harder.

4. **Force Arrow Diagrams**  
   Give students simple situations, such as a book on a table, a falling ball, or a bicycle slowing down. Have them draw arrows showing the direction and relative size of the forces.

5. **Friction Surface Test**  
   Slide the same object across surfaces such as paper, cloth, carpet, and smooth plastic. Students compare which surfaces create more friction and explain the evidence.

6. **Shape Change Station**  
   Provide safe materials such as rubber bands, clay, sponges, springs, and paper clips. Students observe how pushing, pulling, twisting, stretching, and squeezing can change shape.

7. **Balloon Static Force Demonstration**  
   Rub a balloon on hair or cloth, then hold it near small paper bits. Use the attraction to introduce electric force as a non-contact force.

8. **Newton's Third Law Jump Discussion**  
   Have students perform a small standing jump or push gently against a wall. Discuss how the ground or wall pushes back, connecting the activity to action-reaction force pairs.
