# Speed Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Speed tells how fast an object moves, or how much distance it travels in a certain amount of time.

2. To calculate speed, you must know distance and time.

3. The basic formula is speed = distance / time.

4. Speed units include both distance and time because speed compares how far something travels with how long it takes.

5. Answers may vary. Good examples include meters per second, kilometers per hour, miles per hour, and centimeters per second.

6. The speed is 4 meters per second because 20 meters divided by 5 seconds equals 4 meters per second.

7. Constant speed means an object covers equal distances in equal amounts of time.

8. Average speed is the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken.

9. Average speed may not tell the exact speed at every moment because an object may speed up, slow down, stop, or change speed during the trip.

10. Instantaneous speed is the speed of an object at one particular moment.

11. A car's speedometer shows instantaneous speed.

12. Speed tells how fast an object moves. Velocity tells how fast an object moves in a particular direction.

13. An object can have the same speed but changing velocity if its direction changes. For example, a car moving around a circular track at a steady speed has changing velocity.

14. Speed tells how fast something moves. Acceleration tells how velocity changes, including speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction.

15. Forces can change speed by making an object speed up or slow down. A push may increase speed, while friction or braking may decrease speed.

16. A falling ball usually speeds up near Earth because gravity pulls it downward and changes its motion.

17. Air resistance pushes against a falling object and can slow it down or limit its speed. A parachute increases air resistance and helps a person fall more slowly.

18. High speed can make transportation more dangerous because fast vehicles need more distance to stop, air resistance becomes stronger, and collisions involve larger changes in motion and greater forces.

19. A steeper line on a distance-time graph usually means greater speed because more distance is covered in the same amount of time.

20. Answers may vary. Good examples include driving safely, riding a bicycle, running in sports, catching a ball, wind speed during storms, rivers flowing, airplanes flying, or timing a walk to school.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Toy Car Speed Trial**  
   Mark a measured track on the floor or table. Students time a toy car over the distance and calculate speed using speed = distance / time.

2. **Walking Speed Lab**  
   Measure a hallway or outdoor path. Students walk it at normal pace and fast pace, then compare their speeds in meters per second.

3. **Average vs. Instantaneous Discussion**  
   Present a sample car trip with stops, slow traffic, and highway driving. Have students explain why the trip's average speed differs from the speedometer reading at a moment.

4. **Speed Unit Match**  
   Give students cards with units such as meters per second, miles per hour, and kilometers per hour. Students match each unit to situations where it is commonly used.

5. **Distance-Time Graph Sketches**  
   Provide short motion stories, such as "stopped, then walked steadily, then ran." Students sketch distance-time graphs and explain what the slope shows.

6. **Speed-Time Graph Sort**  
   Show simple speed-time graphs with rising, falling, and flat lines. Students identify speeding up, slowing down, and constant speed.

7. **Air Resistance Drop Test**  
   Drop a flat sheet of paper and a crumpled sheet from the same height. Discuss how air resistance affects speed during falling.

8. **Transportation Safety Scenario**  
   Give students examples of vehicles traveling at different speeds. Ask them to explain why stopping distance and safety equipment become more important at higher speeds.
