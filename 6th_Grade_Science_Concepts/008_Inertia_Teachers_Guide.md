# Inertia Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Inertia is an object's resistance to changes in motion.

2. Inertia is not a force. It is a property of matter that makes objects resist changes in motion.

3. Newton's first law says that an object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion in a straight line at a constant speed, unless acted on by an unbalanced force.

4. Newton's first law is sometimes called the law of inertia because it describes the tendency of objects to keep doing what they are already doing.

5. An object with more mass has more inertia. More mass means greater resistance to changes in motion.

6. A loaded wagon has more mass than an empty wagon, so it has more inertia and requires more force to start moving.

7. It means a resting object will remain still unless an unbalanced force acts on it.

8. It means a moving object will continue moving in a straight line at a steady speed unless an unbalanced force acts on it.

9. Moving objects usually slow down on Earth because friction and air resistance act against their motion.

10. Seat belts protect passengers by applying a force that slows their bodies down with the car during a sudden stop.

11. Loose objects in a car can be dangerous because they tend to keep moving forward during a sudden stop and may strike passengers.

12. A heavier vehicle has more mass and more inertia, so it requires more force or more distance to slow down and stop.

13. Inertia tends to keep a rolling ball moving, while friction acts against the ball's motion and slows it down.

14. An object moving in a circle needs a force to keep turning because inertia tends to make the object continue in a straight line.

15. When a ball on a string is released, it flies off in a straight-line path from the point of release because the string no longer pulls it toward the center.

16. Inertia still matters in space because objects still have mass, even when they seem weightless. Massive objects are still difficult to start or stop.

17. Lower weight on the Moon does not mean lower inertia because inertia depends on mass, and mass stays the same when an object goes to the Moon.

18. Answers may vary. Good examples include a runner starting a race, a football player changing direction, a baseball being caught, a tennis racket following through, or a soccer ball continuing to roll until friction slows it.

19. Answers may vary. Good examples include thinking a force is needed to keep an object moving, thinking inertia depends on weight instead of mass, or thinking inertia is a hidden push or a kind of energy.

20. Answers may vary. Good examples include a passenger lurching forward when a car stops, a backpack sliding on a bus seat, a rolling ball slowing because of friction, a heavy cart being hard to stop, dishes tending to stay still during a tablecloth pull, or a bicycle continuing forward after pedaling stops.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Coin and Card Demonstration**  
   Place an index card over a cup with a coin on top. Flick the card sideways so the coin drops into the cup. Discuss how the coin's inertia kept it nearly at rest.

2. **Empty vs. Loaded Cart**  
   Push an empty cart or tray, then add books and push it again. Have students compare how much force is needed to start and stop each version.

3. **Seat Belt Scenario Discussion**  
   Use a toy car and small figure or block to model a sudden stop. Discuss why the passenger keeps moving forward and how a seat belt changes the motion safely.

4. **Rolling Surface Test**  
   Roll the same ball across carpet, tile, paper, and cloth. Students observe how friction competes with inertia and changes how far the ball travels.

5. **String and Ball Direction Model**  
   Safely whirl a soft object on a string or demonstrate with a diagram. Ask students to predict the path if the string is released, then connect the straight-line path to inertia.

6. **Inertia in Sports Analysis**  
   Show or describe sports examples such as catching a baseball, stopping after a sprint, or changing direction in soccer. Students identify where inertia appears and what forces change motion.

7. **Moon Inertia Thought Experiment**  
   Ask students whether a heavy equipment box would be easy or hard to stop on the Moon or in a spacecraft. Use the discussion to separate weight from mass and inertia.

8. **Common Misconception Sort**  
   Give students statements about inertia, such as "force is always needed to keep motion going" or "inertia depends on mass." Have them sort each as true or false and explain corrections.
