# Momentum Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Momentum is the quantity of motion an object has.

2. Momentum depends on mass and velocity.

3. The basic formula is momentum = mass x velocity.

4. A parked car has no momentum because it is not moving. Its velocity is zero, so its momentum is zero.

5. Inertia is an object's resistance to changes in motion and depends on mass. Momentum depends on both mass and velocity, so an object must be moving to have momentum.

6. If two objects move with the same velocity, the object with more mass has more momentum.

7. If two objects have the same mass, the object with greater velocity has more momentum.

8. Direction matters because momentum depends on velocity, and velocity includes direction. Momentum acts in the direction the object is moving.

9. A force must act on an object for its momentum to change.

10. Moving the glove backward increases the time over which the ball stops. The same change in momentum happens over a longer time, so the force on the hand is smaller.

11. A collision happens when objects hit each other.

12. During a collision, each object pushes on the other with an equal and opposite force. This follows Newton's third law.

13. Conservation of momentum means that, in a closed system, the total momentum stays the same unless an outside force acts on it.

14. No. Conservation of momentum means the total momentum of the system stays the same. Individual objects may gain, lose, or exchange momentum.

15. Recoil is the backward motion that happens when an object or person pushes or throws something forward and gains opposite momentum.

16. Rockets move by pushing hot gases backward at high speed. The gases carry momentum backward, and the rocket gains momentum forward.

17. A faster car has more momentum because momentum depends on velocity. Increasing velocity increases momentum.

18. Seat belts, airbags, helmets, and padding help manage momentum by increasing the time or distance over which a person slows down or by spreading forces over safer parts of the body.

19. Answers may vary. Good examples include a football player running downfield, a bat changing a baseball's momentum, a soccer ball kicked toward a goal, a hockey puck sliding on ice, or a player bending knees when landing.

20. Answers may vary. Good examples include a loaded shopping cart being hard to stop, a swinging backpack bumping someone, a sled sliding downhill, rushing river water pushing strongly, wind pushing signs or trees, or a moving car needing distance to stop.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Mass and Speed Momentum Comparison**  
   Roll a light ball and a heavier ball at similar speeds into a soft barrier. Have students compare which is harder to stop and connect the result to mass and momentum.

2. **Fast vs. Slow Same Object Trial**  
   Roll the same ball slowly and then faster into stacked cups or blocks. Students observe how changing speed changes the effect of the collision.

3. **Cart Collision Demonstration**  
   Use toy carts or low-friction carts to show one moving cart colliding with another. Students describe how momentum transfers between carts.

4. **Catching With Give**  
   Toss a soft ball to students and have them catch it once with still hands and once by moving hands backward. Discuss how increasing stopping time reduces force.

5. **Recoil on Wheels Model**  
   Have a student sit on a rolling chair or use a toy cart and gently throw a soft ball forward. Observe backward motion and connect it to recoil and conservation of momentum.

6. **Rocket Balloon Activity**  
   Inflate a balloon and release it, or run it along a string. Discuss how air moving backward gives the balloon forward momentum.

7. **Momentum Safety Design Challenge**  
   Give students materials such as paper, cotton balls, tape, and cups to design padding for a small falling object. Connect successful designs to increasing stopping time and reducing force.

8. **Everyday Momentum Sort**  
   Provide scenarios such as a parked truck, a rolling cart, a fast tennis ball, and a slow ship. Students rank or sort them by likely momentum and justify their choices using mass and velocity.
