# Friction Teacher's Guide

## Answer Key

1. Friction is the force that resists motion, or the tendency of motion, between surfaces that are touching.

2. Friction is called a contact force because the surfaces must touch for friction to act.

3. Friction usually acts opposite the direction of motion or opposite the direction an object is trying to move.

4. Surfaces that look smooth still have friction because, under a microscope, they have tiny bumps, ridges, and valleys that catch on one another.

5. The amount of friction can be affected by how rough or smooth the surfaces are and how hard the surfaces are pressed together.

6. Static friction is friction between surfaces that are not sliding past each other. It can prevent motion from starting.

7. Static friction helps you walk because your foot pushes backward on the ground and friction from the ground pushes your foot forward instead of letting it slip.

8. Sliding friction is friction that acts when two surfaces slide past each other.

9. It can be harder to start a heavy box moving because static friction must first be overcome. Once the box is sliding, sliding friction is often less than the maximum static friction.

10. Rolling friction is friction that acts when an object rolls over a surface.

11. Wheels are useful because rolling friction is usually much smaller than sliding friction, so objects are easier to move.

12. Fluid friction is resistance that occurs when an object moves through a fluid, such as a liquid or gas.

13. Answers may vary. Good examples include air resistance on a cyclist, air resistance on a falling leaf, water resistance on a swimmer, or water resistance on a boat.

14. Answers may vary. Good examples include walking, writing with a pencil, holding objects, tires gripping roads, brakes stopping vehicles, screws holding materials, and shoes gripping stairs.

15. Answers may vary. Good examples include slowing motion, wasting energy as heat, wearing down machine parts, wearing down shoes or tires, causing squeaks, and making engines or other machines less efficient.

16. Lubricants reduce friction by placing a slippery layer between surfaces so they do not scrape directly against each other.

17. People may want to increase friction for better grip, safety, and control, such as with shoe tread, tire tread, sand on ice, sports cleats, or climbing chalk.

18. Friction can produce heat by changing motion energy into thermal energy as surfaces rub or as an object moves through a fluid.

19. Moving objects on Earth usually slow down because friction and air resistance act as unbalanced forces opposing their motion.

20. Answers may vary. Good examples include tires gripping roads, brakes using friction to stop wheels, icy roads reducing tire friction, oil reducing friction in engines, bearings reducing friction in wheels, and streamlined vehicles reducing air resistance.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Surface Friction Test**  
   Slide the same object across surfaces such as tile, carpet, sandpaper, and wax paper. Students compare how far it travels and identify which surfaces create more friction.

2. **Static vs. Sliding Friction Pull**  
   Use a spring scale to pull a block or small box. Students observe that the force needed to start motion can be greater than the force needed to keep it moving.

3. **Rolling vs. Sliding Challenge**  
   Move a heavy book or box by sliding it, then by placing it on pencils or small rollers. Discuss how rolling friction differs from sliding friction.

4. **Lubrication Demonstration**  
   Compare sliding two dry surfaces with sliding surfaces separated by a small amount of safe lubricant, wax paper, or plastic wrap. Discuss how lubricants reduce direct contact.

5. **Shoe Tread Investigation**  
   Examine different shoe soles or tire images. Students predict which designs would grip best on dry ground, wet ground, or snow, then explain how tread increases friction.

6. **Air Resistance Drop Test**  
   Drop a flat sheet of paper, a crumpled sheet, and a coffee filter if available. Use the results to discuss fluid friction from air.

7. **Friction Heat Activity**  
   Have students rub their hands together slowly and then quickly. Discuss how friction changes motion energy into heat.

8. **Transportation Safety Scenario**  
   Present driving situations such as dry pavement, rain, snow, and ice. Students explain how friction affects starting, turning, stopping distance, and safety.
