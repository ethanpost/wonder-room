# Work

Imagine that your coach asks you to push a heavy tackling sled across the grass. You lean in, your shoes dig into the ground, and the sled grinds forward. That certainly feels like work.

Now imagine pushing just as hard against a brick wall. Your face turns red, your arms ache, and your friends may admire your determination. But the wall does not move.

In everyday language, both actions may feel like work. In science, only one of them counts as work.

Scientific work happens when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force.

That definition may sound fussy at first, but it is extremely useful. It lets scientists measure how force, motion, and energy are connected.

## Everyday Work and Scientific Work

In ordinary speech, work can mean chores, homework, a job, practice, effort, or anything tiring. Carrying groceries, holding a heavy suitcase, or studying Latin verbs may all be called work.

Science uses the word more carefully.

In physics, work is done on an object when two things happen:

1. A force acts on the object.
2. The object moves in the direction of that force, or at least partly in that direction.

If either part is missing, no scientific work is done on that object.

This is why pushing a sled across grass is work, but pushing a wall that does not move is not work. In both cases you apply a force. Only the sled moves.

## Force Is Needed

Work begins with force.

A force is a push or a pull. You use force when you lift a backpack, kick a ball, pull open a door, draw back a bowstring, or push a lawn mower. Gravity also exerts a force. So does friction. So does a stretched rubber band.

But force alone is not enough for work. If you hold a heavy book above your head without moving it, your muscles may get tired, but no work is being done on the book in the scientific sense. The book is not moving through a distance.

Your muscles are using energy inside your body to keep contracting, so the activity is still tiring. But physics is asking a specific question: did a force move the object through a distance?

For the book, the answer is no.

## Distance Is Needed

Work also requires distance.

If you lift a backpack from the floor to a desk, you do work on the backpack. You apply an upward force, and the backpack moves upward. If you lift it higher, you do more work because you move it through a greater distance.

If you push a toy car across a table, you do work on the car. The push and the motion are in the same general direction.

If you push a stuck door and it does not budge, no work is done on the door. You used force, but the distance moved was zero.

This can be surprising because the human body can get tired while doing no scientific work on an object. Science is not saying you did nothing. It is saying the object did not move in the required way.

## The Formula for Work

When the force acts in the same direction as the motion, work is calculated like this:

**Work = Force × Distance**

Scientists often write this as:

**W = F × d**

In this formula:

- **W** means work.
- **F** means force.
- **d** means distance.

Force is measured in newtons, which are written as **N**. Distance is measured in meters, which are written as **m**. Work is measured in joules, written as **J**.

One joule is the work done when a force of one newton moves an object one meter in the direction of the force.

So:

**1 joule = 1 newton × 1 meter**

A joule is also called a newton-meter.

## A Simple Work Calculation

Suppose you pull a wagon with a force of 12 newtons for a distance of 3 meters.

Use the formula:

**Work = Force × Distance**

**Work = 12 N × 3 m**

**Work = 36 J**

You did 36 joules of work on the wagon.

The calculation is simple, but the meaning is important. A larger force does more work if the distance is the same. A longer distance does more work if the force is the same.

## No Motion, No Work on the Object

A student may push hard against a wall for ten seconds. His shoes may slide a little, his arms may shake, and he may feel quite heroic. But if the wall does not move, no work is done on the wall.

The force is real. The effort is real. The distance moved by the wall is zero.

Since:

**Work = Force × Distance**

and the distance is zero, the work done on the wall is zero.

The same is true if you hold a heavy backpack still at shoulder height. You are exerting an upward force, but the backpack is not moving upward. No work is being done on the backpack at that moment.

## Direction Matters

Work depends not only on force and distance, but also on direction.

If you lift a suitcase upward, your upward force moves the suitcase upward. You do work on the suitcase.

If you carry that suitcase across a flat floor at the same height, your arms exert an upward force to support it, but the suitcase moves sideways. The upward force and sideways motion are at right angles to each other.

In that case, your upward force does not do work on the suitcase in the scientific sense. Work by a force happens only for motion in the direction of that force, or partly in that direction.

This does not mean carrying the suitcase is easy. Your muscles still use energy to keep the suitcase lifted. But the physics of work on the suitcase is about force acting through distance in the force's direction.

## Work Against Gravity

Gravity pulls objects downward. When you lift an object upward, you do work against gravity.

If you lift a backpack from the floor to a chair, you do work because you apply an upward force and the backpack moves upward. If you climb stairs while wearing the backpack, you do work against gravity because you raise both your body and the backpack to a greater height.

But if you carry the backpack down a level hallway at the same height, you are not raising it. You may become tired, but you are not doing work against gravity on the backpack during that horizontal motion.

The key question is: did the object move upward while an upward force acted on it?

If yes, work was done against gravity.

## Work and Energy

Work is closely connected to energy.

Energy is the ability to cause change or do work. When work is done on an object, energy is often transferred to that object or changed from one form to another.

When you lift a book onto a shelf, you do work against gravity. The book gains gravitational potential energy because it is higher than before. If the book falls, that stored energy can become motion energy.

When you kick a soccer ball, your foot does work on the ball. The ball gains kinetic energy, which is the energy of motion.

When friction slows a sliding box, friction does work too. But it does work in the opposite direction of the motion, changing some of the box's motion energy into heat.

Work is one of the bridges between force and energy.

## Positive and Negative Work

Work can be positive or negative.

Positive work happens when a force helps an object's motion. If you push a cart forward and it moves forward, your push does positive work on the cart. The cart gains energy or keeps moving because of your force.

Negative work happens when a force opposes motion. Friction often does negative work. If a book slides across a desk, friction acts opposite the book's motion and slows it down. The book loses kinetic energy.

Brakes on a bicycle do negative work on the wheels. They use friction to take energy away from the motion and turn some of it into heat.

Positive and negative work are not "good" and "bad." They simply describe whether the force helps or opposes the motion.

## Machines and Work

Simple machines help people do work more conveniently.

A lever, pulley, wheel and axle, inclined plane, wedge, and screw can change the size or direction of a force. A ramp, for example, lets you push a heavy box up to a truck bed using less force than lifting it straight up.

But the ramp does not magically make the work disappear. You use less force, but you push the box over a longer distance. In an ideal machine with no friction, the total work would be the same. In real machines, some extra work is usually needed because friction wastes energy as heat.

Machines are valuable because they can make a hard job manageable. They trade force for distance, distance for force, or change the direction of a force.

## Work and Power

Work and power are related, but they are not the same.

Work tells how much energy is transferred when a force moves an object through a distance.

Power tells how quickly work is done.

Two boys might each carry the same box up the same stairs. If both boxes have the same weight and are lifted the same height, both boys do the same amount of work on the boxes. But if one boy runs up the stairs and the other walks slowly, the runner uses more power because he does the work in less time.

Power is about the rate of doing work.

## Why the Scientific Meaning Matters

The scientific meaning of work may feel strange because it does not always match ordinary language.

Holding a heavy object can feel difficult, but no work is done on the object if it does not move. Carrying a backpack across a level floor can be tiring, but the upward force from your arms does not do work on the backpack if the backpack's height stays the same. Pushing a wall can exhaust you, but no work is done on the wall if it remains still.

This careful definition helps scientists measure energy transfer precisely. It answers a clear question: how much force acted through how much distance in the direction of the force?

Once you understand that question, the idea of work becomes much easier.

## Work in Everyday Life

You do scientific work when you lift a dumbbell, climb a rope, push a swing, pull a wagon, kick a ball, draw a bow, or drag a sled. In each case, a force moves something through a distance.

Other forces do work too. Gravity does work on a falling apple. A stretched rubber band does work on a paper airplane launcher. Friction does work on a sliding hockey puck by slowing it down. The wind can do work on a sailboat by pushing it across the water.

Whenever you see a force causing motion, ask:

- What object is the force acting on?
- Which direction is the force?
- Which direction does the object move?
- How far does it move?

Those questions reveal whether work is being done.

## The Big Idea

Work is not just effort. It is a measurable transfer of energy by force through distance.

The simplest form is:

**Work = Force × Distance**

A force must act. The object must move. The motion must be in the direction of the force, or at least partly in that direction. The unit of work is the joule.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**In science, work is done when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force.**
