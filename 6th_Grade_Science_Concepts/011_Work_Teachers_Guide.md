# Work Teacher's Guide

## Teaching Focus

- Emphasize that scientific work requires both force and motion in the direction of that force.
- Contrast everyday effort with scientific work using examples such as pushing a wall, holding a book still, lifting a backpack, and carrying a backpack across a level floor.
- Connect work to energy transfer, especially gravitational potential energy, kinetic energy, and energy lost to friction.
- Have students practice the formula **Work = Force × Distance** using newtons, meters, and joules.

## Answer Key

1. In everyday language, work often means effort, chores, jobs, or anything tiring. In science, work means using a force to move an object in the direction of that force.

2. Work is done when a force acts on an object and moves it in the direction of the force, or at least partly in that direction.

3. Both are needed because work measures how a force acts through a distance. A force without motion, or motion without a force in the direction of motion, does not do work on the object in the scientific sense.

4. Work = Force × Distance, or W = F × d, when the force is in the direction of the motion.

5. Work is measured in joules (J).

6. W = 12 N × 3 m = 36 J.

7. No work is done on the book because the book does not move. Even though the student applies an upward force, the distance is zero.

8. No work is done on the wall because it does not move. A force alone is not enough for work.

9. The upward force supporting the backpack is perpendicular to the forward motion across a flat floor, and the backpack's height does not change. Work against gravity depends on vertical distance, not horizontal distance.

10. Climbing stairs increases height, so the student does work against gravity on the backpack. Walking across a level hallway at constant height does not raise the backpack, so no work is done against gravity on the backpack during that horizontal motion.

11. Doing work on an object usually transfers energy to it or changes its energy. Work connects force and motion to energy.

12. Positive work happens when the force helps the motion. Negative work happens when the force opposes the motion and tends to reduce the object's energy.

13. Answers may vary. Good examples include lifting a backpack, pushing a swing, kicking a soccer ball, pulling open a door, climbing a rope, or dragging a sled.

14. Answers may vary. Good examples include holding a heavy object still, pushing on an unmoving wall, or carrying a load horizontally at constant height without doing work against gravity on the load.

15. Direction matters because only the part of the motion in the direction of the force counts as work for that force. A force pointing up does not do work on an object that only moves sideways at the same height.

16. A machine can change the size or direction of a force and often lets a person use less force over a greater distance. It does not create energy or avoid the work needed to raise or move a load.

17. Work tells how much energy is transferred when a force acts through a distance. Power tells how quickly that work is done.

18. W = 20 N × 1.5 m = 30 J.

19. Because work equals force times distance, and force is measured in newtons while distance is measured in meters, the unit for work is the newton-meter, which is called a joule.

20. Answers may vary but should state that scientific work means a force moves an object in the direction of the force, is measured in joules, and helps scientists measure energy transfer precisely rather than describe general effort.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Lift vs. Carry Discussion**  
   Have one student lift a backpack onto a chair and another carry the same backpack across the room at constant height. Ask the class which action did work against gravity on the backpack and why carrying across a flat floor is tiring even when no work against gravity is done.

2. **Spring Scale Work Measurement**  
   Use a spring scale to measure the force needed to pull a box across the floor for a measured distance. Multiply force in newtons by distance in meters to calculate work in joules.

3. **"Was Work Done?" Card Sort**  
   Prepare scenario cards such as holding a book still, kicking a ball, pushing a stuck door that does not move, climbing a ladder, and carrying a crate across a level floor. Students sort them into "work done" and "no work done on the object" groups and explain each choice.

4. **Staircase Height Estimate**  
   Measure or estimate the vertical height of a short flight of stairs. Discuss how climbing stairs involves work because body weight and backpack weight are raised through a vertical distance, even though the walking path is slanted.

5. **Joule Calculation Stations**  
   Set up simple problems with known forces and distances, such as lifting labeled weights or pulling carts set distances. Students practice using W = F × d and express answers in joules.

6. **Positive and Negative Work Demo**  
   Push a book across a desk so the class sees forward work from the push, then let friction slow it and discuss friction doing negative work. Connect both ideas to energy gain and energy loss.

7. **Ramp vs. Direct Lift Preview**  
   Compare lifting a weighted box straight up with pushing it up a ramp to the same height. Measure or estimate force and distance for each method and discuss how machines can reduce force but often increase distance.

8. **Everyday Work Journal**  
   Ask students to record three activities at home or in sports where work is done in the scientific sense. Have them identify the object, the force, the direction of motion, and whether energy was transferred.
