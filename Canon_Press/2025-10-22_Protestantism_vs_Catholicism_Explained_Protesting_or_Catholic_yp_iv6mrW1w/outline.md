# Detailed Outline

## Introduction and Series Purpose

### Rory Wilson introduces *Protesting or Catholic*, a six-part series on Protestant–Catholic distinctives.

### This episode is a high-altitude overview of the real differences; not an academic dissertation but a tour for laypeople on both sides.

### Supplemental resources will be linked for deeper study.

### Respect for Catholic friends and thinkers; conservative Catholics and Protestants are allies on many issues.

### The divide is not trivial: differences in justification, the gospel, Mary, veneration of saints, sacraments, papal authority, councils, and scripture.

### Secularism pushes Christians together as co-belligerents, but we cannot assume we are the same deep down.

### Thesis: All followers of Christ should reject several cornerstone teachings of the contemporary Roman church as profound errors.

## What Is Roman Catholicism?

### The Roman Catholic Church teaches that it is the one true church established by Christ, presided over by the bishop of Rome (the Pope).

### The Pope is Jesus Christ's visible representative on earth and wields apostolic authority; this claim is central to Roman identity for the last millennium and a half.

### Papal succession and the keys

- The Pope claims succession from the Apostle Peter (given the keys to bind and loose in Matthew 16).
- Those keys and apostolic authority are believed passed through a 2,000-year chain of popes to the present.

### Church councils (e.g., Nicaea 325) are held under papal approval and are alleged to be guided by the Holy Spirit, teaching infallibly on faith and morals.

### The Pope can also speak infallibly in certain circumstances.

### A good Roman Catholic must submit to scripture and to church teaching accumulated over centuries through councils and papal pronouncements.

### The magisterium (teaching office)

- Councils and papal declarations form the magisterium, which guards the "deposit of faith" and preserves the church from heresy.
- The magisterium is the authoritative interpreter of this deposit.
- The deposit of faith has two equally authoritative parts: scripture and tradition.
- One must accept on faith that the magisterium's interpretations of scripture and tradition are absolutely authoritative.

### Council of Trent: no one ought to dare contradict the magisterium's interpretation of scripture.

### Roman church believes accumulated magisterial teaching is the Spirit-guided outworking of doctrine (John 16:13: "he will guide you into all truth").

### Over time this came to include: confession to a priest, purgatory, justification by faith and works, immaculate conception and assumption of Mary, prayers to and veneration of saints, transubstantiation, and others.

### These teachings were refined over centuries after Christ's ascension and the apostles' death; there has been diversity and outright disagreement.

### Global breadth of the Roman church sustains ongoing diversity: Latin Mass, interpretation of Vatican II, liberation theology, and more.

## What About Protestantism?

### Historical origin: Protestants were originally protesting Catholics; churches began as renewal and reform movements within Western European Latin churches in the early 1500s.

### The Reformation was scripture-based reform of doctrine and practice, shifting away from accumulated tradition.

### Problems and corruptions had festered for years; the famous spark was Martin Luther's 95 Theses at Wittenberg (1517).

- Luther wrote against contemporary teachings and practices, especially indulgences (cash payments for relief from punishment in purgatory).
- Issues broadened into full reform on the basis of scripture alone over against papal declarations and magisterial tradition.

### Nations that broke with Rome: Scotland, England, the Netherlands, Scandinavia, German principalities.

### Two main traditions emerged: Lutheran and Reformed; Anabaptists and others also emerged.

### No centralized church government → many Protestant denominations, each with distinct geographic and historic origins.

### Common bond: repudiation of centralized Roman authority and infallible tradition.

### Denominations are governmental structures only; they do not claim to be the one true church; often good relationships across secondary differences.

### The Five Solas (core beliefs uniting historic Protestants)

- *Sola Scriptura*: Scripture alone is the infallible rule for faith and practice; it is the standard to which all things conform; it transcends pope and magisterium.
- *Solus Christus*: The work of Christ alone is the basis on which sinners are justified before God.
- *Sola Fide*: Justification in Christ is received by faith alone (Galatians 2:16: not justified by works of the law but through faith in Jesus Christ).
- *Sola Gratia*: Salvation from start to finish is by the grace of God alone.
- *Soli Deo Gloria*: All of this is to the glory of God alone.

### Summary: Scripture alone is the infallible rule; we are justified by grace through faith in Christ alone to the glory of God alone.

### For historic Protestantism, everything in church history can be reformed in light of scripture; we should seek to teach and preach Christ more faithfully according to his word.

### Ecumenical councils and great theologians have genuine authority only insofar as they embody and submit to scripture.

### Protestants did not see this as innovation but as *ad fontes*—return to an approach shared with much of the early church; restoration, not creation of something new.

### Contrast: not "new vs. ancient" but "ancient sources vs. ancient sources plus accumulated medieval tradition."

### No one is obligated to believe in the assumption of Mary or her immaculate conception on the Pope's say-so; to require faith in these for salvation is an innovation and corruption of the gospel.

### Protestant diversity (often emphasized by Catholic apologists) can be distinguished as:

- Geographic: theologically aligned churches (e.g., Dutch Reformed, PCA) without a global government → jurisdictional complexity.
- Theological schism: e.g., Lutherans separating over Christ's presence in the Eucharist; often only Lutherans commune at Lutheran churches.
- Friendly institutional distinction: differences (e.g., infant baptism) that don't preclude fellowship and communion across Evangelical, Methodist, Baptist, Presbyterian, Anglican, Pentecostal, etc.

### Many Protestants commune and fellowship across these traditions; common love of scripture as ultimate authority and familial affection remain.

### Battles over theological liberalism (last century and a half) add complexity for both Protestants and Catholics.

### Since Protestants do not view their churches as the one true church but as part of the universal church, lack of institutional centralization is less of a problem.

## What Issues Do Protestants Have with Catholics?

### Doctrinal and institutional accretions: slow, organic change over long periods; most traditions that distinguish Catholicism are not apostolic or biblical but result of evolution over centuries.

### The problem is not change per se but change unaccountable to and even contradictory to the word of God; adding to the gospel is not a small matter.

### Church history is long and decentralized; a teaching appearing in the fifth century is "late"—hundreds of years from the apostles (as distant as Oliver Cromwell from Donald Trump); errors could have crept in between Paul and Basil or Chrysostom, let alone the Middle Ages.

### Four central problems (to be addressed in the series)

#### The papacy

- Not expressly taught in scripture (contrary to Vatican I).
- Historical evidence shows the bishop of Rome's role in the early church was not what Roman apologists claim today.
- The Pope now claims extraordinary powers; the papacy is sustained more by institutional heft than by strength of historical claims.
- Covered in depth in episodes 2 and 3.

#### The cult of Mary and Marian dogmas

- Mary is most blessed, Theotokos, full of grace, given the greatest honor conceivable for a human.
- If Protestants are right, Mary would be appalled at how Roman Catholics relate to her.
- Official hyper-veneration and later Marian dogmas are contrary to scripture and church history and detract from the unique honor due to Christ.
- Explored in episode 4.

#### Justification by faith

- The current Roman church mangles Paul on grace and justification (especially Galatians and Romans).
- Resulting mistakes led to strange developments: indulgences, storage of merits, priestly mediation of forgiveness; the gospel is obscured and the believer robbed of security.
- Covered in episode 6.

#### Inconsistencies, errors, and undefined tradition

- Tradition and magisterial authority are extremely undefined (e.g., "What is tradition?").
- Disagreement over how often the Pope has spoken infallibly (e.g., only twice vs. many times); many popes believed they were speaking authoritatively on subjects no longer recognized.
- Ambiguity leaves thousands of possibly infallible documents unread and unstudied.
- Unsavory elements in tradition: burning heretics, intense anti-Semitism, indulgences for crusades, subjugation of infidels—once embraced, now ignored.
- Contradictory answers depending on document or theologian: Are all non-Catholics damned? (Unam Sanctam, etc.) Is all monetary interest sinful? Did the Pope authorize Spain to conquer and reduce infidels to "perpetual servitude"? (Dum diversas.) Do papal indulgences remain valid and questioning them damnable?
- Tradition supporting official theology was riddled with forgeries accepted as foundational, later quietly omitted from canon law and theological argument.
- Authority of tradition is a focus of episode 5.

## Conclusion

### All these errors ought to be rejected; they are not supported by the word of God or by early church tradition and they distract from the true preaching of Christ.

### Cardinal John Henry Newman is often misquoted: "To be deep in history is to cease to be Protestant."

### To be even deeper in history means moving past trends and fads of different eras back to the infallible word of God and the teachings of Christ and his apostles.

### History and scripture, the early church fathers and the apostles, led the reformers to see how far the Renaissance church had wandered from the pure proclamation of Christ.

### To be truly deep in history is to be Pauline and Protestant: clinging to the pure word of God and rejecting accumulated errors and abuses—indulgences, medieval papal power grabs, forgeries, aberrant Marian theology, and more.

## Core Ideas

- Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) is the infallible rule for faith and practice; Protestantism rejects the Roman magisterium and tradition as equally authoritative with scripture.
- Justification is by grace through faith in Christ alone (*solus Christus*, *sola fide*, *sola gratia*); the Roman teaching of justification by faith and works and related practices (indulgences, merits, priestly mediation) obscures the gospel.
- The papacy is not taught in scripture; the bishop of Rome's role in the early church was not what Roman Catholicism claims; the papacy is sustained by institutional power more than by historical or biblical evidence.
- Roman Catholic distinctives (Marian dogmas, purgatory, transubstantiation, etc.) are doctrinal accretions developed over centuries, not apostolic or biblical, and some contradict scripture.
- Tradition and magisterial authority in Roman Catholicism are undefined and inconsistent; tradition includes forgeries and morally troubling teachings that are now ignored, undermining claims to infallibility.
- Historic Protestantism presents itself as restoration (*ad fontes*) to scripture and early church approach, not as innovation; denominational diversity does not undermine shared commitment to scripture as ultimate authority.
- Deep engagement with history and scripture leads to a Protestant, Pauline position—rejecting accumulated errors and returning to the pure word of God and the teaching of Christ and his apostles.

## Key Terms

- **Magisterium** — The teaching office of the Roman church (councils and papal pronouncements), which claims to guard and authoritatively interpret the deposit of faith.
- **Deposit of faith** — In Roman Catholicism, the faith once delivered to the saints, composed of scripture and tradition as two equally authoritative parts.
- **Five Solas** — Reformation slogans: Scripture alone, Christ alone, faith alone, grace alone, glory to God alone.
- **Sola Scriptura** — Scripture alone is the infallible rule for faith and practice.
- **Indulgences** — In Roman teaching, remissions of temporal punishment for sin (often linked to payment); one catalyst for the Reformation.
- **Transubstantiation** — The doctrine that the bread and wine of the Eucharist become the body and blood of Christ in substance while retaining the appearance of bread and wine.
- **Theotokos** — "God-bearer"; title for Mary as mother of God, used in orthodox Christology.
- **Ad fontes** — "To the sources"; Renaissance and Reformation impulse to return to original texts (e.g., scripture and early church) rather than medieval tradition.
- **Unam Sanctam** — Papal bull (1302) often cited regarding the necessity of submission to the Roman pontiff for salvation.
- **Dum diversas** — Papal bull (1452) cited in connection with authorization of conquest and subjugation of infidels.
