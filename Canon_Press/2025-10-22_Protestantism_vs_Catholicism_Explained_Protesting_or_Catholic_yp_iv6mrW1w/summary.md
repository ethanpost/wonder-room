# Summary

This material contrasts Roman Catholicism and historic Protestantism. Rome teaches that it is the one true church under the Pope, who holds apostolic authority from Peter; its magisterium guards a “deposit of faith” made of scripture and tradition as two equal authorities. Protestants emerged in the early 1500s as reform movements (sparked by Luther’s 95 Theses) that rejected papal and traditional infallibility in favor of scripture alone. The Five Solas—Scripture alone, Christ alone, faith alone, grace alone, glory to God alone—sum up the Protestant stance: we are justified by grace through faith in Christ alone, with scripture as the only infallible rule. The outline argues that key Roman distinctives (papacy, Marian dogmas, justification by faith and works, undefined tradition) are not taught in scripture, rest on weak history, and often contradict the gospel; deep engagement with history and scripture is presented as leading to a Protestant, Pauline commitment to the pure word of God.

# Core Question

Are the central teachings that divide Roman Catholicism from Protestantism—the papacy, tradition as equal to scripture, justification by faith and works, Marian dogmas—faithful to scripture and early Christianity, or are they later accretions that obscure the gospel?

# Key Concepts

- **Scripture vs. scripture + tradition** — Protestants hold scripture alone as the infallible rule; Rome holds scripture and tradition as two equal parts of the deposit of faith, interpreted by the magisterium.
- **Justification** — Protestants: by grace through faith in Christ alone; Rome: by faith and works, with sacramental and priestly mediation, leading to practices like indulgences and “stored” merits.
- **The Five Solas** — Scripture alone, Christ alone, faith alone, grace alone, glory to God alone; the shared core of historic Protestant belief.
- **Doctrinal accretions** — Many distinctively Catholic teachings (papacy, Marian dogmas, purgatory, transubstantiation) developed over centuries and are not apostolic or biblical; some contradict scripture.
- **Ad fontes** — Protestants frame their position as restoration to scripture and the early church, not innovation; the contrast is “ancient sources vs. ancient sources plus medieval tradition.”
- **Tradition undefined** — Roman tradition and magisterial authority are vague and inconsistent; tradition includes forgeries and morally troubling teachings that are now ignored, undermining claims to infallibility.

# Why It Matters

The Protestant–Catholic divide concerns authority (who interprets the faith?), the gospel (how are sinners justified?), and the nature of the church. As secularism pushes Christians together, the material insists the differences are not superficial: they touch the core of the Christian message. Understanding these distinctives helps people see why the two traditions disagree, evaluate claims about history and scripture, and recognize what is at stake in debates over the papacy, Mary, justification, and tradition.
