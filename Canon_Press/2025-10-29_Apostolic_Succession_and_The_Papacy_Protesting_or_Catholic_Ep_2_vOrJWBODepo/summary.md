# Summary

Roman Catholicism’s defining claim is the apostolic authority of the papacy: the bishop of Rome asserts universal jurisdiction and infallibility. That claim rests on four intertwined doctrines—apostolic succession, Petrine primacy, Roman primacy, and the infallibility of the church—and if any one fails, the whole edifice collapses. The material argues that both apostolic succession and Petrine primacy are weak foundations. In the New Testament and earliest Christian writings, “bishop” and “elder” are the same office; a distinct episcopal office emerged only later, about a century after Peter, as a practical measure for unity (St. Jerome and modern scholarship agree). Scripture does not give Peter authority over the other apostles (James presides at Jerusalem; Paul rebukes Peter; the keys are given to all in Matthew 18), and bishops succeed the apostles only in ordinary ministry, not in the extraordinary apostolic role. Rome’s unique claim is also contested by Antioch and Alexandria, and Rome’s early prominence was largely political. The takeaway: bishops were a practical organizational development, not the divinely mandated basis of church legitimacy and authority.

# Core Question

How did Rome arrive at its dramatic claims for the papacy (universal jurisdiction, infallibility), given that these powers are not clearly set out in Scripture—and do apostolic succession and Petrine primacy provide a sufficient basis?

# Key Concepts

- **Four doctrines, one chain** — Apostolic succession, Petrine primacy, Roman primacy, and infallibility of the church undergird the papacy; if one is wrong, the papal project collapses.
- **Bishop and elder were one office** — In the NT and Apostolic Fathers, episcopos and presbyteros are interchangeable; a distinct office of bishop emerged later as a practical measure, not by divine mandate.
- **Jerome’s testimony** — Bishops were chosen by presbyters for unity (e.g., at Alexandria); they are successors of the apostles through the presbyterate, not through a special exclusive lineage.
- **Petrine primacy is tenuous** — James presides at Jerusalem; Paul rebukes Peter; the keys are given to all; the “rock” was read differently by the fathers; ecumenical dialogue admits the NT offers no sufficient basis for Roman primacy.
- **Ordinary vs. extraordinary succession** — Bishops succeed the apostles only in ministry (preaching, sacraments, discipline), not in the extraordinary apostolic role (eyewitness, revelation, judging the twelve tribes), so they cannot inherit Peter’s supposed unique authority.
- **Rome’s prominence was political** — Other sees (Antioch, Alexandria) have Petrine claims; what distinguished Rome was its status as capital of the empire, not a clear divine mandate.

# Why It Matters

The question of whether the pope has universal jurisdiction and infallibility shapes ecumenical dialogue, Protestant–Catholic debate, and how Christians understand church authority and the relationship between Scripture and tradition. Understanding that apostolic succession and Petrine primacy lack the biblical and historical support often claimed for them helps evaluate Rome’s claims and clarifies that later episcopal government can be a prudent development without requiring belief in a divinely instituted three-office hierarchy or a unique Roman succession.
