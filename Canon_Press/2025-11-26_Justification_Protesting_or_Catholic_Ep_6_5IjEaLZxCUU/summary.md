# Summary

Canon Press closes its six-part *Protesting or Catholic* series with a wide-angle comparison of **justification** in confessional Reformed theology and the **Council of Trent** as popularly received. The Reformed side is presented as **forensic**: God **counts** sinners righteous by **imputing Christ’s righteousness** to those **united to Christ by faith**, with **imperfect** post-conversion works and **final confidence** resting on Christ’s finished work. The Roman Catholic side is presented as **transformative**: baptismal **renewal** and **infused** grace establish **inherent righteousness** such that, with Christ’s grace **preceding, accompanying, and following** works, the believer’s deeds can **truly merit** eternal life at judgment. The episode argues that **merit mechanics** and the **definition** of justification—not only slogans about faith—set the real dividing line, then traces **faith** grammar on both sides (Westminster’s **instrumental** living faith vs Trent’s need for **hope and charity** and **fides caritate formata**). It ends with pastoral and Pauline appeals (**Philippians**, **Romans**, **James**, **Psalms**, **Ephesians**, **Galatians**) favoring **extrinsic** confidence in Christ over an **intrinsic** merit ladder sustained by sacraments.

# Core Question

Do believers stand accepted before God on judgment day because **grace-shaped personal righteousness truly merits** eternal life, or because they are **clothed in Christ’s perfect righteousness** credited to them in **union with him**—and can both sides affirm “grace alone” while still meaning something radically different by **peace with God**?

# Key Concepts

- **Forensic vs transformative justification** (declared righteous vs made inherently just)
- **Imputation** of Christ’s righteousness vs **infusion** of sanctifying grace and **inherent justice**
- **Initial vs final justification** and the **sacramental** pathway (baptism, penance, Eucharist)
- **Condign merit**, Trent’s **heated** debates, and claimed **post-Trent anathemas** on certain Augustinian lines
- **Concupiscence** and whether remaining desire is **sin**, as a hinge for **true merit** language
- **Saving faith** as **notitia / assensus / fiducia** vs faith’s **substance** as **intellectual assent** plus distinct **hope and charity**
- **Union with Christ** in Paul as the speaker’s assurance anchor against a **“sacramental treadmill”** reading

# Why It Matters

Justification is not an abstract relic of the Reformation; it shapes **conscience**, **assurance**, **preaching**, **evangelism**, and how people hear **God’s law** and **gospel**. If **works** truly **merit** salvation in any strict sense, pastoral questions shift toward **sufficient intrinsic righteousness**; if **Christ’s obedience** is the sole **ground** of acceptance, pastoral questions shift toward **resting** on another’s **finished** work amid **real** sin that remains. The episode invites viewers to test both schemes against **Scripture’s** courtroom and **in Christ** language—and to notice where **Trent’s** categories, as summarized, align or clash with **Paul’s**.
