## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and guest

- Gavin Ortlund introduces the channel as theology and apologetics pursued in an **irenic** (peace-seeking) way.
- Guest **Joshua Shuping**: pastor in the **Christian and Missionary Alliance**, church in **Russellville, Arkansas**; married with three children; originally from Florida; previously served in **northeastern Pennsylvania**; story is about returning to evangelicalism after serving as an Orthodox priest—not to attack other traditions.

### Pre-conversion background and path into a Reformed Baptist church

- In his late 20s Shuping describes himself as **unconverted**, a **syncretist** with an eclectic “American grab-bag” spirituality (including study of Far Eastern philosophy and theology).
- God used Christian friends and the impending birth of his son **Isaiah**; a pivotal sense of God asking whether he would give his son his “best guess” or God.
- At a used Christian bookstore in **Fern Park / Castleberry, Florida**, a young man engaged him, shared the gospel winsomely with strong Bible knowledge, and “evangelized” him despite Shuping’s prior exposure to church, youth group, Young Life, Windy Gap, etc.
- That church was **Reformed Baptist**: strong discipleship, verse-by-verse preaching (sometimes very long), attentive congregation—but also **strident**, strong on **separation** (false religion vs. Christianity), which jarred someone from an eclectic background.

### Attraction to Eastern Orthodoxy

- He kept reading; encountered **Philokalia**, **The Way of a Pilgrim**, authors such as **Theophan the Recluse**, and broader Eastern Orthodox spirituality.
- Orthodox sources spoke to his **experiential** side and helped with **Trinity** and **Incarnation** in ways Western presentations had not (he had misunderstood doctrine almost as mythic “Zeus / demigod” caricatures, not attributing fault to his friends).
- Works such as **Lossky** (*The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church*) and **Athanasius** *On the Incarnation* clarified **fully God and fully man** and Trinitarian theology beyond shallow analogies (e.g., water states).
- Attraction included rhetoric of the **unchanging Church**, **hesychasm**, **theosis**, beautiful **liturgy** (e.g., **St. John Chrysostom**), **sanctification of time** and feasts, and the Philokalia’s scope (roughly 4th–15th c. texts on prayer and the interior life).

### Seminary, ordination, and tenure as a priest

- Felt **called to ministry** early after conversion; wanted to teach and share the gospel.
- Entered Orthodox **catechumenate** around age **29**; after catechumenate and birth of daughter **Emma**, attended **St. Vladimir’s Orthodox Theological Seminary** (Yonkers, New York)—about **four years**, MDiv plus extra year **ThM**, graduated around **2015** per his recollection.
- Returned to Florida, **chaplain** about a year, then ordained in **ROCOR** (Russian Orthodox Church Outside Russia) though he had begun in **OCA**; served as priest about **five years** total in Orthodoxy.

### What he appreciated in Orthodoxy (non-bashing frame)

- **Liturgy**: structure created space for reflection, formation, meditation; heavily **biblical** (Psalms, Scripture); theologically rich hymns.
- **Historicity**: names like **Chrysostom** and **Basil the Great** on liturgies; stability amid cultural drift.
- **Interior life / “biblical psychology”** in patristic and Philokalia streams—insight into thoughts, attention, quiet before God; he found this more approachable in EO than in his prior evangelical experience (with nuance that Catholics have related resources).
- **Heroic prayer** practices (e.g., long prayer, **Jesus Prayer**).

### Reasons for return—experiential and doctrinal (two broad categories)

**Initial fit with certain modern EO moods (his account):**

- He was not deeply challenged doctrinally at entry in the way he later needed; **universalism** (e.g., **David Bentley Hart**—all ultimately saved) and **perennialism** resonated with his prior syncretism; **Frithjof Schuon**, **Philip Sherrard** (translator of Philokalia with **Kallistos Ware**), etc.
- **Reformed Baptist** preaching had emphasized **penal substitution**, hell, necessity of salvation, **Christ alone**—heard as harsh “legalism” and a **wrathful** God; he did not yet understand **impassibility** / **immutability**.
- Popular EO presentation: no **divine wrath**, no **penal substitutionary atonement (PSA)**—that had drawn him in but later became a reason tension grew.

**Seminary and patristic/hymnographic evidence (his reading):**

- At seminary, rhetoric often: “we know where the Church is, not where it isn’t”; **fullness** vs. less full forms outside Orthodoxy; non-Orthodox not necessarily damned in casual conversation—yet he began finding **PSA-like** language in **St. Symeon the New Theologian** (homilies, incl. *On the First-Created Man*), **festal hymns** (e.g., feasts of the Cross), **St. Cyril of Alexandria**—including a definition of **God’s wrath** as **justice applied destructively**, not passionate anger.
- A priest told him to check the **Menaion** and assured him PSA wasn’t there; Shuping believed he found it there.
- **John Chrysostom** (on 2 Corinthians 5 and Galatians 3 per his memory): imagery of Christ as **substitute** for the criminal, bearing **punishment** and **guilt**.
- Conclusion he draws: **20th–21st century popular EO presentations** often contradict what he found in fathers and hymnography; widespread claim “Orthodoxy is the alternative to PSA” conflicted with his research.

### Doctrine of the Church—COVID study project and formal documents

- During **COVID** closures, he taught parishioners from **older conciliar/catechetical** sources rather than only modern priests/bishops: **St. Filaret of Moscow**’s catechisms (late 1800s translations), **Dositheus** / **Confession of Dositheus** (Jerusalem, **1672**), **St. Peter Mogila**’s confession/catechism—re-typesetting and republishing neglected translations.
- **Dositheus**: visibility of the Church—statements that one cannot be called **Christian** outside canonical Orthodoxy; clashed with seminary-era “there are Christians elsewhere” talk.
- Modern vs. formal voices: cites **Hieromonk Patrick (Ramsey)** and **Fr. Josiah Trenham** (per his summary) as candid about **exclusive** formal ecclesiology vs. what many parishioners assume.
- **Synodikon of Orthodoxy** (read annually, **Sunday of Orthodoxy**): **anathema** on those who affirm the **Filioque** (Spirit proceeds from Father **and Son**)—implication: binding exclusion of Catholics and Protestants who hold that; also (in his reading) **double anathema** for **unleavened bread** in the Eucharist, with **Armenian Orthodox** named—he could not affirm such exclusivity.
- **Protopresbyter Michael Pomazansky**’s standard English dogmatics text as reinforcing similar strict lines.

### Unity, fragmentation, and evangelical ecclesiology (response to Ortlund)

- EO exhibits much **real disunity** despite rhetoric: **paper unity**—e.g., **Constantinople vs. Moscow**, **Old Calendarist** splits, local prejudice **cradle vs. convert**, internal bickering.
- **Unity around doctrine** is not automatic: rejecting PSA while **Chrysostom** or **Patriarch Jeremiah II** (Luther dialogue) affirm aspects of substitutionary themes = internal tension over what “unity” means.
- **Maximalist unity criteria**: many **canons**, seven ecumenical councils, Marian feasts, icon controversy outcomes, calendar, Filioque, etc.—vs. **Cyprian of Carthage**’s 3rd-c. context (no full later apparatus). Such maximalism cannot unify even with **Oriental Orthodox** (e.g., **Chalcedon**).
- Thought experiment: EO, OO, Catholic, Anglican each claim **unchanged** faith, fathers, councils—same three slogans; suspicious parallel.
- **Reformation** as lowering threshold from maximalism to a **baseline** (not minimalism): **Scripture alone**, **Christ alone**, **grace alone**, **faith alone**, **God’s glory alone**; most confessional evangelicals share **Apostles’** or **Nicene** creed.
- **Denominations** as **distinction**, not necessarily **division of Christ**—analogy from liturgy: Christ **broken but not divided** in the Eucharist; many orgs need not divide Christ.
- Orthodox **formal** position (as he reads it): non-Orthodox outside salvation ordinarily; evangelicals typically do not say all non-[denomination] are damned; he argues converts should face whether they affirm Baptist **grandma** is **not** Christian/saved per strict EO ecclesiology.

### History—evangelicals and the historic Church

- **“All Christian history is evangelical history”**—continuity through people and places (e.g., imaginary **German village** pre-Reformation: same priests and laity **Catholic one day, Lutheran the next** after Wittenberg; question who “moved”).
- **Luther** stripping medieval “coats and hats” vs. innovating the gospel.
- **Messiness** is universal (e.g., **Corinth**); EO/RC rhetoric of total resolution is overstated.

### Bishops, succession, and evangelical use of history

- **Anglicans/Episcopalians** and many **Lutherans** have bishops—partial answer to “you have no bishops.”
- **Presbyterian view** (aligned with Ortlund’s prior arguments per Shuping): **elder** and **bishop** as **one office** biblically; separating a third **episcopal** tier is the **innovation**; cites **Jerome** (4th–5th c.) in that vein.
- **Apostolic succession** as **laying on of hands** through **elders** into Protestant orders; **Luther**, **Peter Martyr Vermigli**, many priests became Reformed; **Baptist** lines also debated but many priests joined early Baptists.
- Organic succession: **flesh-and-blood** transmission of faith across generations in a village narrative.
- **Sola scriptura** strength (immediate gospel) vs. **handicap** (less push to master history); average **cradle Orthodox** may not know patristic detail more than average evangelical—names without substance.
- **Patristics as discipline**: early Lutherans (e.g., **Chemnitz**, **Gerhard**) returned to fathers against scholastic manuals; **Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers** series edited by **German Reformed** and **Anglican** scholars—Protestant scholarship benefits all readers.
- Pastoral center: **Christ** is life, not **Augustine/Aquinas/Nazianzen** as ultimate anchor—evangelical instinct.

### Eucharist / Lord’s Supper

- In **Protestant history**, **real presence** is the **normal** doctrinal position among informed formularies and theologians; rejection of **transubstantiation** is often misread as **mere symbol**—many Reformers denied change in bread’s substance while affirming true feeding on Christ.
- Examples he names: **Herman Bavinck**, **Heinrich Bullinger** (Second Helvetic Confession), **Peter Martyr Vermigli**, **Beza**, **Thomas Boston**—soul feeds on Christ truly, not necessarily **local corporeal** presence in the elements.
- **Sun and rays** image (Bullinger / Second Helvetic): Christ in heaven, presence “shining” through the sacrament; bread’s substance remains bread; mystery retained.
- Judge traditions by **founding documents** and major theologians, not only pew opinions—parallel to not judging EO by one parish only.

### Advice for evangelicals considering another tradition

- **Dissatisfaction** with local church often gets **externalized** as “the church is wrong” instead of a call to **go deeper** and **serve** where one is.
- **Backfill history**: trace **denomination**, **seminary**, **theologians** (**Spurgeon** → Puritans → etc.) instead of leaping from **Apostolic Fathers** to modern EO/RC claims without the **middle** 2000 years.
- **Meet in the middle**: early church + **Reformation** arguments (**Luther**, **Calvin**, **Chemnitz**, **Gerhard**, **Beza**) rather than stopping at a few Greek fathers and assuming modern institutional claims.
- Reformers were **patristic readers**; shallow local evangelicalism ≠ the whole Western story.

### Advice for evangelicals wanting more historical rootedness

- **Gerald Bray** on historical theology (title paraphrased in conversation—book on God speaking / historical theology).
- **W. Robert Godfrey** (Ligonier): multi-part **church history DVD** set (~20 min lectures) with booklets—overview of shape of church history.
- **Reformation Study Bible** (e.g., **Joel Beeke** edition): roughly one article per century.
- Treat reading/listening as **providential** formation for being the Church **now**.

## Core Ideas

- Irenic apologetics can host difficult **conversion narratives** across traditions without bashing.
- Attraction to Orthodoxy often combines **liturgical beauty**, **patristic depth**, and **reaction against** harsh or shallow Western presentations.
- **Patristic and hymnographic** material complicates neat **“East vs. West on PSA”** slogans—at least in this speaker’s reading.
- **Formal conciliar/catechetical** texts may articulate **stricter ecclesiology** than casual seminary or parish talk.
- **Unity** claims require scrutiny: **rhetoric vs. practice**, and **criteria** for communion (maximalist vs. creedal baseline).
- Evangelical **historical** identity can be framed as **continuity** through **reformation** of accretions, not absence of history.
- **Real presence** in the Supper is a mainstream **Reformed/Protestant** heritage distinct from **transubstantiation** and from **mere memorialism**.

## Key Terms

- **Irenic**: peace-seeking, charitable discourse.
- **Syncretism / perennialism / universalism**: themes Shuping associates with his pre-conversion mindset and with some modern Orthodox-adjacent reading.
- **Penal substitutionary atonement (PSA)**: Christ as substitute bearing penalty/guilt; he argues it appears in sources often said to oppose it.
- **Impassibility / immutability**: divine attributes that reframed “wrath” for him.
- **Theosis / hesychasm / Philokalia**: Eastern emphases he valued.
- **ROCOR / OCA**: Orthodox jurisdictions in his path.
- **Filioque**: Western creedal addition; focus of Synodikon anathemas in his account.
- **Synodikon of Orthodoxy**: liturgically proclaimed text with anathemas (Sunday of Orthodoxy).
- **Dositheus (1672)**, **Peter Mogila**, **Filaret of Moscow**: confessional/catechetical sources he republished.
- **Sola Scriptura / Solas**: baseline for evangelical unity in his argument.
- **Real presence** vs. **transubstantiation**: distinction for Protestant Eucharistic doctrine.
