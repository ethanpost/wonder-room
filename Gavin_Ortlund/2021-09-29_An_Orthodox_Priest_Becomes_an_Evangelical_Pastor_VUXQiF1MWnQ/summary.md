# Summary

Gavin Ortlund interviews Joshua Shuping, a Christian and Missionary Alliance pastor who spent roughly five years as an Orthodox priest (ROCOR) after St. Vladimir’s Seminary. Shuping traces his path from syncretism through a Reformed Baptist conversion, into Eastern Orthodoxy for liturgical depth and patristic clarity on the Trinity and Incarnation, and finally back to evangelicalism. His turn hinged on reading older conciliar and catechetical texts during COVID—alongside fathers and hymnography—that, in his judgment, undercut common modern Orthodox rhetorical contrasts on atonement and ecclesiology. He argues that formal documents and liturgical anathemas teach an exclusivity he could not affirm, while “unity” rhetoric often masks real jurisdictional and parish-level division. He ends by defending evangelicals’ historical continuity (Reformation as stripping accretions, not inventing the gospel), a single-office reading of elder/bishop, classical Protestant real presence in the Eucharist, and practical steps to study church history without leaving one’s tradition.

# Core Question

How should someone weigh popular and pastoral presentations of a tradition against older formal texts and patristic sources—and what happens to claims of unity, history, and sacramental theology when that comparison is pressed?

# Key Concepts

- **Irenic dialogue** across Christian traditions without bashing.
- **PSA, wrath, and patristic/hymnographic** language as a tension point between narrative and sources.
- **Formal ecclesiology** (Synodikon, confessions, dogmatics) vs. informal inclusivist talk.
- **Maximalist vs. creedal baseline** models of church unity.
- **Protestant real presence** distinguished from transubstantiation and from mere memorialism.
- **Historical backfilling** (denomination → seminary → Reformers → fathers) before converting out.

# Why It Matters

Stories of clergy crossing traditions shape how people understand Orthodoxy, Protestantism, authority, and salvation. This conversation offers a detailed insider account of which sources and experiences moved one person’s conscience—useful for fair-minded comparison, for diagnosing when rhetoric outruns documentation, and for evangelicals who need encouragement that confessional Protestantism has its own deep historical and sacramental resources without requiring maximalist institutional unity.
