## Detailed Outline

### Purpose and framing

- **Part two** of reflections on **leaving the Eastern Orthodox Church**, following a **conversation hosted on Gavin Ortlund’s YouTube channel**.
- Speaker (**Joshua Shooping**) addresses **questions and issues** raised after part one.
- **Central topic:** **church exclusivity**—clarifying that the critique is **not** merely that Orthodoxy is “too exclusive” in a generic sense.

### What is *not* being criticized: generic exclusivity

- The objection is **not** to exclusivity **as such** (every tradition draws boundaries).
- The target is a **particular kind of sectarianism** tied to **Orthodox ecclesiology** (with **functional parallels** drawn to **Roman Catholic** claims in places).

### “Rorschach” / lack of consensus within Orthodoxy

- Orthodoxy is described as lacking a **single, stable consensus**—like an **inkblot** or **Rorschach**: people **read in** what they want.
- Different people anchor “the fullness of the church” in **high liturgy**, **Eucharistic piety**, **iconography**, **theosis**, **prayer traditions**, or “all of the above,” with **gospel** sometimes **smothered** amid talk of fullness.
- This **blends into** the **exclusivity** question: who counts as **in** the one true church when **what Orthodoxy is** is itself contested?

### Historical narrative: Constantine to imperial Christianity

- After **Constantine**, worship moves from **homes** toward **large public** **basilicas/cathedrals**; **monasticism** rises in the **early fourth century** as spirituality shifts.
- **Liturgical theology** distinguishes **monastic rite** and **cathedral rite** emerging in the **fourth century**, later **blended** over centuries—so **“nothing has changed”** is questioned when **archaeology of liturgical texts** shows **adaptation** and **cultural** variation.
- **Latin (Western) rite** is described as **equally ancient** in some respects and **strikingly different** in feel from the **Eastern** rite; other **rites** have **fallen out of use** or been **partially restored** (e.g. references to **Gallican** streams).
- **Fourth-century “metamorphosis”:** church becomes **public**, no longer **hiding** from persecution or **dependent on synagogues** in the same way.

### Theodosius, law, and “lex orandi, lex credendi”

- Under **Theodosius** (end of fourth century), **Christianity** becomes the **only legal religion**; **being a good citizen** and **being Christian** **merge** legally.
- Speaker ties this **ecclesiological metamorphosis** to **elaborate courtly/Oriental** style in worship (investments, processions), with **Hagia Sophia**-type influence cited.
- The principle **the rule of prayer becomes the rule of faith** is invoked to argue **real change** across **Constantine → Theodosius → Justinian** and beyond (e.g. **iconoclasm** in the eighth century), so **simple continuity** from the **first three centuries** is called **unsustainable**.
- Examples of discontinuity: **monastic service schedule (typicon)**; **exclusively celibate/unmarried bishops**—things the **early church** would **not** have recognized as a full package, in the speaker’s telling.

### Councils, canons, and widening criteria of “Christian”

- After **Nicaea** and the **second ecumenical council** (Constantinople), **Assyrian Church of the East** breaks away; later criteria require accepting **Ephesus** and an expanding **corpus of canons** and decrees.
- After **Chalcedon**, **Oriental Orthodox** communities **do not** accept it; **Orthodox canonical teaching** (as described) would require them to **accept Chalcedon** and **subsequent ecumenical councils**—**major and minor** decisions—as **infallible declarations of the Holy Spirit**.
- **Ecumenical councils** are said to function for many Orthodox as **equivalent to Scripture** (or **nearly** so, **depending on whom you ask**).
- Result: a **constant increase** in **criteria** for who counts as **Christian** vs **not**.

### Orthodox exclusivity: visible church and the Spirit

- **Orthodox ecclesiology** (as portrayed): the **church** is the **visible body of Christ**; **outside** that visible communion one is **outside** the body.
- **Logical chain offered:** you either **have** the **Spirit of Christ** or you **don’t**; if you have the Spirit you **must** be **in the church**; therefore **outside the church** ⇒ **no Spirit** ⇒ **not truly Christian**.
- Reference to a **decree** (transcript: **Descithius**) and **Orthodox canonical tradition**: one must be **under a bishop** in that system to be considered **Christian**, let alone **fully** in the **church**.
- Speaker judges this exclusivity **beyond reasonable biblical proportion** compared with **Reformation** **solas**-shaped Protestant concerns.

### Contrast with Protestant denominational boundaries

- **All denominations are exclusive** at **some** level, but **Eastern Orthodoxy** is distinguished by tying **salvation** to **one visible ark**—**non-Orthodox** are **implicitly treated as unsaved** because **outside** that ark.
- **Presbyterian** example: **excluding** someone from **Presbyterianism** is **not** the same as saying they **cannot be saved**; **Francis Turretin** is cited as **affirming** that **non-Presbyterians** are **not necessarily** outside the **church**.

### Protestant view of visible vs invisible church

- **Church** is an **article of faith**, not **reducible to sight** alone—yet **visible churches** exist and differ in **purity** according to whether they **preach the gospel** (death/resurrection, **penal substitution**, **union with Christ**, victory over sin/death/evil, **fullness of atonement**), administer **baptism** and **Eucharist** **biblically**, and practice **discipline**—**marks** of where the **one holy catholic apostolic church** **manifests**.
- **Visible** **manifestation** is **not** **coextensive** with **one denomination’s** boundaries; the **invisible** reality of **believers** shows up in **various organizations**—including **Orthodox**, **Catholic**, **Oriental Orthodox**, **Presbyterian**, **Dutch Reformed**, **Lutheran**, **Alliance**, **Reformed**, etc.
- **Excluding** from **my denomination** ≠ **denying** **Christian** identity or **brother in Christ** status (in confessional Protestant ecclesiology as described).

### Pastoral experience: chaplaincy alongside other traditions

- Speaker worked in a **Florida hospital** with an **Eastern Orthodox** pastor, a **charismatic Episcopal** priest, a **Baptist** pastor, and a **Seventh-day Adventist** pastor.
- **Orthodox ecclesiology** would force calling them **not real Christians**, which he rejects as **false** given their **devotion** and **Spirit**-guided lives.
- **Affirming Christians outside one’s denomination** is called a **biblical** and **experiential** imperative.

### Scripture: sectarian slogans in Corinth

- **1 Corinthians**: “I am of Paul / Apollos / Cephas / **even of Christ**”—**sectarian** party spirit.
- Applied: **only our church is the true church**, **only we have Christ**—argued to be **contrary to Scripture**.
- **Unity** is sought **in Christ**, not in **“my group alone has Christ.”**
- Speaker **affirms** that **Orthodox believers have Christ** even when they **will not** reciprocate the affirmation.

### “Apostate” language

- He accepts being called an **apostate from a sect** in order to be **true to Christ**; denies **apostasy from Christ**.
- **Apostasy** here means rejecting **false Eastern Orthodox ecclesiology**, not Christ.

### Which communion is “the true church”?

- The **eclectic / Rorschach** picture of Orthodoxy is **not** accepted as **the** true, **uncorrupt**, **inerrant** church—especially when **Roman Catholics** make **parallel** claims and **who plays pope** to decide becomes contested.

### Protestant reading of Scripture vs “private judgment” trope

- Pushback against the claim that **Protestants** are each their **own pope** via **private interpretation**.
- Speaker counters with **wide Protestant** engagement with **plain meaning**, **Scripture interpreting Scripture**, and **community**—citing **Reformed** and other authors (**Bavinck**, **Turretin**, **Chemnitz**, **Gerhard**, **Wesley**, **Edwards**, **Owen**, **Boston**, **Goodwin**, etc.) as **not** mere **privateers**.
- **Anti-Protestant Orthodox apologetics** often **misstates** what Protestants teach; personal experience: when checking claims (“Protestants teach X”), **almost invariably** it **was not** the case—driving deeper reading of **Reformed** sources.

### Closing clarification

- **Critique** of **Orthodox ecclesiological exclusivism** is **not** **whining** about **boundaries** **per se**.
- **Every church** should **clarify boundaries** and be **exclusive** in **that** sense.
- What is **rejected** is **reductionistic exclusivism** that **ties salvation** to **membership in one denomination** alone.

## Core Ideas

- Orthodox exclusivity under critique is **sectarian** and **soteriological** (outside the visible Orthodox church ⇒ not truly saved), not mere **institutional** boundary-drawing.
- Historical **fourth-century shifts** (space, law, liturgy, monasticism) complicate **“unchanging continuity”** narratives.
- **Ecumenical councils** and **canons** expand **who must agree with what** to count as **in** the church.
- **Confessional Protestant** models can **exclude from a denomination** while still **affirming** **Christians** elsewhere.
- **1 Corinthians** party spirit is used as a **biblical** check on **“only we have Christ”** triumphalism.

## Key Terms

- **Church exclusivity** — who is in/out of the **true church** and on what grounds.
- **Visible body of Christ** — Orthodox formulation cited: church as **visible** communion.
- **Lex orandi, lex credendi** — worship shapes belief; used to argue **development** across centuries.
- **Ecumenical councils** — treated in the talk as **nearly Scripture-level** authority for many Orthodox.
- **Marks of the church** (Protestant list in talk) — **gospel** preaching, **sacraments**, **discipline**.
- **Sectarianism** — party spirit (“I am of …”) applied to **communion rivalry**.
