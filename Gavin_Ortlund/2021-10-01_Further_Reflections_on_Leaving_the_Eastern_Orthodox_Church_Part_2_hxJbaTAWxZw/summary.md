# Summary

Joshua Shooping continues his reflections on leaving Eastern Orthodoxy by narrowing a common misunderstanding: he is not attacking “exclusivity” in general, but a specific **sectarian ecclesiology** that treats the **visible Orthodox communion** as the **sole ark of salvation**, implying that those outside lack the **Spirit** and are **not truly Christian**. He sketches a **fourth-century shift** (public basilicas, monastic and cathedral rites, Theodosian establishment, and the interplay of worship and belief) to question **simple continuity** claims, then traces how **councils and canons** multiply criteria for who counts as **in** the church. He contrasts this with **confessional Protestant** boundaries: one can **exclude** from a **denomination** yet still affirm **brothers in Christ** elsewhere—supported by pastoral experience and by **1 Corinthians**’ warning against party spirit—while rejecting caricatures of Protestant **sola scriptura** reading.

# Core Question

When a church says it alone is the **visible body of Christ**, does that kind of **exclusivity** differ in **kind** (not only degree) from the boundaries Protestant denominations draw—and is that difference **faithful to Scripture and experience**?

# Key Concepts

- **Sectarian vs. ordinary exclusivity** — boundaries about membership vs. boundaries that **map onto salvation**.
- **“Rorschach” Orthodoxy** — competing visions of “fullness” without a single consensus.
- **Lex orandi, lex credendi** — prayer/worship shaping doctrine, used here to argue **historical development**.
- **Visible church and the Spirit** — the attributed logic: outside the visible communion ⇒ **no Spirit** ⇒ **not Christian**.
- **Marks of the church** (as listed) — **gospel**, **sacraments**, **discipline**, as tests of **visible faithfulness** without **one-denomination** identity.

# Why It Matters

How Christians define the **church** affects **conscience**, **evangelism**, **ecumenism**, and whether believers in other communities are treated as **imaginary** or **real**. The talk is a compressed argument that **where** you draw lines—and **whether** those lines imply **damnation**—changes the **moral and spiritual posture** of entire traditions toward one another.
