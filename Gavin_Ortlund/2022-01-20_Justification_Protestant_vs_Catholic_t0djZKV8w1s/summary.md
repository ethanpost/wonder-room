# Summary

Gavin Ortlund sketches a **Protestant account of justification** with a deliberately narrow aim: **clarify concepts and vocabulary** so listeners can see why an informed Protestant holds imputation, forensic reckoning language in Paul, and **sola fide**—without pretending the **Joint Declaration** ended church division or that **Catholic teaching** is simply the **Judaizing** error. He argues much heat comes from **defining “justification,” “faith,” and “works” differently**, then maps **real remaining disagreements** (especially **imputation vs infusion** and the **sacramental/penitential context** in Catholic life) while still naming **substantial overlap** (grace at the beginning, transformation afterward, necessity of good works as fruit). A long **church history** section contends novelty charges should separate **meaning** from **means**, then focuses on **John Chrysostom** as a patristic voice whose **Romans homilies** resist tidy narratives of sixteenth-century invention. The close holds **Trent’s anathemas** in view as a live obstacle yet points to **shared devotional gravity** (e.g., **Anselm**) beneath technical disagreement.

# Core Question

When Protestants and Catholics say **“justified,” “faith,” and “works,”** are they answering the **same question** with contradictory answers—or **different questions** encoded in **shared words**, with both **overlap** and **genuine formal differences** that still matter for unity and assurance?

# Key Concepts

- **Sola fide** as **instrumental** reception of Christ’s righteousness, not “belief alone” in a minimalistic sense
- **Distinction without separation** between **justification** and **sanctification**, centered on **union with Christ**
- **Initial vs ongoing justification** in Catholic systematization versus Protestant **forensic** usage
- **Imputation vs infusion** as competing accounts of the **formal cause** of righteousness
- **James 2 vs Paul** resolved (in this Protestant reading) by **sense of faith** and **sense of justify**
- **Meaning vs means** in assessing **historical continuity** and “novelty” claims
- **Ecumenism of conviction** and reading the other tradition **at its best**

# Why It Matters

Justification is not only academic: it shapes **conscience, assurance, identity, and charity** in actual churches. Ortlund’s framing matters because sloppy slogans (“faith alone” vs “faith plus works”) fuel **mutual anathemas and caricatures**, while patient definition-work can reduce **false conflict** and clarify **true conflict**—especially where **canon law, sacramental practice, and formal cause** still touch people’s spiritual lives and **ecumenical hope**.
