## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and framing

- Second **Truth Unites** interview with **Josh Shuping**, pastor of **Alliance Church** in **Russellville, Arkansas**, author of *Disillusioned: Why I Left the Eastern Orthodox Priesthood and Church*; this conversation focuses on **theology** (the prior interview focused more on his story).
- After the first video, Shuping and his wife faced **strong online negativity** (anger, harsh criticism); Ortlund recalls someone giving Shuping an **anathema** for leaving Orthodoxy, and Shuping responding by **blessing** that person.
- Shuping describes enduring criticism through **grace**, expecting some hurt reactions, trying to **empathize** with disappointment, and **limiting** exposure to toxic internet discourse from all traditions.
- Ortlund states the aim: **theological critique** motivated by **concern for truth** and a **pure gospel**, not personal attack or venting.

### Three structural concerns in the book

- Shuping organizes critique around **ecclesiology** (doctrine of the church), **icons** (veneration of images), and **mariology** (doctrine of Mary).
- He emphasizes these are tied to **formal, canonical** positions—not merely contemporary trends—so they map onto the **official** Eastern Orthodox framework and, in his view, are especially damaging to the overall claim.

### 1) Exclusivistic ecclesiology

- Shuping summarizes the concern: in his reading of **canonical** teaching, Eastern Orthodoxy formally maintains that **no one outside their communion is truly Christian**—not merely “not in full communion” but **lacking the Spirit**, **outside the ark**, **unsaved**—which he likens to **sectarian** “we alone have the truth.”
- **Historical backdrop (Shuping’s narrative):** After **Constantine** and especially moving toward **Theodosius** and **Justinian**, **church and state** increasingly fused; Christian identity and **citizenship** intertwined, which he argues **intensified** rigid boundaries and politicized disputes (e.g., **icons** tied to imperial sides).
- He describes an atmosphere where refusal to **venerate icons with affection** could be treated as placing one **outside the church** with **condemnation** language (keys, **Gehenna**).
- On **anathema**, he notes post-conciliar correspondence to an **empress** clarifying **damnation** / separation from God; he sees little medieval **exception** until **more recent** centuries—Ortlund adds **Synod of Jerusalem (1672)** as making stark claims about who counts as Christian under an Orthodox bishop.
- Shuping calls this a **double-edged** situation: if such statements are **false**, why have they not been authoritatively overturned? He connects modern **softening** or selective distance (“Latin captivity,” a phrase he attributes to **Kallistos Ware**) to **20th-century** intellectual and cultural pressures, including Orthodoxy **engaging Western academia** (he mentions **Georges Florovsky** teaching at **Princeton** after **Westminster** faculty had left for **Westminster Seminary**).

### Ortlund’s clarification: fruit, exclusivism, and universalism

- Ortlund distinguishes his **Matthew 7** “tree by its fruits” argument from **universalism** or **religious pluralism**: the target is **institutional exclusivism** he sees as **inconsistent with the New Testament**, not a denial that **confession of Christ** matters.
- Shuping agrees **fruit** must be assessed scripturally but insists **proper confession of Christ** is part of fruit; **faith comes by hearing the gospel**; fruit **verifies** authentic faith rather than **saving** apart from Christ. Ortlund adds **1 Corinthians 12:3** as a **doctrinal** test.

### Anxiety, assurance, and Protestant “catholicity”

- Ortlund reads from Shuping’s book (**p. 30**): competing **“one true church”** claims (he notes **Roman Catholic**, **Oriental Orthodox**, **Eastern Orthodox** in broad form) can send seekers on an **exhaustive**, **expert-level** hunt—yet **Romans 10**-style assurance is framed as knowing Christ.
- Shuping describes **pastoral anxiety**: people parsing **patriarchal politics**, **wars**, **WCC** membership, **Old Calendarist** splits, etc., as if **salvation** hung on mastering **contemporary church politics**—an **alternative object of faith** beside Christ and the gospel.
- For anxious believers, Shuping often turns to **Nicene** or **Apostles’ Creed**: the **church** is an object of **faith** trusted in **Christ**, not a **mathematical** problem solved by **ecclesiological rationalism**.
- On **Protestantism’s strength**, Shuping argues **confessional Protestant** documents (e.g., **Book of Concord**, **Westminster Standards**, **Three Forms of Unity**, **Thirty-Nine Articles**, **1689 London Baptist Confession**) do **not** claim **administrative unity equals the only true church**; **diversity of administration** reflects time, place, language, and culture, while **spiritual oneness** need not mean **Byzantine bureaucratic lockstep**.

### 2) Venerating icons

- Shuping challenges the popular slogan of an **“unchanged”** church; he recalls **seminary** liturgical history showing **repeated change**, contrasting with **romantic** time-travel imaginings of identical worship from **Justin Martyr** to **Cyril of Alexandria** to **John of Damascus**.
- **Patristic era (his reading):** Early apologetic literature repeatedly stresses **Christians do not venerate images** (sometimes opposing pagan image practice); he frames this as ***a fortiori*** (strong positive argument), not mere **argument from silence**.
- He claims the **arguments later used for icons** were earlier **used against** Christian practice until roughly a **century before John of Damascus**, when defenders **redeployed** them; **parish-level** appeals to “we’ve always done this for generations” do not match **critical history** for him.
- **Architectural art** on walls does not, in his view, establish **liturgical veneration** or **affection**; some evidence may be **outside** primary worship spaces.
- **Second Council of Nicaea (787)** (“Nicaea II”): he stresses **binding anathemas** and the equation of refusal with rejecting **Christ** and the **Incarnation** in maximal terms; councils are treated as **infallible** alongside Scripture in much Orthodox systematics, so the issue is **canonical**, not optional.
- Ortlund cites **Richard Price**’s scholarly introduction to **Nicaea II** (Acts translation): the **iconoclast** historical claim—that **reverence toward images** did not go back to the **apostles** or early **golden-age fathers**—would be judged **correct** by impartial historians today; iconophile historiography as **denial of history**; concerns about **forged** texts and **weak** exegesis.
- Shuping contrasts **pastoral “free veneration”** talk with what he reads as **forced *proskynesis*** in the **conciliar** text—**compelled affection** he finds alien to **gospel freedom**.
- He finds **John of Damasus**’s move from **Incarnation** to **mandatory icon veneration** a **non sequitur**; **Old Testament types** (e.g., **bronze serpent**) are types of **Christ**, not of **iconography** as a system.
- **Biblical case:** the **Second Commandment**; if the Incarnation **relativized** it, Shuping expects explicit **NT** teaching; the **latria/dulia** distinction looks to him like a **post-hoc** justification (Ortlund’s analogy: a **wife** forbids **kissing other women**—a later “types of kisses” excuse without permission).
- Shuping notes biblical warnings that images are **not alive** and those who bow become **like** them—arguments he says John of Damascus **does not address**.
- **Spiritual concern:** reliance on **external painted images** vs. **indwelling Spirit**, **Abba Father**, Word **heard** inwardly—risk of **externalizing** union with God.
- **Historical mechanism (his thesis):** post-Constantinian influx of **less-converted** populations and **state pressure** made it hard to **tame** pagan **idolatry**; practices were **baptized** rather than eliminated—linked (he anticipates) to **mariological** excess as **goddess** impulses **reframed**.

### 3) Mariology

- Orthodoxy’s formal **Mary** theology is conveyed especially through **canonical hymns** and **feasts**, not only isolated patristic quotes—**half** of the major yearly feasts concern **Mary**, framing the liturgical **year** (her **Nativity** first, **Dormition** last in the cycle he describes).
- Later hymnography (7th–9th c. and following, in his sketch) ascribes a **supra-historical** role: one cannot approach **Jesus** unless **Mary** “blesses” the approach—mirroring **incarnation** logic in a way he finds **soteriologically** problematic.
- Ortlund reads **9th-century** prayers (Shuping’s book, **pp. 76–77**): the petitioner **dares not** approach Christ directly but seeks **salvation through Mary’s intercession**; Mary must **persuade** the **righteous judge**—language Shuping and Ortlund treat as **Mary doing propitiatory work** proper to **Christ** alone.
- Shuping argues this coheres with core devotionals like the **Akathist Hymn**, so “**both/and** (Mary leads to Jesus)” is often, in his view, **rhetoric** masking **functional displacement** of Christ’s **unique** mediation.

### Reform, Cyprian, and apologetic fairness

- Shuping suggests **Eastern Christianity** needed **reformation**, but **changelessness** rhetoric and **canonical maximalism** make **correction** institutionally **difficult**—admitting error at **Nicaea II**, for example, would undermine claimed **patristic** authority structures.
- On **Cyprian**: **pre-Constantinian**, **persecution** context; his polemic targets **rigorists** creating a **pure church** by refusing **restoration** of the **lapsed**—a different problem-set than later **ecumenical** fine-grained **communion** disputes (**Chalcedon**, **Ephesus**, etc.); attributing **maximal modern canonical unity** to Cyprian is, for Shuping, **anachronistic**.
- **Appendix engaging Josiah Trenham:** Shuping warns against **apples-to-oranges** comparisons—**street-level Protestant** messiness vs. **idealized official** Orthodox/Catholic theology (and vice versa). He recommends reading **systematic** Protestant dogmatics (e.g., **Herman Bavinck**, **Francis Pieper**) to see **full** **soteriology** (e.g., **union with Christ**), not judging a tradition only by **bad local parishes**.
- Closing contrast: Protestant **confessions** give relatively **clear** **ecclesiology** without, in his reading, **Nicaea II**-level **icon** coercion or the **three canonical problems** (exclusivism, icons, Mary) that block simply **preferring** an Orthodox systematician’s prose while **ignoring** canons.

## Core Ideas

- Formal **Eastern Orthodox** teaching on **church**, **icons**, and **Mary** is argued to be **maximal**, **binding**, and **pastorally burdensome** in ways distinct from **confessional Protestant** self-descriptions.
- **History** and **Scripture** are deployed to challenge **unchanging tradition** claims, especially around **image veneration** and **Nicaea II**.
- **Spiritual assurance** should rest on **Christ** and **creedal faith**, not on solving **impossible** institutional puzzles.
- **Fair comparison** requires **official-to-official** and **practice-to-practice** symmetry, not **idealized** one tradition vs. **caricatured** another.

## Key Terms

- **Exclusivistic ecclesiology** — institutional claim that salvation/true Christianity is confined to one visible communion (as Shuping reads EO canons).
- **Anathema** — in the conciliar context discussed, understood as **condemnation** / separation from God, not mere excommunication-lite.
- ***Proskynesis* / veneration** — bowing, kissing, **affection** toward icons; contrasted with “optional local custom” vs. **conciliar obligation**.
- ***Latria* / *dulia*** — worship vs. veneration distinction criticized as **extra-biblical** systematizing.
- **Synod of Jerusalem (1672)** — cited for stark statements on who counts as Christian under Orthodox episcopacy.
- **Nicaea II (787)** — Seventh Ecumenical Council; central to icon doctrine and **anathemas**.
- **Akathist Hymn** — major Marian devotional text said to align with maximal intercessory language.
- **Latin captivity** — phrase Shuping attributes to **Ware** as a rhetorical distancing from harsh past statements.
- ***A fortiori*** argument — reasoning from the early apologists’ unanimous anti-image posture to the improbability of silent universal icon **veneration**.
