# Summary

Gavin Ortlund interviews Joshua Shuping, a former Eastern Orthodox priest now pastoring in Arkansas, about the theological case in *Disillusioned*. Shuping argues that three **canonical** clusters—**exclusive ecclesiology**, **mandatory icon veneration** (especially as defined at **Nicaea II**), and **mariology** expressed in **hymns and prayers**—create a system that is spiritually harsh, historically overstated as “unchanging,” and pastorally burdensome. Ortlund adds historical and bibliographic support (e.g., **Richard Price** on Nicaea II), clarifies his own **Matthew 7** argument against **institutional exclusivism** without **pluralism**, and highlights **creedal faith** and **fair comparison** between traditions as pastoral and intellectual correctives.

# Core Question

When a communion claims to be the **one true church** with **infallible councils** and **maximal anathemas**, how should seekers weigh **historical evidence**, **canonical texts**, **Scripture**, and **gospel assurance**—especially when online discourse and church politics amplify fear?

# Key Concepts

- **Exclusivistic ecclesiology** — salvation and Christian identity tied strictly to one institutional boundary (as Shuping reads Orthodox canons).
- **Nicaea II (787)** — conciliar binding force, **anathemas**, and the role of **affection** toward icons in the argument.
- **Patristic witness on images** — early apologetic literature versus later icon theology; ***a fortiori*** vs. **silence** debates.
- **Mariology in hymnody** — feasts and prayers as **formal** theology; **Akathist** and printed prayers as allegedly **Christ-displacing** intercession.
- **Apples-to-oranges apologetics** — comparing **ideal** official theology to **worst** local Protestant practice (or the reverse).

# Why It Matters

Many people convert or anguish over communions under the pressure of **fear**, **romanticized history**, and **asymmetric** arguments. This conversation models how to **name** concrete theological stakes, **cite** primary and scholarly sources, **pastor** anxious consciences toward **Christ**, and **compare traditions fairly**—skills that matter for honest ecumenical discourse and for anyone weighing **Orthodoxy** and **Protestant confessionalism**.
