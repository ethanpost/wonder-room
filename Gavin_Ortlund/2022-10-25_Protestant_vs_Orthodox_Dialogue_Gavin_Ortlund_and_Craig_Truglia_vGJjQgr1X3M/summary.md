# Summary

Gavin Ortlund and Craig Truglia each explain why they hold their tradition—Protestant and Eastern Orthodox—and then press each other on the hardest points of disagreement. Ortlund argues that Protestantism is a better approximation of truth because its principle of **semper reformanda** keeps errors reformable, and that recognizing **genuine spiritual fruit** outside any single institution is a matter of conscience grounded in Jesus's teaching on trees and fruit. Truglia counters that Scripture is materially sufficient but must be read through the **apostolic interpretive tradition** preserved in the church's succession, and that **schism is listed as a damnable sin** in Galatians 5:19–21—the discovery that drove his conversion. The two clash over whether the **Cyprianic hardline** (no salvation outside the institutional church) applies to post-1054 divisions, whether early evidence on **icons** supports veneration or aniconic worship, and whether Augustine's letters open space within patristic thought for recognizing Christians outside canonical boundaries. Both close with an appeal for **charitable disagreement** and against the hateful online rhetoric that damages all sides.

# Core Question

Is institutional **schism** a soul-destroying sin that forces every Christian to identify and remain within the one true church, or can genuine divisions among trinitarian Christians coexist with the Holy Spirit's salvific work on both sides of the boundary?

# Key Concepts

- **Semper reformanda** — Protestantism's structural claim that councils and traditions are fallible and therefore correctable, contrasted with Orthodoxy's irreformable dogmas.
- **Schism as damnable sin** — Truglia's scriptural anchor in Galatians 5:19–21 (*dichostasiai*, *haireseis*) and the unanimous patristic reading from Ignatius onward.
- **Material sufficiency + apostolic tradition** — Scripture contains all saving doctrine, but its meaning is fixed by the interpretive tradition received from the apostles and preserved through succession.
- **Spiritual fruit as ecclesiological evidence** — Ortlund's argument from Matthew 12 and Galatians 5 that the Holy Spirit's demonstrable work outside Orthodoxy cannot be denied without self-deception.
- **Cyprianic hardline in context** — the dispute over whether patristic statements against Gnostic-level heresies in a tiny church can be extended to two billion post-1054 Christians.
- **Icons in the first three centuries** — competing readings of the same sources: explicit archaeological and textual evidence of veneration (Truglia) vs. patristic language distinguishing Christian from pagan worship (Ortlund).

# Why It Matters

This dialogue models how two informed, committed Christians can name their deepest disagreements—including the claim that the other is **damned**—without descending into caricature. It surfaces the questions every Christian exploring Orthodoxy or Protestantism must eventually face: whether the church's unity is **institutional or spiritual**, whether tradition is an **interpretive lens** or a **fallible guide**, and whether the moral gravity of schism outweighs the moral gravity of binding consciences to what one believes is error. The exchange also illustrates the real-world cost of **online hostility** between traditions and makes a case that charitable dialogue is itself a form of gospel witness.
