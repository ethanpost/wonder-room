# Protestant-Orthodox Relationship: The Moment That Defined It

## Metadata

- Author: Gavin Ortlund
- Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u95i1nSrWzA

## Detailed Outline

### Background: Early Protestant Affirmation of Eastern Orthodoxy

- The Protestant Reformers (Luther, Calvin) defended the legitimacy of the Eastern Orthodox Church against Rome's claims of exclusivity.
- At the 1519 Leipzig Debate, Johann Eck (Catholic opponent) articulated the position that all Eastern Orthodox Christians are damned.
- Luther responded: "Nothing more detestable than this blasphemy could be spoken."
- Calvin similarly affirmed the Eastern Orthodox Church's legitimacy (referenced in a recurring quote from Calvin's writings, edited by John McNeill).
- Key question Ortlund raises: where did the Protestant–Eastern Orthodox relationship go from there?

### The Tübingen–Constantinople Correspondence (1570s–1580s)

- A group of Lutheran theologians from Tübingen (southern Germany) initiated a theological correspondence with Jeremiah II, the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople.
- The Ecumenical Patriarch holds a kind of representative leadership role among Eastern Orthodox Christians.
- The Lutherans sent Jeremiah a copy of the Augsburg Confession (the Lutheran confession of faith).
- Each side sent three letters to the other (six letters total).
- Ortlund describes this interchange as one of the most important ecumenical moments in all of church history, yet largely unknown.
- The letters are collected in a published book referenced in the video description.

### Why the Protestant–Orthodox Relationship Is Distinctive

- Unlike Protestant–Catholic, Catholic–Orthodox, or Eastern Orthodox–Oriental Orthodox relations, the Protestant–Orthodox relationship lacks centuries of direct contact, shared history, and shared assumptions.
- It is "almost like two people from a different culture talking to each other."
- This distance makes the dialogue especially interesting and illuminating, raising unique questions for both sides.
- Ortlund notes personal intellectual growth from engaging Eastern Christianity, particularly the essence-energies distinction in the Palamite tradition.

### Observation 1: The Lutherans Regarded Jeremiah as a Christian and the Orthodox Church as a Church

- This recognition was not assumed in the 16th century; the dominant Roman Catholic view denied ecclesial status to those outside Rome.
- The Lutherans addressed Jeremiah with diplomatic courtesy and elaborate titles ("God-beloved sir," "the most honorable Lord," "the all-holy ecumenical patriarch").
- More importantly, they understood him as a fellow Christian.
- They expressed a genuine desire for unity in the Christian faith.
- Opening of their first letter: "If the merciful Heavenly Father through his beloved Son … would so direct us on both sides … we become close to one another in our agreement on the correct teaching and the cities of Constantinople and Tübingen become bound to each other by the bond of the same Christian faith and love, there is no event that we should desire more."
- They expressed openness to God working "on both sides" — not a one-sided demand.

### Observation 2: Areas of Agreement and Disagreement

#### Agreements

- The nature of Scripture as the inspired Word of God.
- The doctrine of God and the Trinity.
- The doctrine of Christ (two natures).
- The nature of evil.
- Christ's saving office, death, and Resurrection.
- Christ's future Second Coming.
- Communion in both kinds (bread and wine), against the Roman Catholic practice of withholding the cup.
- Opposition to papal overreach.
- Rejection of obligatory priestly celibacy.
- Rejection of medieval Western soteriological innovations: indulgences, purgatory, the treasury of merit, the transfer of excess merit.
- Jeremiah himself acknowledged at the end of his second letter: "We have agreed on almost all of the main subjects."

#### Disagreements

- The Filioque clause (whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or from the Father and the Son).
- Free will — an important divergence.
- The nature of justification.
- The number and theology of the sacraments.
- The mode of baptism (e.g., pouring vs. immersion).
- Praying to the saints, icons, and relics.
- The nature and infallibility of the Church — perhaps the biggest disagreement.
  - Jeremiah's position (paraphrased): "Those in the Church are holy of the truth; those who are not holy of the truth are not in the Church."
  - This reflects a more absolutist ecclesiology: the Church cannot err.

#### Ortlund's Framing

- The "core" or "tree trunk" is shared: the same God (Trinity), the same Mediator (Jesus Christ), the same basic outline of redemptive history (creation ex nihilo, fall, Incarnation, Second Coming).
- The "branches" or "periphery" contain the disagreements.
- Compared to other interfaith dialogues, the Protestant–Orthodox relationship has far more common ground (e.g., a Christian and a Hindu lack a shared linear vision of history; a Christian and a Buddhist lack a shared understanding of the human condition; a Christian and a Mormon lack a shared Trinitarian theology).
- The disagreements are still important: they concern worship, ecclesiology, and soteriology, and some carry anathemas.
- Both traditions appear to be "in the ballpark of what we call Christianity."

### Observation 3: Two Differing Visions of Catholicity

#### Jeremiah's Approach

- **First letter**: Warm but condescending. He called the Lutherans "spiritual sons of my humble self" and addressed them "with the cheerfulness of a father to his children." Patronizing tone; lacked genuine humility.
- **Second letter**: Enjoined the Lutherans to "put aside their irrational innovations" and enter the Eastern Orthodox Church. His position: unity comes only through institutional incorporation into Orthodoxy. He concluded: "Nothing else is the cause of dissension than this, and only this, which when you correct it, we will be … in agreement."
- **Third letter**: When the Lutherans did not yield, Jeremiah adopted a rebuking posture. He sarcastically exclaimed, "You call yourselves theologians!" He then ended the correspondence: "We request that from henceforth you do not cause us more grief nor write to us on the same subject … going about your own ways … no longer concerning dogmas, but if you do write, only for friendship's sake. Farewell."
- Ortlund's colloquial summary of Jeremiah's three letters: (1) "I'm happy to help," (2) "You guys have issues," (3) "Stop talking to me."
- Ortlund characterizes this as a model of how not to do ecumenism: demand immediate acquiescence, then cut off dialogue when it doesn't happen.

#### The Lutheran Approach

- They did not shy away from defending their positions: "We cannot bear to be called heretics; indeed we embrace all the articles of the Catholic faith, rejecting absolutely none of them, and we respectfully make use of the sacraments which were instituted by Christ."
- They repeatedly argued: "Our doctrine is not new; it is founded upon the Word of God."
- After Jeremiah cut off dialogue, they acknowledged his request but expressed hope it would be reconsidered in due time.
- Their closing: "We offer to the God of all our true friendship … we wish your Holiness with all our heart all that is best and a prayerful wish for salvation, and above all this, that the Holy Spirit will lead all the activities of your Holiness, the honor of God and the salvation of his Church. Amen."
- Ortlund notes this was a remarkably respectful sign-off for the historical context.

#### The Two Visions Contrasted

- **Jeremiah's vision**: Unity = institutional incorporation into the Orthodox Church. Catholicity is bounded by institutional membership.
- **The Lutheran vision**: Unity = adherence to the gospel of Christ as held forth in Scripture. Key quote: "We desire from the bottom of our hearts to preserve a God-pleasing peace with all who love the gospel of Christ, who hold the right interpretation of Christ, the unique teacher who speaks to us through the words of the Old as well as the New Testaments."
- Ortlund argues the Lutheran model is more compelling because it (a) lacks the institutional exclusivism characteristic of every major non-Protestant church, which is difficult to maintain in the 16th century and especially in the 21st, and (b) lacks the arrogance of assuming one's own views are superior right out of the gate.

### Irenicism: A Posture, Not a Compromise

- Ortlund defines irenicism as "aiming for peace."
- Common misunderstandings: that irenicism signals weakness, compromise, or something optional that can be set aside when times are tough.
- Ortlund's corrective: Irenicism is not compromise; it requires all the strength one has; it is needed precisely in tough times; it reflects God's heart for the Church.
- Many Protestants lack the spirit of catholicity reflected by the Tübingen Lutherans, veering instead into either "feisty sectarianism" or "squishy ecumenism."
- The ability to "wrangle for the truth but with love and respect and a desire for peace" is often missing from all traditions.

### Concluding Appeal to Protestant Viewers

- Affirming true Christians and true churches outside Protestantism does not compromise any historic Protestant standard (except possibly some fringe groups).
- The Lutherans did not compromise a single official Lutheran article of doctrine when they treated Jeremiah as a Christian within the Christian Church to whom Christian obligations and love are due.
- Protestantism has not claimed to be the one true Church — this is a strength to reclaim.
- Ortlund aspires to model this on his YouTube channel: conviction and openness together.
- Final quote repeated as a model: "We desire from the bottom of our hearts to preserve a God-pleasing peace with all who love the gospel of Christ."

## Core Ideas

- The 1570s–1580s correspondence between the Tübingen Lutherans and Patriarch Jeremiah II is one of the most important and underappreciated ecumenical events in church history.
- The two sides shared substantial theological common ground but differed on significant issues including ecclesiology, justification, the Filioque, and the sacraments.
- The exchange reveals two fundamentally different visions of catholicity: institutional incorporation (Jeremiah) vs. gospel-centered unity (the Lutherans).
- Irenicism — seeking peace without compromising conviction — is a Protestant heritage worth reclaiming and is not a sign of weakness.
- Treating fellow Christians in other traditions as genuine Christians does not violate any mainstream Protestant confession.

## Key Terms

- **Catholicity**: The wholeness or universality of the Church; the question of who counts as part of the true Church.
- **Irenicism**: A posture of aiming for peace in theological dialogue, distinct from compromise or doctrinal indifference.
- **Augsburg Confession**: The primary Lutheran confession of faith, presented to Emperor Charles V in 1530; the document the Tübingen Lutherans sent to Jeremiah II.
- **Filioque**: The clause added to the Nicene Creed in the Western Church stating the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father "and the Son"; a major point of contention between East and West.
- **Ecumenical Patriarch**: The title of the Archbishop of Constantinople, holding a representative leadership role among Eastern Orthodox Christians.
- **Essence-Energies Distinction**: A theological framework in Eastern Orthodoxy (especially the Palamite tradition) distinguishing between God's unknowable essence and his knowable energies or activities.
- **Treasury of Merit**: A medieval Western concept that the surplus merit of saints can be applied to other believers, associated with the practice of indulgences.
- **Ecclesiology**: The theological study of the nature, structure, and mission of the Church.
