# Summary

In the 1570s–1580s, Lutheran theologians from Tübingen sent the Augsburg Confession to Ecumenical Patriarch Jeremiah II of Constantinople and engaged him in a six-letter theological exchange. Despite finding substantial common ground on core doctrines — the Trinity, Christology, Scripture, and shared concerns about medieval Roman Catholic innovations — they could not resolve disagreements on the Filioque, justification, ecclesiology, and the sacraments. The correspondence ended when Jeremiah cut off dialogue after the Lutherans refused to convert. What emerged were two starkly different visions of catholicity: Jeremiah's demand for institutional incorporation into the Orthodox Church versus the Lutherans' commitment to gospel-centered unity with all who love Christ. Gavin Ortlund argues the Lutheran model — irenicism that combines conviction with openness — is the better heritage for Protestants to reclaim.

# Core Question

When two Christian traditions share a deep theological core but differ on important matters of worship, ecclesiology, and soteriology, what does faithful engagement look like — demanding institutional unity or pursuing peace with all who hold the gospel?

# Key Concepts

- **Two visions of catholicity**: Jeremiah required the Lutherans to become Orthodox for unity; the Lutherans defined unity around shared love for the gospel of Christ as held forth in Scripture.
- **Irenicism**: Aiming for peace without doctrinal compromise — not weakness but a demanding posture that requires spiritual strength and reflects God's heart for the Church.
- **The tree-trunk-and-branches metaphor**: Protestants and Orthodox share the trunk (God, Trinity, Christ, redemptive history) while diverging on branches (Filioque, sacraments, ecclesiology, justification).
- **The ecumenical failure pattern**: Jeremiah's trajectory — warmth, then demand for acquiescence, then termination of dialogue — as a model of how not to do ecumenism.
- **Protestant heritage of openness**: No mainstream Protestant confession claims Protestantism is the one true Church; recognizing true Christians elsewhere does not compromise Protestant doctrine.

# Why It Matters

This largely forgotten exchange exposes a tension still alive today: whether Christian unity requires institutional conformity or can rest on a shared gospel core. Ortlund's analysis challenges Protestants who veer toward sectarianism or indifference alike, offering a concrete historical model for holding conviction and charity together — a posture needed in an era of sharp theological tribalism across every tradition.
