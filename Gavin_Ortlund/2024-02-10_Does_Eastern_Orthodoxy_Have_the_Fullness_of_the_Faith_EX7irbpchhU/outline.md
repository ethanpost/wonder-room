# Does Eastern Orthodoxy Have the "Fullness of the Faith?"

## Metadata

- Author: Gavin Ortlund
- Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EX7irbpchhU
- Date: 2024-02-10

## Detailed Outline

### Introduction: The appeal of the "fullness of the faith"

- The phrase "fullness of the faith" is commonly invoked by Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic Christians as a reason to join their communion: "We're not denying God is at work elsewhere, but we possess the fullness."
- Ortlund highlights Jonathan Pageau's appearance on Roshan's channel, where Pageau described discovering in Orthodoxy "the fullness of the faith."
- Pageau's framing is ecumenical—he does not claim non-Orthodox people are not Christian or are damned—but he describes Orthodoxy as sitting atop a hierarchy of participation, with other traditions lower on the spectrum.
- Ortlund appreciates Pageau's ecumenical posture but argues that it is **not the historic Eastern Orthodox view**. What Pageau describes is actually a more Protestant way of thinking imported into modern Orthodoxy.

### Two models for construing the "one true church" claim

- Ortlund introduces two labels for the ways the "one true church" claim has been understood:
  1. **Hierarchical view** — There is one true church, but the boundaries are porous; other traditions participate to varying degrees. "We know where the church is, but we don't know where it isn't."
  2. **Noah's Ark view** — There is one true church and the boundary is absolute. If you are not on the ark, you are in the water and will drown. Whether you are five yards or five miles from the ark makes no difference.
- Timothy Ware, author of a widely read book on Orthodoxy, is cited as an example of a modern Orthodox thinker who adopts the more inclusive hierarchical construal.
- All major non-Protestant traditions—Roman Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, Oriental Orthodoxy, the Assyrian Church of the East—make a "one true church" claim, but they differ on what that means for outsiders' sacraments and salvation.

### Ortlund's thesis: The Noah's Ark view is the historic Orthodox position

- Ortlund argues that the **hierarchical/fullness-of-the-faith view is a modern innovation**; the historic Orthodox position from the 9th through the 19th century is the Noah's Ark view.
- The implication: if you are not explicitly, knowingly Eastern Orthodox, you are damned.
- To the extent that modern Orthodox Christians adopt the hierarchical view, they should scale back their claim of being "the unchanging church," because this represents a genuine change.

### Scope of the historical survey

- Ortlund focuses on the period from the **9th to the 19th centuries**—roughly from the lead-up to the Great Schism through the pre-modern era.
- He acknowledges the **church fathers** (pre-9th century) are more complicated: Justin Martyr spoke of pagans who lived justly as members of the body of Christ; Augustine has ambiguous passages about whether Donatists could be saved; Cyprian's "no salvation outside the church" was applied to Gnostics and Marcionites in a context of roughly one million total Christians.
- The doctrine of "no salvation outside the church" grew stricter as the church became entwined with the Roman Empire in the 4th century.
- Ortlund leaves room for further study of the 5th-century christological schisms (Chalcedonians vs. non-Chalcedonians), which he has not examined in sufficient depth.

### The theological framework: Filioque as damnable heresy

- The core logic connecting doctrine to damnation runs as follows:
  1. Western innovations like the **filioque** (the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father **and the Son**) are heresy.
  2. Heresy places you outside the canonical boundaries of the Orthodox Church.
  3. Being outside the church cuts you off from the grace of the Holy Spirit given in the sacraments.
  4. Being cut off from sacramental grace means being cut off from salvation.
- The recurring metaphor is the Ark of Noah: inside the ark = salvation; outside the ark = destruction, regardless of proximity.
- Ortlund claims he has found **zero affirmations** from any major Eastern Orthodox theologian, council, or document between the 9th and 19th centuries stating that non-Orthodox Christians (Roman Catholics or Protestants) can be saved. He has asked repeatedly and received only assertions that "that's not the Orthodox view" without historical documentation.

### Paisius Velichkovsky (18th century)

- Paisius Velichkovsky, an 18th-century Orthodox monk and theologian, wrote to a priest warning him about the filioque.
- Velichkovsky called the filioque **"the first and most important of all the heresies"** and argued that rejecting it is the unanimous position of the church.
- Key quote: "All the holy ecumenical teachers… say that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father and nowhere have they written that He proceeds from the Son also. Thus if uniates think exactly like the Romans in such a serious heresy, **what hope do they have for salvation** unless they openly renounce this Spirit-fighting heresy and become united again with the Holy Orthodox Eastern Church."
- He further urged: "Depart and flee from the unia as speedily as possible, **lest death overtake you in it** and you be numbered among the heretics and not among the Christians."
- **Uniates** (Eastern churches in communion with Rome while retaining their own liturgy) are explicitly included among those without hope of salvation.
- Ortlund acknowledges Velichkovsky is one theologian, but argues this perspective is representative of the unanimous pre-19th-century Orthodox position.

### The 1672 Synod of Jerusalem (Confession of Dositheus)

- Background: Cyril Lucaris, Patriarch of Constantinople, had expressed views that sounded Protestant or Calvinist. In response, the 1672 Synod of Jerusalem produced the **Confession of Dositheus** (Patriarch of Jerusalem), an officially approved statement of Eastern Orthodox belief.
- The Confession repeatedly categorizes Protestants as **"wicked heretics."**
- On the necessity of bishops: "The dignity of the bishop is so necessary in the church that without him neither church nor Christian could either be or be spoken of. He is… as necessary to the church as breath is to man or the sun to the world."
- The consequence drawn from this: "When these forsake the church, they are forsaken by the Holy Spirit, and there remains in them **neither understanding nor light but only darkness and blindness.**"
- This is a Noah's Ark view: outside the church (defined by communion with the bishop, whose office is distinct from that of priest), the Holy Spirit has forsaken you entirely. There is no spectrum of participation.

### The Patriarchal Encyclical of 1895

- Issued in response to Pope Leo XIII's invitation to unity with Rome.
- Maintains that Roman Catholicism is heretical, self-consciously operating within a tradition of thought stretching back centuries.
- Key quote: "The Western church from the 10th century downwards has privily brought into herself through the papacy various and strange and heretical doctrines and innovations, and so she has been torn away and removed far from the true and Orthodox Church of Christ. **How necessary then it is for you to come back and return to the ancient and unadulterated doctrines of the church in order to attain the salvation in Christ** after which you press."
- The final words make the soteriological stakes explicit: those outside the Orthodox Church are seeking salvation but cannot attain it without returning.

### The 1848 Encyclical of the Eastern Patriarchs

- A letter issued by the **four Eastern Patriarchs** in response to Pope Pius IX's epistle to the Eastern churches.
- Identifies the filioque as **damnable heresy** on the level of Arianism.
- Repeatedly refers to the Roman Catholic Church as **apostate and heretical**.
- Key quote: "The one Holy Catholic and Apostolic Church… proclaimed of old… that the said novel doctrine of the Holy Ghost proceeding from the Father and the Son is **essentially heresy**, and its maintainers, whoever they be, are heretics… and that the congregations of such are also heretical, and that **all spiritual communion and worship of the Orthodox sons of the Catholic church with such is unlawful.**"
- Note: "Catholic" here refers to the Eastern Orthodox Church, which has historically also called itself the Catholic Church.

### Other examples of Orthodox exclusivism

- **Mark of Ephesus** (15th century): held the same exclusivist position.
- **Sigillion of 1583**: makes similar claims, though its textual history is disputed, so Ortlund does not rely on it heavily.
- **Longer Catechism of St. Philaret** (19th-century Metropolitan of Moscow): uses the same Noah's Ark imagery — "To have part in Christ's salvation, we must necessarily be members of His body, that is, of the Catholic Church."
- Ortlund anticipates the counterargument: some will try to reinterpret these passages through a modern framework where one can be a member of the church "invisibly" or "implicitly."
- He rebuts this by pointing to the explicit language of the sources: they demand that people **openly renounce** the filioque and **run** from heretical communions. There is no concept of unknowing or invisible membership in these texts.
- Ortlund challenges anyone who disagrees to provide **historical documentation** of an alternative view from this period; he believes the exclusivist position is unanimous for a millennium.

### Medieval Roman Catholic views (parallel exclusivism)

- Roman Catholics held the mirror-image position toward the East: those outside communion with the Bishop of Rome are off the Ark of Noah, drowning in the flood waters of sin.
- Only a few Jesuit theologians in the early modern era articulated a different view, and it was controversial and suppressed.
- The modern Roman Catholic position (especially after Vatican II) is much more inclusive, but the medieval view was exclusivist.
- Key documents Ortlund has treated in other videos:
  - **Unam Sanctam**: "It is altogether necessary for salvation that every human creature be subject to the Roman Pontiff."
  - **Cantate Domino** (Council of Florence, 15th century): strong, explicit affirmation of no salvation outside the Roman Church.

### Response to Dave Armstrong's counterargument

- Dave Armstrong argued that since valid baptism occurs outside the Roman Catholic Church and baptism confers salvation, salvation must occur outside the church.
- Ortlund responds by quoting the continuation of the Cantate Domino: "The unity of the ecclesiastical body is of such importance that **only for those who abide in it** do the church's sacraments contribute to salvation."
- A valid baptism for someone entering Rome does not mean that baptism was **salvific** for someone remaining outside. The text explicitly limits the saving efficacy of sacraments to those within the body.
- If baptism automatically conferred salvation and membership in the body of Christ, the warning would be meaningless.

### Modern reinterpretations are anachronistic

- People introduce qualifications such as: you can submit to the pope "implicitly"; you can be in the church "unknowingly" or "invisibly."
- Ortlund argues these are **modern innovations with zero historical precedent** for 600 years. No medieval pope would have recognized the concept of unknowing or implicit membership in the church, any more than one could be "unknowingly on the Ark of Noah."
- **Francis Sullivan** (Catholic scholar): "The teaching of St. Thomas and of the whole medieval tradition required **explicit Christian faith** for the salvation of everyone in the Christian era. After the suppression of the Jesuit order, hardly any Catholic theologians dared to question the traditional teaching on this point."
- This raises a practical concern about the value of an allegedly **infallible magisterium**: if everyone can misinterpret the magisterium itself for 600 years, its practical utility is greatly reduced. How do we know the current interpretation will not also be overturned in another 600 years?

### The Protestant alternative

- Protestants affirm the doctrine of "no salvation outside the church" but differ in a crucial way: they **deny institutional exclusivism**.
- The Protestant position: the one true church **coheres within multiple institutions**. The church is institutionally fragmented, which is tragic, but the fragmentation is real and must be acknowledged.
- Ortlund documents this with historical consistency:
  - **Luther and Calvin** recognized true churches outside Protestantism and criticized Catholics for damning the Orthodox.
  - The **Lutherans in the 1570s** wrote to Jeremiah II, Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople, addressing him as a fellow Christian.
- Protestants historically have recognized that the true church is not restricted to one institution.
- On the filioque specifically, Ortlund praises Jonathan Pageau's position: the filioque is not a slam-dunk issue on either side. The idea that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Son in the context of creation (as opposed to the eternal procession) can be found in Eastern Fathers as well.

### The visible/invisible church distinction

- A common misunderstanding: Protestants are accused of denying the visible church.
- Drawing from **Augustine** and **John Wycliffe**, Protestants have historically affirmed both a visible and an invisible aspect of the church. The distinction is modest:
  - **Visible church**: all baptized Christians in principle—you can count them.
  - **Invisible church**: those truly united to Christ—only God knows who they are.
- Example: **Judas Iscariot** was a member of the visible church but not of the invisible church.
- The adjectives are not essential; the underlying distinction is. The point is not that the church is invisible but that it is **not restricted to one institutional hierarchy**.

### Assurance of salvation

- Ortlund connects the topic to his core pastoral concern: **assurance of salvation**.
- The exclusivist claims of Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism impose **ecclesial anxiety** on people and sometimes **intimidate** them into conversion through fear of damnation.
- Ortlund pastors his audience with Jesus's promise: "Whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me **has eternal life**" (John 5:24).
- On Judgment Day, a believer can appeal directly to Christ's promise: "I repented of my sins, I surrendered my life to you, I took up my cross and followed you—now I appeal to your promise." Christ is not a liar.
- Salvation is secure on the basis of the promise of Jesus Christ, not on picking the correct institution.

## Core Ideas

- **Noah's Ark vs. hierarchical construal**: Two models for understanding the "one true church" claim—the historic Orthodox position (Noah's Ark: absolute boundary, no salvation outside) versus the modern softened position (hierarchical: spectrum of participation, porous boundaries).
- **The filioque as the hinge of damnation**: From the 9th to 19th century, affirming the filioque was treated as heresy that placed one outside the church and therefore outside salvation.
- **Modern inclusivism as innovation**: The more ecumenical, hierarchical reading of the "one true church" claim—whether Orthodox or Catholic—is a modern development with no pre-19th-century precedent, undermining claims of doctrinal unchangingness.
- **Institutional exclusivism vs. doctrinal boundaries**: Protestantism's distinctive contribution is rejecting institutional exclusivism while retaining doctrinal boundaries—the true church coheres within multiple institutions.
- **The visible/invisible church distinction**: Not a denial of the visible church but a recognition that only God knows who is truly united to Christ, and this reality crosses institutional lines.
- **Assurance of salvation**: Exclusivist ecclesiology produces pastoral harm by making salvation contingent on institutional membership rather than on Christ's promise.

## Key Terms

- **Fullness of the faith** (claim that one tradition possesses the complete deposit of Christian truth, with others having only partial truth)
- **Hierarchical construal** (model where the "one true church" is at the center of a spectrum, with other traditions participating to lesser degrees)
- **Noah's Ark view** (model where the "one true church" has absolute boundaries — inside is salvation, outside is damnation)
- **Filioque** (Western addition to the Nicene Creed: the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father "and the Son")
- **Institutional exclusivism** (the claim that the one true church is restricted to one institutional hierarchy)
- **Uniates / Uniate churches** (Eastern churches in communion with Rome that retain their own liturgy)
- **Confession of Dositheus** (officially approved Eastern Orthodox doctrinal statement from the 1672 Synod of Jerusalem)
- **Cantate Domino** (decree from the Council of Florence, 15th century, affirming no salvation outside the Roman Church)
- **Unam Sanctam** (papal bull declaring submission to the Roman Pontiff necessary for salvation)
- **Visible church / invisible church** (Protestant distinction: all baptized Christians vs. those truly united to Christ, known only to God)
- **Semper reformanda** (always reforming — the Protestant principle that all traditions and councils are fallible)
- **Infallible magisterium** (the claimed authority of the Roman Catholic teaching office to define doctrine without error)

### Reciting

- The two models: **hierarchical** (modern, inclusive, spectrum) vs. **Noah's Ark** (historic, exclusive, absolute boundary).
- The filioque-to-damnation chain: heresy → outside the church → no sacramental grace → no salvation.
- The Protestant distinctive: not denying the visible church or that schism can happen, but denying that the true church is restricted to one institutional hierarchy.
- The visible/invisible church distinction: visible = baptized Christians you can count; invisible = those truly united to Christ, known only to God. Judas was in the first but not the second.
