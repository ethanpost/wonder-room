## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and context

- This is a dialogue hosted on "The Transfigured Life" podcast between Dr. Gavin Ortlund (Protestant, Reformed Baptist, senior pastor of First Baptist Church of Ojai, CA; YouTube channel "Truth Unites") and Fr. Stephen De Young (Eastern Orthodox priest at Archangel Gabriel Orthodox Church; PhD in biblical studies from Amridge University; author and podcaster).
- The hosts, Father Jonathan and Luther, frame the conversation as a discussion rather than a debate, posing questions to both guests before opening the floor for direct exchange.
- The dialogue runs approximately 90 minutes and covers Sola Scriptura, Holy Tradition, the relationship between scripture and tradition, icon veneration, and how to adjudicate between ecumenical councils.

### What is Sola Scriptura? (Ortlund)

- Ortlund identifies two common caricatures of Sola Scriptura:
  1. **"Everything you believe must be explicit in scripture"** — Even the strongest articulations of the sufficiency of scripture allow doctrines to be inferred or deduced from scripture. Many formulations address only what is necessary for salvation. No mainstream historic Protestant view requires a chapter-and-verse proof text for every belief.
  2. **"The Bible is the only authority"** — Sola Scriptura is not a rejection of church history, councils, offices, or tradition in general. All of these are tremendously valuable. The claim is simply that they are **fallible**.
- The positive definition: Scripture is the **only infallible rule** for the church in the post-apostolic era. After the apostles die, the period of public divine revelation is over; no ongoing mechanisms of infallibility persist in the church.
- The church's pronouncements are fallible — they can err. This does not mean they always will, but there is no preservation from error.
- The conviction is grounded in a view of revelation: Paul says in Galatians 1:8 that even an angel preaching a contrary gospel should be accursed. The goal is to remain tethered to the apostolic deposit.
- Scripture is **ontologically unique**: it is "God-breathed" (2 Timothy 3:16), "carried by the Holy Spirit" (2 Peter 1:21), quoted by Jesus as God speaking, and called "the oracles of God" (Romans 3:2). Scripture is the speech of God and is therefore paramount.
- Francis Turretin (Reformed theologian) clarified that the issue is not whether the church has any judgment in controversies of faith, but what serves as the supreme standard by which everything else is adjudicated.

### What is Holy Tradition? (De Young)

- De Young distinguishes the Orthodox understanding of tradition from the Roman Catholic understanding:
  - **Roman Catholic view**: Propositional content of revelation not recorded in scripture, handed down orally through bishops — a kind of secret oral deposit.
  - **Orthodox view**: Holy Tradition is **the life of the Holy Spirit in the church**. The Holy Spirit has been active within the assembly of God's people from the beginning of humanity through today.
- The Holy Spirit's actions, being the actions of God, are infallible, perfect, and holy.
- Scripture has a **pride of place** within Holy Tradition. De Young agrees with Ortlund's positive statements about scripture; the Orthodox do not have a lower view of scripture but a higher view of some other things.
- The Holy Spirit not only inspired the scriptures but has governed their transmission, copying, and preservation, and guides their interpretation and application within the church.
- This guidance is **collective, not individual**: Christ's promise that "the Holy Spirit will lead you into all truth" uses the plural "you." It is not a promise to any individual reader or even to the Bishop of Rome, but to the church as a whole.
- Ecumenical councils carry greater authority than local councils because they represent a greater gathering of the whole body. The more of the church that is gathered, the more the results reflect the guidance of the Holy Spirit.
- The process takes time: the formation of the New Testament canon occurred over centuries, guided by the same Spirit who inspired the texts. Similarly, councils may not be clearly recognized as authoritative immediately, but over time God reveals which councils were guided by the Spirit — Nicaea on the Trinity, Chalcedon on Christology — in a process analogous to the recognition of the canon.
- Scripture retains pride of place in liturgical life: only scripture is read in worship, and the hymns and structure of worship are saturated with scriptural quotation and allusion. The rest of Holy Tradition can be understood as a way of reading and applying scripture as guided by the Holy Spirit.

### Is Sola Scriptura of divine inspiration or practical necessity? (Ortlund)

- Ortlund says Sola Scriptura is not itself divinely inspired; it is **the effort to be faithful to that which is divinely inspired**, and to that extent it is a practical necessity.
- The core concern: we need a guard against errors that can creep into the church. The church can be alive even while there are errors — errors, corruptions, deviations, and accretions (the slow, gradual growth of innovations).
- **The Pharisees as a parallel**: The Pharisees had legitimate, God-given authority (Jesus said they sit on Moses' seat in Matthew 23), yet they yoked people to error by appealing to oral tradition. They claimed an oral law from Moses roughly equal in authority to the written law. Jesus opposed them in Mark 7 not merely for having the wrong traditions, but for their **inflated view of tradition as such**, saying "many such things you do." The consequence: the word of God is nullified.
- Ortlund says many street-level arguments against Sola Scriptura could have been made by the Pharisees against Jesus.
- On specific areas of Protestant–Orthodox disagreement (intercession of saints, icon veneration), Ortlund is "firmly persuaded" these are **later innovations and accretions** that can be seen creeping in slowly after the apostolic age.
- The concern with the church claiming infallibility: if the church claims authority tantamount to scripture itself, there is no mechanism for detecting errors.
- The telephone game analogy: oral communication is extremely easy to garble over time. As early as the second century, conflicting claims of apostolic tradition appear.

### The Orthodox critique of Sola Scriptura (De Young)

- **Authority shifts from the Holy Spirit to the text**: De Young argues that Sola Scriptura moves the locus of authority from the Holy Spirit himself to the written text. He appeals to Paul's contrast between "the letter" and "the spirit" as reflecting a difference between how a Christian and a Pharisee keep the commandments.
- **The problem of adjudicating interpretations**: If everything is revisable, who decides between competing interpretations of scripture? De Young offers the example of a self-identified "Biblical Unitarian" Protestant who wants to revise Nicaea I based on his reading of the Bible. From outside Protestant internal discussions, the concrete difference between what this Unitarian does with Nicaea I and what Ortlund does with Nicaea II is unclear.
- **Practical dangers**: Within American and European Protestantism, the principle of Semper Reformanda has been taken to extremes — ordaining women, changing moral teachings — all framed as scriptural arguments. Many converts to Orthodoxy find the claim that nothing is revisable very attractive.
- **The Roman Catholic contrast**: Even in the Roman Catholic Church, things now seem revisable, but worse — the magisterium can simply change its mind without even making a scriptural argument.
- **Sola Scriptura's historical context**: De Young argues Sola Scriptura arose in the 16th century because it served a practical purpose. The Reformers faced falsified documents and misattributed patristic works supporting papal claims. The Hebrew Old Testament from local synagogues and the Greek New Testament published by Erasmus were texts Rome had not tampered with. Basing everything on scripture made eminent practical sense in that milieu.
- **Changed circumstances**: We are no longer in that situation. We now have archaeologically recovered first-century synagogues in Galilee — some where Christ preached — that contain iconography. Calvin's claim that there were no icons before the fifth century is now known to be false. We have the Dead Sea Scrolls, we can identify which patristic texts are genuine, and we have a much better picture of apostolic Christianity and Second Temple Judaism.

### Ortlund's response to the critique

- **Proliferation of division** is a genuine weakness of Protestantism, but it is different from the claim of infallibility: Protestants do not claim infallibility for their interpretations, so differing interpretations are not a falsification of the Protestant system.
- **Icons vs. religious art**: The issue is not the presence of religious art in buildings, catacombs, or engravings. The issue is the **veneration** of images — the specific theology required by Nicaea II: praying through the icon as a window to heaven, figural representation where what is given to the image passes through to the prototype. The practice involving kissing, lighting candles, deep kneeling, and bowing is the target. Anathemas are attached to the rejection of this specific theology.

### The role of oral tradition in the New Testament

- Father Jonathan raises 2 Thessalonians 2:15 ("hold fast to the traditions which I have taught you, whether by word or by letter") and Paul's instruction to Timothy to teach what he received to others who can teach still others (2 Timothy 2:2), establishing first through fourth generations of transmission.
- Ortlund responds: The word "tradition" is ambiguous. During the apostolic era, oral traditions existed alongside the writing of scripture — that is not what Sola Scriptura opposes. The claim is about the **post-apostolic era**: once the apostles die and no more scripture is being written, we only access apostolic traditions through a fallible transmission process.
- When early Christians like Irenaeus appeal to tradition, they are often referring to something coincident with the content of scripture, not a separate norm. Martin Chemnitz identified eight uses of "tradition" in the church fathers; only the eighth — tradition as a parallel, co-equal source alongside scripture — is at odds with Sola Scriptura.
- The concern is specifically with traditions that (a) lack a plausible relationship to apostolic teaching and yet (b) are commanded to be received with equal reverence as scripture.
- **Examples of the telephone game in the second century**: In the Easter date controversy, both sides appealed to apostolic tradition for their view. In the third-century dispute about rebaptism of those baptized by heretics, both sides appealed to apostolic tradition. Irenaeus appealed to apostolic tradition for eccentric views (e.g., Jesus dying as a middle-aged man).

### Are there infallible authorities outside scripture?

- **De Young**: The framing needs to shift. The locus of infallible authority is the Holy Spirit — God himself. Wherever the Holy Spirit is working, that authority is carried, and it is preeminently but not exclusively in the scriptures. The check on individual claims of the Spirit's guidance is consultation with the rest of the church. If one concludes that the ecumenical councils are wrong, one must humbly accept that perhaps the Holy Spirit is not guiding that conclusion. Wherever God acts, he acts perfectly and infallibly; the difficulty is in recognizing where he has acted, and that recognition happens through the church.
- **Ortlund**: The Holy Spirit works in many ways, both individually and corporately, that do not yield infallibility. In the Reformed tradition, sermons are called "the word of God" and the Holy Spirit is said to speak through the preacher, but no one claims each word is infallible. The question is: where is the Holy Spirit's work **guaranteed** to be infallible? On Nicaea II specifically, Ortlund invokes the Council of Hieria and the Council of Frankfurt as counter-councils that he believes also reflect the Holy Spirit's work. The difference between Frankfurt and Nicaea II, and between Hieria and Nicaea II, involved a great deal of politics (e.g., Empress Irene).

### Open discussion: Icon veneration and Nicaea II

- **Ortlund's challenge**: How do you know the Holy Spirit is guiding the church such that Nicaea II is right and Frankfurt is wrong? Throughout the Old Testament, idolatry crept in and persisted for long periods. The judgment that Nicaea II is correct appears to be an after-the-fact determination that could easily have gone the other way.
- **De Young's response**: Most of the same arguments apply to Nicaea I — Arianism was ascendant, Constantine's personal chaplain was Arian, and it could easily have gone the other way. The answer from the Orthodox perspective is the subsequent 1,200 years of history in which the findings have been affirmed and reaffirmed, not just by other councils but in practice by the whole church. This reflects Christ's promise to send the Holy Spirit to lead the church (plural) into all truth.
- **De Young on Frankfurt**: Frankfurt's theological argument against Nicaea II was that icons could not function the way Nicaea II claimed because icons were not homoousios with what they represented. The Franks argued that the Eucharist was homoousios with Christ after consecration and was therefore the only true icon — leading to Eucharistic adoration. The bad theology under Frankfurt's decision undermines its authority.
- **Ortlund's response**: He is more familiar with Hieria making the homoousios argument, not Frankfurt. He reads the Frankish theologians as primarily making historical and exegetical arguments — pointing out misattributed patristic quotations and scriptural misinterpretations at Nicaea II.
- **Ortlund on the church being a law unto itself**: If the confidence that Nicaea II is correct rests on its subsequent reception over 1,200 years, this makes the church self-referentially authoritative. Not all Christians who affirm the Nicene Creed accept Nicaea II; significant opposition persisted in Western Europe into the 1100s. In the Old Testament, idolatry persisted for long periods among God's people — longevity alone is a flimsy basis for guaranteeing truth.
- **De Young's response**: If something is a clear violation of scripture, that would settle it (and he agrees about scripture's authority). The core difference is that he does not see icon veneration as a gross violation of scripture, while Ortlund does. If something is consonant with scripture, enters the church, and persists, that can be read as the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

### The trinitarian theology behind icon veneration (De Young)

- The first document using the argument "what is given to the image passes to the prototype" is from St. Basil the Great, who used it to explain Christian monotheism against the charge that Trinitarian worship amounts to worshipping three Gods. The worship offered to the Son (who is the express image of the Father) passes through to the Father — one God is worshipped.
- This theological principle extends to Christian ethics: man is made in the image of God, so whatever respect or disrespect is shown to a fellow human is ultimately directed toward God (cf. the Parable of the Sheep and the Goats: "whatever you did to the least of these, you did to me").
- The bridge to iconography: the icon functions like a photograph of a loved one — kissing a photo of one's wife is not infidelity but an expression of love mediated through the image.
- Eusebius of Caesarea's opposition to iconography is consistent with his semi-Arian or Arian theology: rejecting the trinitarian principle of image-and-prototype naturally leads to rejecting its applications in ethics and iconography.
- De Young notes that many early Christians Ortlund cites as opposing iconography "don't have 'Saint' in front of their names" — they were dissident in other areas, making their dissidence on iconography potentially connected.

### The argument from silence and historical evidence

- **De Young**: If icon veneration was a common practice, we would expect written sources primarily from those who disagreed with it. Common practices do not generate written defenses until challenged — the same dynamic occurred with the doctrine of the Trinity, which was not explicitly articulated until it was challenged by Arianism. The Jerusalem Talmud records rabbis condemning people who lined up to kiss images as idolaters; since pagans did not worship this way, this likely describes Christian practice in the second or third century.
- **Ortlund's response**: He makes a different kind of historical claim than the Unitarian makes about the Trinity. The New Testament itself contains all the building blocks for the Trinity (baptism in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit; explicit affirmations of the Son's deity; monotheism). Icon veneration does not have analogous scriptural building blocks. The evidence unanimously and resoundingly favors the view that any cultic use of images was rejected by Christians for the first 500 years.
- **Ortlund's final concern**: Inconsistency in how historical claims are wielded — the Orthodox make strong historical claims (e.g., Nicaea II's claim that icons are apostolic legislation), but when counter-evidence appears, qualifications multiply (we don't have all the information; it didn't need to be defended; those who opposed it were dissidents). He sees overwhelming evidence that early Christians before 500 AD did not bow before images and pray through them as a window to heaven.
- **De Young's final response**: His Unitarian friend makes substantially the same historical argument about the Trinity: "Show me someone teaching the Nicene doctrine before Nicaea." The parallel holds at the historical level even if the theological argument differs. Just as the biblical building blocks for the Trinity were present before their full articulation, so the building blocks for icon veneration (the trinitarian principle of image-and-prototype, the ethical principle of honoring the image of God in persons) were present before the practice was formally articulated.

## Core Ideas

- **Sola Scriptura as the only infallible rule**: Scripture is ontologically unique (God-breathed, carried by the Holy Spirit), and after the apostolic era no ongoing mechanism of infallibility persists in the church. Church tradition, councils, and offices are valuable but fallible.
- **Holy Tradition as the life of the Holy Spirit in the church**: The Orthodox define tradition not as a secret oral deposit but as the ongoing action of the Holy Spirit within the gathered church — inspiring, transmitting, and guiding the interpretation of scripture, and shaping the church's worship and doctrine over time.
- **The problem of adjudicating interpretations**: Both sides agree that fallibility enters when humans interpret scripture or tradition. They disagree about where the check on fallibility is located — in scripture alone (Protestantism) or in the collective discernment of the church guided by the Holy Spirit (Orthodoxy).
- **Icon veneration as a test case**: The historical and theological status of icon veneration crystallizes the disagreement. Ortlund sees it as a late accretion with no pre-500 AD evidence; De Young sees it as grounded in trinitarian theology and ethical principles present from the beginning, with hints of early practice.
- **Council reception vs. council authority**: De Young argues that a council's authority rests not on the messy historical circumstances of its convening but on how the church receives it over time. Ortlund argues this makes the church a self-referential authority with no external check.
- **The Pharisees as a Sola Scriptura parallel**: Ortlund uses the Pharisees' appeal to oral tradition and Jesus's rebuke in Mark 7 as a precedent for Sola Scriptura — legitimate authorities can err, and their errors must be measured by God's revealed word.

## Key Terms

- **Sola Scriptura** (scripture alone is the only infallible rule of faith and practice in the post-apostolic church)
- **Holy Tradition** (in Orthodox theology: the life of the Holy Spirit in the church, encompassing scripture, councils, liturgy, and the ongoing guidance of the Spirit)
- **Theopneustos** (Greek: "God-breathed"; 2 Timothy 3:16; the origin of scripture's authority in the Spirit of God)
- **Ontological uniqueness** (Ortlund's term for the claim that scripture has a qualitatively different status from all other authorities)
- **Semper Reformanda** (always reforming; the Protestant principle that all church traditions and pronouncements are revisable)
- **Accretion** (Ortlund's term for the slow, gradual introduction of innovations into church practice)
- **Ecumenical council** (a council representing the whole church, carrying greater authority than a local council in Orthodox theology)
- **Robber council** (a council whose decisions were later rejected by the church as not guided by the Holy Spirit)
- **Homoousios** (Greek: "of one substance"; used at Nicaea I for the Son's relationship to the Father; invoked at Frankfurt regarding the Eucharist and icons)
- **Figural representation** (the Nicaea II theology that what is given to the image passes through to the prototype)
- **Aniconism** (opposition to the cultic use of images in worship; Ortlund identifies this as the unanimous early Christian position)
- **Iconoclasm** (the movement to destroy icons, culminating in the 8th–9th century controversy settled by Nicaea II)
