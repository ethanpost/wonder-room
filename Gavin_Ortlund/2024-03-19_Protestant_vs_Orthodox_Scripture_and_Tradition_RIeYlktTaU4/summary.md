# Summary

A 90-minute dialogue between Dr. Gavin Ortlund (Reformed Protestant) and Fr. Stephen De Young (Eastern Orthodox) on Sola Scriptura and Holy Tradition. Ortlund defines Sola Scriptura as the claim that scripture is the only infallible rule in the post-apostolic church — not a rejection of tradition, councils, or church offices, but a recognition of their fallibility. De Young defines Holy Tradition as the life of the Holy Spirit in the church, encompassing the inspiration, transmission, and interpretation of scripture through the collective guidance of the gathered church over time. Both agree on scripture's supreme importance but disagree on whether infallibility extends beyond the text to the church's corporate reception and interpretation. The disagreement crystallizes around icon veneration: Ortlund sees it as a late accretion with no evidence before 500 AD, while De Young traces it to trinitarian theology (Basil's image-to-prototype principle) and early hints of practice recorded in the Jerusalem Talmud.

# Core Question

If both Protestants and Orthodox affirm scripture's supreme authority, where does the check on fallible human interpretation reside — in scripture alone as an external standard (Sola Scriptura), or in the collective discernment of the church guided by the Holy Spirit over time (Holy Tradition)?

# Key Concepts

- **Sola Scriptura as modest claim**: Not "Bible only" but "only infallible rule" — tradition, councils, and offices are valuable but revisable.
- **Holy Tradition as the Spirit's life**: Not a secret oral deposit but the ongoing action of the Holy Spirit in the gathered church, with scripture holding pride of place within it.
- **The Pharisee parallel**: Ortlund argues that Jesus's rebuke of the Pharisees' inflated view of oral tradition in Mark 7 establishes a precedent for measuring all tradition by scripture.
- **Conciliar reception**: De Young argues that a council's authority is determined not by the circumstances of its convening but by how the church receives it over time — a process guided by the Holy Spirit.
- **Icon veneration as test case**: The debate over whether early Christians venerated icons before 500 AD serves as the concrete battleground for the abstract disagreement about scripture and tradition.
- **The Unitarian counter-parallel**: De Young argues that a Unitarian makes the same historical argument against the Trinity that Ortlund makes against icon veneration, highlighting the limits of arguments from historical silence.

# Why It Matters

This dialogue maps the deepest fault line in Christian epistemology: how believers anchor themselves to apostolic truth across two millennia of transmission. The question is not academic — it determines how Christians adjudicate live controversies (women's ordination, moral teachings, liturgical practice) and whether the church possesses a self-correcting mechanism that is itself trustworthy. For anyone navigating the growing Protestant-to-Orthodox conversion movement, this conversation lays bare the strongest arguments on both sides and the real costs of each position: Protestantism's vulnerability to interpretive fragmentation versus Orthodoxy's vulnerability to the charge that the church becomes a law unto itself.
