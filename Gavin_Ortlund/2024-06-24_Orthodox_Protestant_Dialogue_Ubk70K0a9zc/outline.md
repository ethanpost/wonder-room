## Detailed Outline

### Event context and introduction

- In-person dialogue hosted at First Greek Evangelical Church in Athens, near the Parthenon; Pastor Iōtus (host) moderates. Ortlund presents first; **Fr. Demetrios** (introduced as Father Demetrius Batellos in the opening; Eastern Orthodox priest, lecturer in theology at St. Andrew’s Greek Orthodox Theological College, Athens) responds, followed by open Q&A.
- Ortlund frames the evening as honoring Christ in speech, seeking **unity sincerely** where possible and **truth without compromise**—tensions he says require the Holy Spirit’s help.
- He uses a missionary story: people with no category for God as Father; when they grasp it, one says that if God is their Father and the missionary’s Father, they are **brothers**. Ortlund applies this to dialogue across Christian divisions while not minimizing real disagreement.

### Unity, truth, and J. Gresham Machen on Marburg

- Ortlund quotes Machen on the **1529 Marburg Colloquy**: Machen faulted Luther for failing to unite with the Reformed on the Lord’s Supper but praised Luther’s refusal to treat the dispute as a trifle. **Indifferentism**—treating the Lord’s Table as unimportant—would have been worse than division.
- Takeaway Ortlund draws: sometimes it is better to be wrong than indifferent; truth matters to the Lord and for Christian living.

### Four areas of agreement (Ortlund)

#### 1. “Mere Christianity” (broad shared faith)

- Historic Protestants often treated Eastern Orthodoxy as **Christian**, not as having lapsed from the faith. Calvin criticized Rome for treating the “Greek church” as schismatic; Luther likewise reacted strongly to Catholic denigration of the Orthodox (Ortlund cites Calvin’s Institutes and a 1519 Lutheran context).
- **Correspondence of Lutheran theologians with Patriarch Jeremiah II** (Constantinople): six letters; Lutherans affirmed shared salvation in Christ and desire for closer unity; substantial agreement on scripture, God, Trinity, Christ’s two natures, evil, Christ’s saving work, second coming, and shared opposition to Rome on issues such as communion in both kinds, indulgences, papacy, and purgatory. Jeremiah said they had agreed on “almost all of the main subjects” but also noted disagreements and urged Lutherans to enter the Orthodox Church—dialogue did not resolve divisions, yet Lutherans regarded him as a Christian brother.
- **John Jewel (Anglican)**: if Protestants were schismatic for leaving Rome, Rome was schismatic for leaving Eastern Orthodoxy; Jewel showed historical sympathy toward Orthodoxy and appreciation for Orthodox criticism of practices such as private masses, distorted sacraments, purgatory, and pardons.
- Early Protestants distinguished **substance of the faith** from **errors and accretions** (Ortlund cites Perkins, Turretin, and his own “mere Christianity” label). That substance was held to be shared not only with Eastern Orthodoxy but also, in some formulations, with Oriental Orthodoxy and others.

#### 2. Doctrine of God and the Trinity

- Early Protestants articulated the Trinity in continuity with the **first four ecumenical councils** and early creeds (Apostles’, Nicene, Athanasian). Examples: Anglican Article 1; Belgic Confession; Augsburg Confession Article 1 referencing Nicaea.
- **Filioque** is flagged as a real disagreement (West: Spirit proceeds from Father and Son); many Protestants followed the Western formulation but **Turretin** argued dispute over filioque is not “deadly heresy”—one aspect of trinitarian doctrine that does not erase broader agreement.

#### 3. Christology and the work of Christ

- Protestant confessions align with **Chalcedon** (two natures, one person); Article 2 of the 39 Articles is explicitly Chalcedonian; similar patterns in Augsburg, Westminster, Methodist confessions.
- On Christ’s work, Protestants placed themselves in continuity with the early church, including **Christ’s descent** (Lutheran, Anglican, Reformed traditions). Emphases on atonement may differ but the narrative shell is shared: virgin birth, sinless life, death, burial, resurrection, ascension, return.

#### 4. Social witness

- In a secularizing West, Ortlund argues Protestants and Orthodox can cooperate where **basic Christian morality** is challenged: dignity of the person, marriage and family, objective morality. He cites **Peter Kreeft**: when a lunatic is at the door, quarreling brothers reconcile.

### Four areas of disagreement (Ortlund, mostly descriptive)

#### 1. Ecclesiology: one institutional church?

- Orthodox tradition (with nuance over time) has often claimed to be the **one true church**; Western innovations such as the filioque have been treated as heresy placing people outside canonical boundaries and, in some strands, outside saving grace mediated in the sacraments.
- Ortlund cites **Paisius Velichkovsky** (18th c.) on those who affirm the filioque; **Confession of Dositheus** (1672, Synod of Jerusalem) on the necessity of the bishop and language about being forsaken by the Holy Spirit when outside the church; and 19th-century patriarchal encyclicals portraying Catholics and Protestants as heretics off the “ark.”
- He notes that today some broader acknowledgment of salvation appears, yet **denial of valid Protestant sacraments** and “Protestant communities are not churches” remains common in Orthodox teaching as he understands it.
- **Protestant contrast**: “No salvation outside the church” was affirmed, but Reformers denied that **one institution** exhausts the church. **Calvin**: the church is the society of saints spread through the world and ages, united by one doctrine and Spirit; we are not schismatic from *her* but desire her bosom. Ortlund clarifies this is not universalism: boundaries exist, but not **institutional exclusivism** around a single hierarchy.
- **Mark 9** (“not against us is for us”): the person in view was acting in Christ’s name; **Matthew 7**—good fruit, good tree. Example of a village reached online: baptism, creed, Eucharist, deliverance—**that is a church** even without ties to a wider structure; where Christ is present in word and sacrament, a valid church may exist.

#### 2. Scripture, tradition, and the church

- Both sides affirm scripture as **infallible**. Protestants: scripture is the **only infallible rule**; the church is **not** infallible and must measure herself by scripture.
- **Dositheus** (as summarized by Ortlund): the church, like scripture, is infallible.
- Protestant view: post-apostolic organs are **fallible** and **reformable** under scripture—parallel to Jesus correcting Pharisees with scripture in **Mark 7**. Ortlund calls this the deepest **methodological** disagreement, affecting what “Christianity” is taken to be.

#### 3. Salvation and justification

- Ortlund acknowledges Orthodox (and patristic) emphasis on **deification** and gifts to the wider church; remaining tension: **formal cause** of justification for Protestants—**imputed righteousness** of Christ; standing before God is not based on what is within us but on Christ’s alien righteousness (**Romans 4** and related). Grounds for **assurance**: internal righteousness is never a sufficient basis before a holy God.

#### 4. Worship and devotion

- Protestant concerns: **intercession of saints**, **veneration of icons**, relics (unresolved in 1580s Lutheran–Orthodox exchange). Mapped loosely to **Solas**: disagreement (2) touches **sola scriptura**; (3) **sola fide** / **sola gratia**; (4) **Solus Christus** and **Soli Deo Gloria**—mediation and glory. Core Protestant claim: certain practices do not go back to the apostles.

### Ortlund’s closing theses

1. From a Protestant view, regard each other as **Christians** and value common ground.
2. Disagreements are **serious**—a real ecclesial divide; do not trivialize.
3. **Keep dialoguing**; historically, Orthodox–Protestant conversation has barely begun; misunderstanding remains; technology enables more.

### Fr. Demetrios’s response

#### “Mere Christianity” and ecumenical generosity

- C.S. Lewis’s *Mere Christianity* reminds that faith must be proclaimed and defended—especially against secularism and atheism; constant polemic over in-house differences is a **luxury of a bygone era**.
- Gratitude for Lutheran/Calvinist **recognition of Orthodox Christian identity** (also serving their case against Rome). The **substance/accidents** (or substance/accretions) analogy: we often assume we disagree on substance and agree on accidents; **probably the reverse**—large common ground with real but proportionate differences.

#### Trinity and Christology

- Strong convergence on **faith in the triune God**. Nuanced differences: **essence–energies**, East often **persons → essence**, West **essence → persons**. **Filioque** is the weightiest difference; **ecumenical progress**: a recent **Lutheran–Orthodox common statement** (week before this event per Fr. Demetrios): Father as cause of the Spirit’s procession; **Nicene–Constantinopolitan Creed in original form without filioque**; Greek patristic **“through the Son”** language to be explored as a bridge.
- **Christology**: both affirm **Chalcedon**; shared confession of one person, fully God and fully man, life/death/resurrection for salvation. Differences in **atonement** need exploration. Orthodox also receive **three post-Chalcedonian** ecumenical councils as binding.

#### Social witness

- Agreement on need for witness; Fr. Demetrios says **evangelicals surpass Orthodox** in social work—Orthodox have much to learn.

#### Ecclesiology: charismatic vs. canonical boundaries

- He appeals to **Fr. Georges Florovsky**: **canonical** limits of the Orthodox Church are real but do not exhaust **charismatic** limits; **grace of the Spirit** is present outside canonical Orthodoxy. Many non-Orthodox will be saved; many Orthodox will not.
- Past **exclusivist language** often reflected **threat** (Catholic unionism, Protestant missions in Orthodox lands) and **pastoral warning** to the flock; parallels Protestant harsh rhetoric (e.g., Luther on the papacy; Calvin and Servetus—he names these cautiously as shared human failings).
- **Orthodox “confessions”** (Dositheus, Cyril Lucaris, etc.) **do not equal Protestant confessions** in authority; some were **Calvin-influenced** and later **condemned**. Today most Orthodox **take for granted** that non-Orthodox are Christians; converts with Trinitarian baptism are typically **received by chrismation**, not baptism. **Holy and Great Council (Crete, 2016)** referred explicitly to **Christian churches**.

#### Scripture, tradition, infallibility

- Agreement that scripture is **God-breathed** and infallible. **Hilary of Poitiers** (as cited): scripture is not in what we read but in what we **understand**—the **church secures correct understanding**; scripture is often misunderstood; if it were always clear, Protestants would agree on everything.
- **Church infallibility** does not mean no mistakes; many councils erred. It means that in **fundamental doctrines** (Trinity, Christology, etc.) the church cannot be ultimately led astray—or the gates of Hades would prevail.

#### Justification and worship

- Orthodox discomfort with **purely legal** categories; emphasis on **ontology**—righteousness **imparted**, not merely imputed; become righteous, not only deemed righteous. **Baptism** as entry; **faith** and willingness; thereafter **good works** necessary for salvation—some proximity to **New Perspective on Paul** (as he characterizes it).
- **Icons**: witness to **incarnation** and embodied faith; communion with Christ **through** the icon. **Saints**: **totus Christus**—honor to saints is Christ’s honor; asking saints to pray as we ask fellow Christians. **Liturgical/aesthetic life**: fasting, long services, Jesus Prayer, **Divine Liturgy**; **lex orandi, lex credendi**—**doxology and dogma** belong together. Orthodox liturgical life is, in his view, **more advanced**; admiration for Christians who persevere without that support.

### Open dialogue (selected threads)

- **Are we both Christians?** Fr. Demetrios: **unambiguously yes**; ecumenism from its inception; dialogue between Christians toward union. **Reception**: Christians from Catholic or Protestant backgrounds received by chrismation; non-Christians by baptism—a clear distinction.
- **Ortlund’s follow-up**: (1) tension with **much historical Orthodox rhetoric**; (2) what counts as **valid Eucharist** for a Protestant? Fr. Demetrios: first millennium **oneness** of the church means schism is always problematic; truth matters for how disagreement affects **ecclesial identity** and **sacraments**; **grace** is present in the Protestant world; **baptism** implicitly recognized when Protestants are chrismated on entry to Orthodoxy.
- **Pastor’s intervention**: some Orthodox require **rebaptism** for marriage converts; **Greece** as privileged majority church vs. “threat” narrative. Fr. Demetrios: distinguish **official teaching** vs. **popular/fundamentalist** practice; **Patriarchate of Constantinople** and **Church of Greece**: valid **Trinitarian baptism** → chrismation (his experience in London); conservative voices still deserve a hearing—often from exaggerated but sincere concern for **truth** (he links back to Machen/Luther anecdote).
- **Holy and Great Council (2016)**: document on the church references **Christian churches and communities**; debate among Orthodox continues; tension between **church is one** and **church is divided**.
- **Evangelism**: focus primarily on **non-Christians**; evangelizing other churches causes trouble; Greece and Europe **secularizing rapidly**; for unity’s sake, **evangelize one’s own people** first while remaining open to other traditions (example: school visits to a Bible college to see another tradition without aiming at conversion).
- **John 3:5–7**: Ortlund notes **Protestant diversity** (Carson: not necessarily baptismal; others: baptismal regeneration); ties to **Ezekiel** water imagery. Fr. Demetrios: traditionally **baptism** (water and Spirit).
- **“What must I do to be saved?” (five minutes):** Ortlund: **gospel**, **faith and repentance**, **baptism**, join a church; good works as fruit, not instrument of **legal standing**. Fr. Demetrios: **metanoia**, faith, love, works; Christ’s life/death/resurrection; join a **vibrant Christian community**; perseverance matters (parable of the soils).
- **Theology of beauty**: Ortlund—Protestants should learn from Orthodox **intentional beauty** in worship space; caveats for poverty/modernity. Fr. Demetrios—story of the **Chora** monastery mosaics (he visited in his early twenties; the building’s later civic status under Turkish government is mentioned in passing); beauty with truth and repentance; hymnography (Chrysostom, Basil). Ortlund clarifies Protestant concern is not all religious art but **use** of icons: **bowing, prayer through the icon**, physical mediation—argued **non-apostolic** (**sola scriptura** + history).
- **Imputed vs. imparted righteousness**: Ortlund—**legal dimension** real (God as judge); **union with Christ** central; **Luther**: ultimate trust not in one’s own works for standing; **Romans 4** “credited” language; need to be **clothed** in Christ on the last day. Fr. Demetrios—no boasting before God; legal imagery exists in fathers; Orthodox stress **ontological/medicinal** transformation; story of translating “make us worthy” vs. “count us worthy” in the Lord’s Prayer—**being made worthy** by grace, not merely counted, while never meriting salvation.

## Core Ideas

- Unity and truth are both Christian duties; indifferentism about doctrine can be worse than honest division.
- Historic Protestantism often **recognized Orthodoxy as Christian** and shared creedal DNA through the early councils and creeds.
- The deepest flashpoints are **ecclesiology** (where the church’s boundaries lie), **authority** (scripture alone infallible vs. church guarding infallible understanding), **justification** (imputation vs. impartation/legal vs. ontological emphasis), and **cultus** (icons, saints, liturgical life).
- Contemporary Orthodox voices like Fr. Demetrios stress **Florovsky’s** canonical vs. charismatic distinction, **chrismation** reception of baptized Christians, and ecumenical documents—while acknowledging messy history and praxis.

## Key Terms

- **Filioque** — Western addition: Holy Spirit proceeds from Father and Son; major East–West trinitarian divide.
- **Sola scriptura** (as used here) — Scripture as **only infallible rule**; church and tradition fallible and reformable.
- **Imputed righteousness** — Protestant articulation: legal standing based on Christ’s righteousness, not inherent merit.
- **Imparted / ontological righteousness** — Emphasis in this Orthodox presentation: becoming righteous by grace, not only being declared righteous.
- **Chrismation** — Orthodox reception of converts already baptized in the Holy Trinity.
- **Totus Christus** — Christ head and body; rationale for honor to saints united to Christ.
- **Lex orandi, lex credendi** — Worship (especially liturgy) and doctrine mutually inform each other.
