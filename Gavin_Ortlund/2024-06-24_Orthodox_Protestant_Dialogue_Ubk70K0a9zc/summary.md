# Summary

Gavin Ortlund opens an in-person Athens dialogue by pairing sincere ecumenical charity with a refusal to treat doctrine as trivial, using historical Protestant recognition of Eastern Orthodoxy and shared Nicene–Chalcedonian creedal DNA as four “agreement” pillars—then naming four serious disagreements: ecclesiological exclusivism and sacraments, scripture vs. church/tradition as the ultimate rule, justification (imputation vs. impartation), and contested worship practices (icons, saints). Fr. Demetrios responds with gratitude, Florovsky’s canonical vs. charismatic church, modern reception by chrismation, a Hilary-flavored argument that the church secures scriptural understanding, and a warm affirmation that the conversation is between Christians. The Q&A presses history vs. present practice (rebaptism, exclusivist rhetoric, Crete 2016), evangelism in secularizing Greece, John 3:5–7, beauty in worship, and a closing exchange on imputed and imparted righteousness that surfaces partial convergence under grace.

# Core Question

How can Protestants and Eastern Orthodox honor each other as Christians and keep talking when their official narratives disagree sharply on where the church ends, which voice is infallible, how righteousness before God works, and what worship practices are apostolic?

# Key Concepts

- **Unity vs. indifferentism** — Division may be painful, but treating core doctrine as unimportant is spiritually worse (Machen on Marburg).
- **Sola scriptura (infallible rule)** — Scripture alone as infallible norm vs. the church as guardian of infallible understanding of scripture.
- **Canonical vs. charismatic ecclesiology** — Official Orthodox boundaries vs. the Spirit’s grace beyond those lines (Florovsky, as presented).
- **Imputation vs. impartation** — Legal standing in Christ’s righteousness vs. ontological transformation by grace (with both sides affirming gift and judgment language in places).
- **Reception rites** — Chrismation as practical recognition of Trinitarian baptism when Protestants enter Orthodoxy, contrasted with lingering rebaptism in some settings.

# Why It Matters

The talk names tensions that shape real relationships—marriage across traditions, missionary work in historically Orthodox lands, whether communion tables are “the same meal,” and how much past anathemas should define present charity. It models calm, source-aware disagreement and shows where modern statements and practices have moved without pretending centuries of harsh rhetoric never existed—helpful for anyone navigating Protestant–Orthodox friendship, apologetics, or ecumenical reading lists.
