# Why Young Men Are Becoming Eastern Orthodox

## Metadata

- Author: Gavin Ortlund
- Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MXNL8BP3S90
- Date: 2024-12-04
- Folder: Gavin_Ortlund

## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and aim

- Ortlund cites a *New York Post* article titled along the lines of young men leaving traditional churches for “masculine Orthodox Christianity in droves.”
- The article references a survey of Orthodox churches in the United States reporting a **78% increase in converts in 2022** compared with pre-pandemic levels in **2019**.
- The trend is described as **not slowing down**; it is **mostly men**, especially **young men**.
- **Goal of this video:** not a detailed critique of Eastern Orthodox theology (he points to other videos on icon veneration, institutional exclusivism, debates, etc.) but a **shorter, pastoral** word to **Protestants and especially evangelicals** to **pay attention** and **learn** from the phenomenon.
- **Three questions:** (1) Why is this happening? (2) Why does it matter? (3) What should we do about it?
- **Sponsor segment:** Trinity Evangelical Divinity School (TEDS)—encouragement toward formal theological education (online, modular, in-person); Ortlund studied there (post-doctoral fellowship) and grew up in the area.

### 1) Why this is happening

- Ortlund treats individual conversions as unique but highlights **three recurring factors** echoed in the article and in his own conversations.

#### A. Search for stability (tradition, rootedness, permanence)

- People want something that **does not feel constantly changing**—“standing on a rock” amid chaos.
- Eastern Orthodoxy is said to **powerfully appeal** to that hunger, even if one disputes the claim of a wholly “changeless” church.
- **Convert testimony (Anglican background):** frustration that there was “a lot of change” even in one lifetime and **no way to stop the change**—a sense of **powerlessness** as culture shifts and churches are swept along.
- Young people experience **constant change** elsewhere; a **structured, continuous** form of faith feels comforting.
- Repeated language from converts: wanting to be somewhere **stable**, **ancient**, **not going to change**.
- Another quote (article): everything is changing—Protestant churches, Catholic Church, culture, government; people want what is **historic** and **not built on sand**.
- Ortlund notes some perceive **Roman Catholicism** (e.g. under **Pope Francis**) as **unstable like sand**, alongside Protestantism, while Orthodoxy is perceived as stable—he reports this as a **sociological perception**, not necessarily his theological agreement.
- Imagery of **rock vs. sand** links forward to **Josiah Trenham’s** book ***Rock and Sand*** (Orthodox priest featured in the article).

#### B. Sense of rigor (especially resonant with young men)

- Article discusses **masculinity in worship** and **rigor**.
- Converts compare former Protestant settings to a **TED Talk** (sermons) or a **rock concert** (worship)—Ortlund says he has used similar criticism of some evangelical practice.
- **Jordan Peterson** (quoted): unlike a Protestant service dependent on the preacher, criticizing an Orthodox service is **“like going to a ballet and asking what’s going on”**—a dismissive question; the liturgy is **orchestrated** with many layers, not centered on one person.
- Ortlund agrees Protestant settings often **put more pressure on the preacher** as focal point.

#### C. Online apologetics (“internet Orthodoxy”)

- A **catechist** at an Orthodox parish in **California** (speaker associates with **Josiah Trenham’s** church, **Riverside** area) is quoted: the parish is full of **young converts**; growth attributed partly to the **internet**.
- Phrase: **“Internet Orthodoxy”** as a recognized phenomenon—people arrive saying they were **arguing online**, discovered Orthodoxy, found the church.
- Broader point: **YouTube, podcasts, social media, even TikTok** shape major life decisions; this is **sociological reality** whether one likes it or not.
- Ortlund frames his own channel (**Truth Unites**) as trying to **meet needs** and be a **friend to young men** wrestling with theology and angst.

### 2) Why it matters

#### A. Do not minimize the Protestant–Orthodox divide

- **Naive parental reaction** (example): child becomes Orthodox after college—“at least they’re taking faith seriously.”
- Ortlund stresses **serious theological and ecclesial differences**; he tries to honor **common ground** but also **honesty** about division.
- **Josiah Trenham, *Rock and Sand* (p. 153 cited):** exercise for Protestant inquirers—two columns, **“heresies and errors”** vs. **“truth,”** writing thoroughly what they bring from Protestantism; rationale: some are tempted to see conversion as a **denominational switch** rather than **“conversion from heresy to Orthodoxy.”**
- Ortlund: he **disagrees** with which side is heresy vs. truth but appreciates the **stakes** as historically authentic Orthodox self-understanding.
- **Not** like Baptist ↔ Presbyterian moves; Orthodox framing is closer to **“you’re not doing church”** (e.g. his reference to **1672 Synod of Jerusalem** on **not having a valid Eucharist** among non-Orthodox).
- He references his prior video inviting answers on **saints, councils, and “no salvation outside Eastern Orthodoxy”**—diverse replies; his **historical thesis**: Orthodoxy has **restricted salvation to herself**, with **recent softening** in some quarters.
- Bottom line: converts are entering a communion that has treated Protestants as **heretics**; **stakes are high**.

#### B. The trend exposes weakness in much contemporary Protestant practice

- **One-sentence thesis:** The **stability** and **rigor** people seek must be **found in the gospel of Jesus Christ**, which will require **reform and renewal** in **Protestant church practice**.

### 3) How Protestants should respond

#### Pay attention and study

- Avoid **ignorance** of other Christian traditions; **online conversation** is imperfect but increases **discussion** vs. a century ago.

#### Retrieve historic Protestant worship (major appeal)

- **Not a small issue**—Ortlund calls the need **dire** and **important**.
- **Generalization (not total condemnation):** much evangelical worship over recent decades feels **watered down** and unlike **most of church history**, which gives **ammunition** to critics who treat **worst examples** as the whole.
- **Seeker-sensitive** instincts have **normalized** an **entertainment** feel and often lack sense of **God’s transcendence**.
- **Convert quote:** “Orthodoxy isn’t about us, it’s about God”—young people **sense pandering** when things are watered down; Ortlund clarifies he does **not** agree Protestant worship **cannot** be God-centered, but the **desire** for worship that **lifts out of self**, connects to **history**, and **challenges** is **healthy** and pastors should **meet** it.

#### Example: pulpit vs. stage

- Anecdote: a church moved preaching from **elevated pulpit** to **stage** with **music stand** to reduce distance and increase intimacy—motives may be **good**; he is **not legalistic** (cites God using **outdoor preaching** in **Second Great Awakening**).
- Point: **pulpits developed for reasons**; many changes lack **historical intentionality**—reform should be **aware**, not merely **cultural drift**.
- **Appeal:** “Now is not the time to dumb things down.”
- **Historic Protestant worship** (e.g. **Presbyterian church ~200 years ago**) had **rich liturgy**, **doctrinal sermons**, **theological hymns**, **beauty**—and Protestantism has **strengths** (e.g. **theology of preaching**).
- Encouragement to retrieve **historic expressions** within one’s own stream (**Methodist**, **Baptist**, etc.).

#### Non-denominational and isolation from history

- If **non-denominational**, have a **principled reason**; consider whether **joining a denomination** is wiser (pragmatic usefulness).
- Even if non-denominational, **study church history** to avoid errors of **rootlessness**.

#### Resource

- Video: **“Evangelical worship needs church history”**—five concrete changes (link in description).

### 4) Gospel assurance: where stability really rests

- **Pastoral close:** needs for **stability in chaos** are **ultimately met in Jesus Christ and his gospel**—interpret present hungers through a **gospel framework**.
- **Church tradition** conversations matter, but **danger**: seeking stability primarily in a **historical expression** rather than **in Christ**.
- **Rock/sand imagery:** point young people to **Scripture** as the **solid rock**—**Isaiah 40** (grass withers; **word of God** stands forever). Connects to **sola scriptura** as locating **ultimate trust** in **God’s words** as the **only infallible rule**; **church** can err.
- **Hebrews 10:** **Jesus’s death** opens **access to God**—imagery of **tabernacle/curtain**; contrast **high priest** entering **Holy of Holies** once a year (Leviticus 16) with tradition about **rope on ankle**—now **every believer** in Christ has **access**.
- **Pastoral application:** anxiety → **prayer**; intellectual angst → **James 1** (God gives **wisdom**); **Holy Spirit** as **paraclete**—ministry of **assurance**, **counsel**.
- **Not** claiming this settles all technical debates with Orthodoxy; it is a **pastoral** word alongside **Protestant worship renewal**.

### Closing

- blunt summary: **Protestant worship** in many places **needs reform** and **deeper church-historical connection**; long-term project; invites engagement on the related video and in comments; **Merry Christmas** sign-off.

## Core Ideas

- Sociological surge in **young male converts** to Eastern Orthodoxy (article + survey) is a **lasting trend** worth Protestant attention.
- Three **drivers**: **stability/tradition**, **rigor/liturgical density**, **internet-mediated discovery** (“internet Orthodoxy”).
- Orthodox **self-understanding** often frames Protestantism as **error/heresy**, not a **flavor** of Christianity—**high stakes** for families and evangelism.
- Protestant response: **retrieve historic Protestant worship** and **transcendence** without abandoning **gospel** and **Scripture** as ultimate **rock**.
- **Sola scriptura** and **direct access to God in Christ** (Hebrews, Isaiah) anchor **stability** pastorally.

## Key Terms

- **Internet Orthodoxy** — converts shaped by online argument and media who then join Orthodox parishes.
- ***Rock and Sand*** — Josiah Trenham’s book (Orthodox perspective on Protestantism); **rock vs. sand** imagery for stable vs. unstable foundations.
- **Seeker-sensitive movement** — Ortlund’s label for trends that normalized **entertainment-shaped** worship.
- **Sola scriptura** — Scripture as **infallible rule**; ultimate trust in **God’s word** over fallible church voice.
- **1672 Synod of Jerusalem** — Ortlund’s example for Orthodox claims about **valid Eucharist** outside Orthodoxy.
