# Summary

Gavin Ortlund reacts to a *New York Post* piece on surging U.S. Orthodox converts—especially young men—and frames a pastoral case for evangelicals to notice the trend. He groups motives under stability, liturgical rigor, and internet-fueled discovery; then argues the shift matters because Eastern Orthodoxy often understands Protestantism in maximal terms (heresy, invalid church), not as a minor style preference. His constructive response is to recover historically serious Protestant worship and transcendence while anchoring ultimate stability in Christ, Scripture, and gospel assurance—not only in a tradition’s aesthetic permanence.

# Core Question

Why are young men moving toward Eastern Orthodoxy in growing numbers, and how should Protestant churches respond without dismissing either the sociological pull or the depth of the theological divide?

# Key Concepts

- **Convert surge (article/survey):** large post-pandemic uptick (e.g. 78% vs. 2019) concentrated among young men.
- **Stability narrative:** hunger for permanence amid cultural and ecclesial flux; rock vs. sand imagery (linked to Trenham’s *Rock and Sand*).
- **Rigor and liturgy:** TED-talk sermons and concert-like worship contrasted with dense, choreographed Orthodox liturgy (Peterson’s ballet analogy).
- **Internet Orthodoxy:** online argument and algorithmic media as pathways into parishes.
- **High-stakes ecumenics:** Orthodox framing of Protestantism as error; synodal and sacramental boundaries (e.g. Jerusalem 1672 cited).
- **Protestant retrieval:** historic liturgy, doctrine, and beauty within evangelical and denominational traditions; companion video on worship and church history.
- **Gospel location of stability:** Isaiah 40, sola scriptura as ultimate authority, Hebrews 10 and direct access to God, Spirit’s assuring work.

# Why It Matters

The video names a live cultural pattern (who is converting and why) that shapes families, campuses, and online discourse. Underestimating Orthodox self-understanding produces naive pastoral responses; ignoring evangelical worship’s thinness hands critics an easy story. Ortlund’s aim is strategic and spiritual: meet legitimate longings inside Protestant renewal and clear gospel confidence, not only in polemics against another communion.
