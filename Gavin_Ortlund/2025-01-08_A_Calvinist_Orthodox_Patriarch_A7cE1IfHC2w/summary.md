# Summary

Gavin Ortlund introduces **Cyril Lucaris**, who served as **Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople** while teaching a **Reformed-style confession** (1629) and corresponding theology in letters. The talk is deliberately modest: it aims for **historical accuracy** about Lucaris—countering forgery theories and caricatures—and **conceptual clarity** about what **sola Scriptura**, **sola fide**, **two sacraments**, **spiritual presence** at the Eucharist, and **visible/invisible church** language looked like in his hands. Ortlund contrasts Lucaris with the later **Confession of Dositheus**, sketches allies like **Meletios Pegalios**, and closes with the **Synod of Jerusalem** as a defining, **anti-Protestant** response that—per **Philip Schaff**—played for Orthodoxy a role akin to **Trent** for Catholicism. Lucaris was **canonized** in **2009**, a fact that complicates dismissive narratives.

# Core Question

How can **church history** be both **contingent** (a patriarch publicly Reformed in doctrine) and **boundary-forming** (later synods hardening Orthodoxy against Protestantism)—and what do Lucaris’s **words** actually claim about **Scripture, justification, sacraments, and devotion**?

# Key Concepts

- **Ecumenical Patriarch** and **Protestant confession** as the same author’s work (authorship defended by **self-testimony**, **synodal witness**, and **letters**).
- **Sola Scriptura** as **ranked authority** (infallible norm vs. fallible church teaching), contrasted with **Dositheus**.
- **Sola fide** with **good works** as **necessary witness**, not **merit** before God.
- **Two sacraments**; **spiritual eating** and **non-transubstantiation** real presence.
- **Visible/invisible church** and **fallible** ecclesial judgment.
- **Synod of Jerusalem** and **modern Orthodox–Protestant** boundaries.

# Why It Matters

Without figures like Lucaris, debates about **Protestant vs. Orthodox** identity easily slide into **stereotypes** (e.g. what “sola Scriptura” must mean) or **forgery myths** that avoid primary texts. This episode shows **real historical complexity** in the **17th-century Greek world**, explains why **later synods** reacted as they did, and gives **precise vocabulary** for discussing **Scripture, the Eucharist, icons, and the saints**—whether one agrees with Lucaris or not.
