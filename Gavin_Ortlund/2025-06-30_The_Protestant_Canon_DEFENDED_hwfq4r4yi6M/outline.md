## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and purpose

- **Canon** = which books count as Scripture; the topic generates anxiety and is often raised against **sola scriptura** (“how can you have Scripture alone without an infallible canon?”).
- Guests: **Ben** (Cleave to Antiquity) and **Javier Purdomo**; goal is to help viewers respond to objections and to ground a Protestant view historically, not to dismiss technical study.

### How the issue surfaces in dialogue

- In debates on sola scriptura, the canon is framed as an **undercutting defeater** for Protestants.
- Common **pop-apologetics** pattern: any use of Scripture is met with “you don’t have an infallible canon.”
- Counter-question offered: Roman Catholics claim **infallible Scripture** and **infallible tradition**—can they produce an **infallible list of infallible traditions**? (parallel structure).

### Infallibility vs. authority (philosophy of knowledge)

- **Epistemology**: “How do you know which books are in the Bible?”
- **Fallible list of infallible books**: Protestants are said to face this problem; speakers argue the objection is often overstated.
- **Key distinction**: **Infallibility** (metaphysical: cannot err) vs. **authority** (practical: binding).
  - Examples: **umpires** (authoritative calls, not infallible); **school rules** (binding but fallible); **courts** (fallible but authoritative); **excommunication** can be binding without being infallible.
- **Joan of Arc** cited: excommunicated and later canonized—shows fallible disciplinary acts on the Catholic side too.
- **Binding** can mean: (1) irreversible/infallible “on earth and in heaven,” or (2) real institutional consequences without infallibility—need to **define terms**.

### Describing major Christian canons (descriptive, not yet argued)

- **Protestant**: 66 books.
- **Roman Catholic**: 73 books; prefer **deuterocanonical** (second in acceptance), not “apocrypha” in their usage; claim is not lesser authority for those books.
- **Eastern Orthodox**: **no single infallibly defined canon list**; answers differ by jurisdiction (e.g., **Orthodox Church in America** publicly aligns with the Catholic list; **Russian Orthodox** historically closer to Protestant-like boundaries in some respects).
- **Ethiopian Orthodox** mentioned as an example of a much larger canon (e.g., 81 books)—illustrates diversity.
- **Two voices in Eastern Orthodoxy** (examples given): **Philaret of Moscow** (Longer Catechism)—follows Athanasius, Cyril of Jerusalem, John of Damascus, **22** canonical OT books; omits Wisdom and other deuterocanonicals as absent from Hebrew; assigns those a **secondary rank** for catechumens. **Michael Pomazansky** (*Orthodox Dogmatic Theology*)—canonical vs. non-canonical distinction. **Nicodemus the Hagiorite** and **Agapios**—“canonical” vs. “readable” books; dislike “apocrypha” for deuterocanonicals but still **distinguish** tiers.
- **Council of Trent** and **two readings** among Catholics:
  - **Exclusionary**: list **closes** the canon (e.g., **William Albrecht** associated with this emphasis).
  - **Inclusionary**: Trent gives a **baseline**; **more books could be added** later (e.g., reunion scenarios)—associated with **Michael Lofton**, **Jimmy Akin**, **Trent Horn** (or room left open).

### “Fallible list of infallible books”—philosophical and historical replies (Gavin)

- **Philosophical**: demanding infallibility for *every* step of recognizing infallibility risks **infinite regress**; every system has a **cutoff** where **fallible reception** meets infallible content (e.g., fallible ears hearing God; fallible counting of ex cathedra statements; fallible discernment of which councils count as ecumenical).
- **Protestant role of the church**: church as **witness** (like John the Baptist pointing to Christ)—**necessary** for discerning Scripture but **subordinate** to Scripture and not infallible in that discernment (developed more in other videos by Gavin).
- **Historical**: an **infallible canon list** is argued to be a **late** development (early modern / late medieval in the form pressed today); early and medieval church had **local, fallible** deliberation and ongoing debate—including **major cardinals** disagreeing on the canon near Trent.
- **Jewish reception** of the Law: largely **fallible reception** of what God gave—parallel for much of redemptive history.
- **Thomas Aquinas** mentioned as in a position analogous in some ways to traditions without a fully settled infallible list.

### Tu quoque and reductio (Javier)

- If “no infallible list until Trent/Florence” defeats Protestantism, it also implicates the **first-millennium church**—a problem for anyone claiming continuity with that church.
- **Reductio ad absurdum** (informal): if an objection entails absurd unintended consequences, the objection fails; analogy of **both parties in the same “closet”**—need cooperative clarity, not mockery.

### Roman Catholic “infallible tradition” list

- Pressed similarly: **no infallible list of infallible traditions**.
- **Eastern Orthodox**: which councils are infallible, which canons of those councils are still held—gets messy; many **conciliar canons** no longer maintained.

### Church history: “Protestant canon is a 16th-century novelty”

- **New Catholic Encyclopedia** (cited charitably): **Jerome** distinguished **canonical** vs. **ecclesiastical** books; latter good for reading, not authoritative Scripture; situation **unclear for centuries**; **John of Damascus**, **Gregory the Great**, **Walafrid**, **Nicholas of Lyra**, **Tostado** still doubted deuterocanonical canonicity.
- **Council of Rome** often cited for 73 books—**Jerome** attended yet **doubted** Apocrypha; **Vulgate prologue**: Apocrypha may be read for **edification**, not **confirming dogma**.
- **Terminology shifts**: “**Apocrypha**” can mean (1) hidden/heretical (e.g., Gospel of Thomas), or (2) **hidden origins** → **secondary rank** relative to “canonical” in a stricter sense.
- **“Canonical”** sometimes = **read in church** (broader); sometimes = **dogma-settling, full divine authority** (narrower)—mixing senses causes confusion.
- **Protestant historical claim** (strong form): many **Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican** voices affirmed a **two-tier** distinction: deuterocanonical / ecclesiastical books **esteemed** but not equal to narrow-canonical Scripture; distinct from **truly bad** apocryphal works (Apocalypse of Peter, etc.).
- **Contemporary Protestant drift**: 19th century as a **watershed** where some Protestants **flatten** or neglect the distinction—making Catholic talking points sound stronger than they should against **classical Protestantism**.

### Jerome and the Gelasian tradition

- “Only Jerome” pushback vs. **scholarship**: Jerome is **clear**; reception in **medieval** and **scholarly** circles differs from **pop apologetics**.
- **Council of Rome / Gelasian decree**: only **extant** copy in **Gelasian** material; **Pope Gelasius** names **Jerome** as authoritative; also **subordinates Rufinus** to Jerome where Rufinus took liberties—complicates “just Jerome went rogue.”
- **Spuriousness debate**: **Denzinger** noted parts of the text may not be authentic; **Damasus** vs. later layers; **C. H. Turner**: section on Holy Spirit **quotes Augustine verbatim** from a work **after** Rome/Damasus—**no historical value** for that section.
- **Jerome’s Hebrew naming** in the decree matching **Prologus Galeatus**—argument that if influenced by Jerome, composition is **later** than Damasus (**H. Horth** cited).
- **Silence**: canon list not cited by Jerome or others **4th–9th centuries** despite frequent canon discussion.
- **Gary Michuta** (Catholic author on larger Catholic Bible): cited as **not** relying on Council of Rome for apologetics—yet Rome is **often** cited online.

### Two-tier canon: usage vs. lists

- **Pattern**: Fathers often **cite** a book as “Scripture” while **excluding** it from a **canon list**—implies **tiered** senses of “Scripture,” not necessarily flip-flopping.
- **Examples**: **Jerome** late citations; **Athanasius**—Against Arians cites Wisdom, Sirach; **Festal Letter 39** excludes them from canon; **Cyril of Jerusalem**—cites deuterocanonical books in lectures but gives a list that excludes them.
- **Catholic apologetics** sometimes stresses **citation/utilization** over **lists**; speakers reply: **Lutherans/Reformed** also **cite** Tobit, Judith, Wisdom as Scripture in a **secondary** sense while denying **full** canonicity—same methodological problem if “citation = full canon.”
- **Lutheran**: **Johann Gerhard**, **Johann Quenstedt**; **Lutheran Service Book** lectionary still uses material from deuterocanonical books in historic Western pattern.

### Cyril of Jerusalem (catechumens)

- Warns against troubling over **disputed** “apocryphal” writings; points to **22** OT books **translated by the seventy**—**Serapion of Antioch**, **Senate of Laodicea**-type tradition in the East.

### East: Baruch, additions to Esther/Daniel, etc.

- Framed partly as **textual criticism** (what counts as **one book**) rather than totally different “canons” in every case—analogy of **extra chapters** vs. different series.
- **Ezra** naming issues (Septuagint vs. Latin tradition)—**Catholic Encyclopedia** on Ezra cited; **3 Ezra** not in Protestant **or** Catholic Bibles—undercuts simple “gotcha” comparisons to **Hippo/Carthage**.

### Jerome “submitting to the churches” (Against Rufinus)

- Claim: quote is **misused**—context is **two Greek versions of Daniel** (Theodotion vs. LXX), not **deuterocanonical vs. Masoretic** debate.

### Numbering: 22 vs. 24 books (OT)

- **22** Hebrew letters → grouping books; not the same as Catholic **73** vs. Protestant **66** in a naive sense—**different ways of counting/grouping**.
- **Examples** (Javier): 22-book numbering—Athanasius, Basil, Cyril of Jerusalem, Gregory Nazianzen, Hilary, John of Damascus, Leontius, Epiphanius, *Dialogue of Timothy and Aquila*, etc.; 24-book—Jerome, Bede, Hugh of St. Victor, etc.

### Two-tier patristic/medieval witnesses (sample list)

- Athanasius Festal 39; **Rufinus** *Commentary on the Apostles’ Creed*; Cyril **Lecture 4**; **Gregory the Great** *Moralia* (books not canonical); *Synopsis Scripturae Sacrae*; Antoninus, Alfonso Tostado; **Glossa Ordinaria** preface (high Middle Ages, before Luther)—**canonical vs. ecclesiastical** distinction.

### Gregory the Great on non-canonical books

- Citation: not acting irregularly taking testimony from books **not canonical** yet brought out for **edifying the church**—then cites **1 Maccabees**—consistent with **Jerome’s two-tier** use.

### Positive case: which canon? Jesus and the apostles

- **Simple aim**: follow the canon **Jesus and the apostles** used.
- **Jesus**: **tripartite** Hebrew Scripture—**Law, Prophets, Writings** (often shortened to Law and Prophets); working assumption among hearers about **which** books.
- **Josephus**, *Against Apion* **1.8**: **22** books; **closed** from the time of **Artaxerxes**—implies exclusion of later/deuterocanonical additions (speakers’ reading).
- **Early lists**: **Muratorian** (mentioned as neglected), **Melito of Sardis** (2nd c.)—shorter canon, **excludes** deuterocanonicals (edges disputed: Esther, “Wisdom” naming, etc.).

### Melito of Sardis (extended)

- Presented as **Christian** canon: law and prophets concerning **the Savior** and **our entire faith**; calls them **Old Testament**—not merely a Jewish catalog for polemic.
- **Eusebius** introduces the list as catalog of **acknowledged** OT books for his **History**—same vocabulary of **accepted/canonical** used elsewhere.
- **Roger Beckwith** (via speakers): Melito’s **25**-book count doesn’t match standard **22** or **24** Jewish numberings—**Samuel/Kings/Chronicles** divided differently; suggests **Christian** editorial choices, not pure Jewish replication.
- Travel **east** to ascertain canon—could fit **two-tier** question (what is read vs. what is **fully** canonical) and consultation with **mother churches**.

### Pastoral close: certainty and Christ

- Technical canon debates often mask **existential** need for **certainty** (“solid rock” vs. “quicksand”).
- **Gavin**: ultimate ground is **Jesus and the gospel**—not smarts alone; **communion with Christ** by the Spirit.
- **Javier**: **church attendance**, **Scripture**, **prayer**, **fasting**, **fighting sin**—anxiety attributed only to academic questions may ignore spiritual neglect.

### Core Ideas

- Sola scriptura debates routinely turn on **canon** and alleged **Protestant epistemic quicksand**.
- **Authority** and **infallibility** must be distinguished; binding authority is **ubiquitous** and often **fallible**.
- Catholic and Orthodox positions also face **lists**, **interpretation of Trent**, and **non-infallible reception**—objections cut both ways.
- Historical **Protestant** and much **patristic/medieval** Western and Eastern material supports a **two-tier** view of OT books: **fully canonical** vs. **edifying/secondary**—not simple 16th-century **invention**.
- **Jerome**, **Gelasian complex**, **Glossa Ordinaria**, **Gregory the Great**, and many **Eastern 22-book** lists are central **evidence**.
- Positive grounding: alignment with **Jesus’ Hebrew Bible**, **Josephus**, and **2nd-century Christian** witnesses such as **Melito** (via **Eusebius**).

### Key Terms

- **Canon** — the fixed collection of authoritative Scriptural books.
- **Sola scriptura** — Scripture as supreme authority; not “no tradition.”
- **Deuterocanonical** — disputed OT books in Catholic/Orthodox Bibles (Catholic preferred term).
- **Apocrypha** — Protestant term for those books; also used by fathers for **spurious** or **second-rank** works (ambiguous).
- **Infallibility vs. authority** — cannot err vs. binding/obligatory.
- **Council of Trent** — Catholic dogmatic moment on Scripture; **exclusionary vs. inclusive** readings of its canon decree.
- **Gelasian Decree / Council of Rome** — complex manuscript history; authenticity debated.
- **Prologus Galeatus** — Jerome’s preface; Hebrew naming issues in textual criticism of decrees.
- **Two-tier canon** — fully dogmatic Scripture vs. ecclesiastical/edifying secondary use.
- **22 vs. 24** — Hebrew/Jewish-style book **numbering**, not simply Protestant vs. Catholic count.
