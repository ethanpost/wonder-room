# Summary

Gavin Ortlund defines **sola scriptura** as the claim that **Scripture is the only infallible rule** for the church’s faith and practice—not that the Bible is the church’s only authority, nor that every doctrine must appear verbatim in the text. He distinguishes **infallibility** (incapable of error) from **authority** (binding decisions that can still be wrong), cites historic Protestant sources on real but fallible church authority, and separates **sufficiency of scripture** (variously stated, including Westminster’s “good and necessary consequence”) from **sola scriptura**. The core argument is cumulative: given **what Scripture is** (God-breathed divine speech, so infallible) and **how it functions** among God’s people (other authorities are subordinate; Jesus and the apostles test tradition and even angels by the written word), there is no second entity that plausibly shares Scripture’s infallible status. Oral chains after the apostles fail the “telephone game”; post-apostolic claims of ecclesial infallibility lack Old and New Testament precedent, develop slowly in history, and create practical confusion. **Canon** questions are deferred to another video.

# Core Question

After the apostolic age, is there **any rule** for the church that is **infallible** in the same sense as **God’s speech in Scripture**—or is Scripture **alone** that kind of rule?

# Key Concepts

- **Sola scriptura** — only **infallible** rule, not “only authority” or “only explicit propositions.”
- **Infallibility vs. authority** — umpire-style binding but fallible decisions vs. incorrigible truth.
- **Theopneustos / God-breathed** — Scripture’s words as divine speech (2 Timothy 3:16; 2 Peter 1:21; Romans 3:2; Matthew 19; John 10:35).
- **Matthew 15 / Mark 7** — legitimate teachers, inflated tradition, and making the **word of God void** (with Exodus citations).
- **Bereans / Galatians 1:8** — testing preaching and even angels by the deposit already given.
- **Four challenges** to post-apostolic infallibility — no OT parallel, no NT blueprint, gradual historical development, messy track record in practice.

# Why It Matters

This is the **authority structure** behind what counts as **obligatory Christian belief** across communions. Ortlund frames it as the difference between binding doctrine grounded in **divine revelation** and **human accretion** papered over as equally sure—stakes that show up in disputes over tradition, magisterium, councils, and what may be required beyond Scripture.
