# Summary

Gavin Ortlund hosts Jordan Cooper and Michael Horton for a **Truth Units** conversation defending **Protestant** commitments around **Scripture**. They argue **sola scriptura** is **not** “Bible alone in a vacuum,” but the claim that **only Scripture** is the **infallible** rule for the church’s **faith and practice**. They contrast this with **Roman Catholic** teaching that pairs **Scripture and Holy Tradition** under the **magisterium**, and they contend that much turns on **equivocation** over **“tradition.”** They cite **patristic** support for **Scripture’s primacy**, use **preaching** and **canon** arguments to show how **Scripture** can be **normed** without requiring a single **infallible list** from the start, and trace **practical stakes** in **obligatory dogmas** (e.g. **Mariology**, **papacy**) and **doctrinal development**.

# Core Question

**Who or what ultimately defines what Christians must believe—Scripture alone as infallible, or Scripture together with other authorities that can bind the conscience—and how do we tell the difference between apostolic “tradition” and later binding teaching?**

# Key Concepts

- **Sola scriptura** as **primary infallible authority**, not **individualistic** “me and my Bible” in isolation.
- **Tradition** as a **multivalent** term (Chemnitz): **apostolic delivery**, **living magisterium**, and **patristic consensus** are not the same.
- **Catholic** model: **Scripture + tradition** as **Word of God**; **Protestant** worry about **subordination** of Scripture to **tradition-as-transmission**.
- **Ontological** distinction: **Scripture** is **God-breathed** in a way **tradition** is not; **preached word** as **means of grace** still **normed by Scripture**.
- **Doctrinal development** vs **apostolicity**: binding claims that appear **late** or **underdeveloped** in **patristic** and **biblical** evidence.
- **Canon objection**: **recognition** of inspired books by the **church** without **equating** “tradition on the canon” with **“whatever the magisterium says today.”**

# Why It Matters

The debate is not only academic: it concerns **what must be believed**, **what can be questioned**, and **where final appeal** lies when **church teaching**, **history**, and **Scripture** appear to **tension**. For Protestants and Catholics—and for anyone watching **conversion** and **apologetics** online—clarifying **definitions** and **authority** helps **map** honest disagreement and **avoid** talking past one another.
