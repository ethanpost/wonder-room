# Summary

James White argues that the Roman Catholic dogma of Mary’s Immaculate Conception is not derived from sound exegesis of the biblical texts usually cited (especially Luke 1:28–30 and related passages), but from church authority reading later theology back into Scripture. He contrasts **sola scriptura** with a functional **sola ecclesia**, surveys Greek lexical and grammatical points about **κεχαριτωμένη**, and extends the argument to Genesis 3:15, the Vulgate Latin tradition, and papal documents including *Ineffabilis Deus* (1854). The lecture also rehearses a historical narrative: alleged lack of patristic unanimity, medieval disagreement (including scholastic opposition), and 19th-century controversy—framed as evidence that the definition is a late development rather than apostolic teaching. The session concludes that binding **de fide** Marian dogma places Rome on the far side of a gospel-level divide over ultimate authority.

# Core Question

Does the Immaculate Conception arise from the plain sense of Scripture and continuous early tradition, or does it depend on ecclesiastical authority and “fittingness” reasoning that treats later theology as a lens through which biblical words must be read?

# Key Concepts

- **Immaculate Conception** (Mary conceived without original sin—not the virgin birth of Christ)
- **κεχαριτωμένη** and the **Ephesians 1:6** parallel (grace/favor vs. sinlessness)
- **Protoevangelium** (Genesis 3:15) and Latin **ipsa/ipsum** issues in Marian readings
- **Ludwig Ott** on what is / is not protected by infallibility (dogma vs. reasons)
- **Suitability / fittingness** arguments vs. Tertullian’s appeal to what God actually revealed
- **Historical development**: Eadmer, scholastic opposition, Dominican–Franciscan debates, 1854 definition
- **Vincentian** / **Schaff**-style arguments about universality and silence in the fathers

# Why It Matters

The lecture is framed as more than a technical Mariological dispute: it is about **authority** (whether Scripture or the magisterium ultimately controls interpretation), **method** (consistent hermeneutics vs. predetermined conclusions), and **gospel boundaries** (whether Marian dogmas defined as faith obligations are compatible with Protestant accounts of apostolic doctrine). For students of historical theology, it also illustrates how **patristic citations**, **medieval schools**, and **modern definitions** are enlisted in arguments over continuity and development.
