### Pope Francis, succession, and public conversation about Rome

- News context: Francis’s serious illness; past statements about resigning if unable to function; Benedict’s resignation as unusual in recent centuries.
- Expectation: renewed public/societal discussion of the papacy, Roman Catholicism, and a conclave.
- Francis’s influence on a future conclave described as “stacking the deck”: removing conservative cardinals, elevating figures aligned with liberation theology, changing voting procedures.
- Historical note: for the first thousand years the people of Rome elected the bishop of Rome; later the College of Cardinals; Reformation-era corruption (money changing hands) cited as historical background.

- **Thesis bridge:** Francis’s pontificate is used to argue that without **sola Scriptura**—Scripture as the sole infallible rule of faith—the church can reinterpret its past and teachings, unsettling many Catholics.

### Authority: sola Scriptura vs. “who decides?” (anti-pope rhetoric, final authority)

- Some Catholics reportedly treat Francis as an “anti-pope”; historical anti-popes removed from succession lists—**who decides** is traced, in this argument, to **Rome** itself.
- Two ultimate authority pictures contrasted:
  - **Protestant (speaker’s view):** *sola Scriptura*—Scripture the sole infallible rule of faith; confessions/catechisms are subordinate and correctable by Scripture.
  - **Roman Catholicism (as argued here):** ultimately **sola ecclesia**—the church as final authority even when “Scripture and tradition and magisterium” are affirmed verbally, because the magisterium defines canon, meaning of Scripture, and meaning of tradition.

### Biblical case for Scripture’s authority (Jesus and Peter)

- **Matthew and the Sadducees:** Jesus does not soften his correction (“You are wrong…”); grounds resurrection hope in Scripture (“Have you not read what was spoken to you by God…”), quoting Moses from centuries earlier—so when Scripture speaks, it is **God speaking**, with accountability across time.
- **2 Peter:** prophets did not originate Scripture by private interpretation; men spoke from God as carried along by the Holy Spirit—uniqueness of biblical authorship stressed.

### Apologetics context and literature

- Recent debates named: **Trent Horn** and **Jimmy Aken** (Catholic Answers) on *sola scriptura* and justification (2024); speaker mentions multiple debates over the years on *sola scriptura*.
- **Sola ecclesia** framed as what Catholic apologists allegedly avoid defending explicitly while attacking *sola scriptura*.
- Reference to *The Roman Catholic Controversy* (Bethany House) as a fuller treatment of Mary, *sola scriptura*, papacy, sacraments, Mass, etc.

### Ecumenical pressure: “fundamentals,” Machen, and “swimming the Tiber”

- **J. D. Vance** cited as a recent high-profile Protestant-to-Catholic convert (about six years Catholic) emphasizing “fundamentals” (Christ’s deity, incarnation, death, burial, resurrection).
- Contrast with liberal Protestant bodies (e.g., **ELCA** mentioned critically) and **J. Gresham Machen’s** *Christianity and Liberalism*: liberalism as a different religion that may borrow Christian symbols.
- Agreement granted: much more common ground with a **conservative believing Roman Catholic** than with a **left/liberal Protestant** on many moral/cultural issues—**but** the speaker argues that without knowing **doctrinal** fault lines, people minimize the Reformation divide.
- Metaphor: “**crossing the Tiber**” (conversion to Rome); many Protestants are said to “**paddle in the middle**”—not urging departure from Rome and tacitly conceding much of Rome’s side historically.

### Reformation history: Luther vs. Erasmus as the “hinge”

- Speaker identifies as professor of church history and apologetics at **Grace Bible Theological Seminary**.
- First **written** Reformation debate: **Martin Luther** (*The Bondage of the Will*) vs. **Desiderius Erasmus** (*The Freedom of the Will*)—Luther reportedly called it the issue opponents “put their finger upon.”
- Central issue: **bondage of the will** vs. freedom of the will; man dead in sin and dependent on grace vs. autonomous choosing—**soteriology** as the opening controversy.
- Pastoral claim: most contemporary “Protestants” (and many Lutherans) would side with **Erasmus** against Luther today.

### Main thesis: Rome’s official gospel and the absence of **shalom**

- Thesis of *The Roman Catholic Controversy* repeated: all the disputed topics (sacraments, priesthood, councils, apocrypha, etc.) matter because they converge on whether Rome’s **official gospel** can give **peace**.
- Internal diversity among Catholics (different schools, regions) acknowledged; target is **official teaching** on the gospel.
- **Mitchell Pacwa** debate (January 1991, San Diego) on justification: setup using greatest commandment (love God wholly)—we have not done it even today.
- Roman framework summarized: **venial** sin stains but does not destroy the grace of justification; **mortal** sin destroys the state of grace and makes one God’s enemy; dying in mortal sin means hell (with a side remark that Francis may not personally believe the traditional formulation).
- **Shalom** explained as positive well-being and right relationship, not mere ceasefire—then the question: if mortal sin is possible before sleep, how can one claim **true shalom** with God tonight?
- **Ludwig Ott**, *Fundamentals of Catholic Dogma* (on justification): uncertainty about the state of grace without special revelation; presumption charged against assured eternal life—because one must **fulfill conditions** to **achieve** justification through sacramental access.
- Contrast stated: Rome = you must fulfill conditions; Bible = **Christ fulfilled all conditions in your place**.
- **Romans 5:1** anchor: justified by faith → peace with God through Christ; Greek *eirene* / Hebrew *shalom* tied to Paul’s thought.

### Romans 3–4 as the diagnostic text

- **Romans 3** (especially the indictment that “every mouth may be shut”): law silencing self-righteous excuses; knowledge of sin; **not justified by works of the law**.
- **Romans 4** recommended as the clearest illustration of biblical vs. Roman gospel differences for conversation partners.
- **Genesis 15:6** / **Romans 4:3**: Abraham believed God, counted for righteousness—cited repeatedly in NT; imputation language.
- **Romans 4:4–5** contrast:
  - To the one who **works**, the reward is not grace but **debt** owed—God cannot owe saving grace.
  - To the one who **does not work** but believes on him who **justifies the ungodly**, faith is counted as righteousness—**saving faith** trusts the God who declares the unrighteous righteous.
- **Joseph Smith’s** “Inspired Version” cited as changing “justifies the ungodly” to **not** justifying the ungodly—held up as a stark misunderstanding still reflected in LDS Bible footnotes (as stated).
- Speaker’s book title **The God Who Justifies** tied to this phrase.

### Romans 4:6–8 and Psalm 32: righteousness “apart from works” = forgiveness

- Paul says David speaks of blessing on the one to whom God counts righteousness **apart from works**, then quotes **Psalm 32** (blessedness of forgiven/covered sin; sin not imputed).
- Argument: forgiveness/non-imputation of sin **is** what Paul means by the counted righteousness apart from works—leading to substitution: sins imputed to another (**sin-bearer**) so they are not imputed to the believer.

### “Blessed man” of Romans 4:8 vs. venial/mortal scheme

- Question posed to Catholics: are you the **blessed man** whose sin the Lord will **not** impute?
- Under venial sin: still punishment, confession, penance, possible purgatory—sin still “counted” in some sense.
- Under mortal sin: loss of grace, need for sacramental restoration—sin imputed in a decisive way.
- Rome’s only consistent “blessed man” moment (as argued): immediately after baptism before further sin—versus Paul’s blessed man as **every** genuine believer in Christ.

### Luther’s “dung heap / snow” illustration and a Catholic counter-caricature

- **Luther** (attribution uncertain): sinners as a **dung hill**; **Christ’s righteousness** like **snow** covering the offense before God’s sight without claiming intrinsic transformation equals justification (**alien righteousness**); **sanctification** as gradual change of the “dung hill,” never the ground of acceptance.
- Rome said to “hate” the illustration; Catholic apologists attack it as mere “legal fiction.”
- Speaker’s reversal caricature: Rome **baptizes** the dung into **gold**—inherent pleasingness; venial sin as flecks on gold needing penance/purgatory; mortal sin reverting the pile to dung—hence uncertainty (**Ott** quoted again).
- Huge difference claimed: heaven because **intrinsic change** vs. heaven because **Christ’s righteousness alone** as plea.

### Galatians, politics, and pastoral close

- **Galatians 2:4**: false brothers; Paul would not yield so the **truth of the gospel** might remain—Judaizers were orthodox on Christ’s person/resurrection but twisted the gospel (speaker’s framing).
- Political application: temptation to mute justification controversies for a “winning coalition” (e.g., speculation about **J. D. Vance** in **2028**); rebuttal that political coalitions do not save; kingdom advance is by gospel transformation of hearts.
- No compromise possible if one loves Catholics and believes Rome’s system lacks peace—without being unkind personally.
- Challenge: be ready to explain **why** you have peace (clothed in Christ’s righteousness); seek opportunities to speak; prayer for those bound by family/tradition who lack peace.

## Core Ideas

- Public focus on papal illness/succession is a practical opening to discuss authority and the gospel with Catholics and neighbors.
- *Sola Scriptura* vs. magisterial final authority is framed as the underlying epistemological dispute behind many surface debates.
- The Reformation’s first great published clash (Luther–Erasmus) centered on **human ability and grace**—soteriology—not only on the papacy or Mary.
- Speaker’s organizing claim: Rome’s official teaching ties justification to **fulfilling conditions** accessible through the church’s sacramental system, producing **uncertainty** about peace with God; Paul ties **peace** to **justification by faith** apart from works.
- **Romans 4:4–5** and **4:6–8** are presented as the clearest biblical pattern: God justifies the ungodly and counts righteousness apart from works by not imputing sin—based on Christ’s work imputed to believers.

## Key Terms

- **Sola Scriptura** — Scripture as the sole infallible rule of faith (with subordinate secondary standards).
- **Sola ecclesia** — label used here for the church as final authority over Scripture and tradition in practice.
- **Magisterium** — teaching authority said to define canon, Scripture’s meaning, and tradition’s meaning.
- **Venial sin / mortal sin** — distinction used in the sermon to discuss loss of grace and imputation.
- **Shalom / eirene** — peace as positive relationship, not only absence of conflict.
- **State of grace** — standing before God in Roman Catholic theology as discussed here.
- **Presumption (sin of)** — in this context, allegedly charging assured salvation as presumptuous without special revelation.
- **Imputation** — crediting righteousness or not imputing sin; central to Romans 4 exposition.
- **Alien righteousness** — Christ’s righteousness as the believer’s plea, external to inherent moral quality.
- **Sanctification** — progressive holiness distinguished from justification in the Luther illustration.
