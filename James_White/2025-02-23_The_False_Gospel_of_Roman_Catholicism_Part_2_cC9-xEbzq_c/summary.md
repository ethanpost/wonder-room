# Summary

James White continues a sermon series by tying headlines about Pope Francis and a possible conclave to the authority question: without Scripture as the sole infallible rule of faith, a church can reinterpret its past. He contrasts *sola Scriptura* with what he calls *sola ecclesia* (the magisterium as final arbiter of Scripture and tradition). The bulk of the message is an exposition of **Romans 4–5**: peace with God follows justification by faith; **Romans 4:4–5** opposes “working” for a wage with believing on the God who **justifies the ungodly**; **Romans 4:6–8** links “righteousness apart from works” to forgiveness and non-imputation of sin (Psalm 32). Against Roman Catholic distinctions between venial and mortal sin and the uncertainty of the state of grace (quoting **Ludwig Ott**), he argues Rome’s official scheme cannot yield the **shalom** Paul describes, and he closes with pastoral urgency about speaking the gospel plainly despite political ecumenical pressure.

# Core Question

Can the gospel that makes **peace with God** tonight be the same gospel that ties final acceptance to **fulfilling conditions** you cannot be sure you have met—and that still **imputes** sin in cases Rome classifies as venial?

# Key Concepts

- **Sola Scriptura** vs. magisterial finality (*sola ecclesia* as labeled here)
- **Justification by faith** and **Romans 5:1** (*eirene* / *shalom*)
- **Venial vs. mortal sin** in relation to peace and imputation
- **Romans 4:4–5** (wage/debt vs. grace; **justifies the ungodly**)
- **Romans 4:6–8** / **Psalm 32** (righteousness apart from works = forgiveness/non-imputation)
- **Luther’s** dung-hill/snow illustration vs. the speaker’s **baptism-to-gold** counter-analogy and **purgatory**
- **Luther–Erasmus** debate as the early Reformation’s soteriological hinge

# Why It Matters

The sermon claims that cultural and political incentives to mute Reformation disputes (e.g., shared conservatism with some Catholics, high-profile converts) can trade away the **truth of the gospel** Paul defends in Romans and Galatians. For listeners, the practical stakes are pastoral and evangelistic: whether assurance of peace rests on **Christ’s finished work imputed** or on **sacramental systems** described as intrinsically uncertain—and whether friends and neighbors hear that distinction clearly.
