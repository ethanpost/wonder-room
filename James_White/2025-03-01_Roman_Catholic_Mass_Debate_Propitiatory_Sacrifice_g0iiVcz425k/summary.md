# Summary

Catholic Answers apologist **Joe Heschmeyer** and Reformed Baptist **James White** debate whether the Roman Catholic **Mass** is a **propitiatory sacrifice**: the same class of issue as **atonement**, **priesthood**, and **assurance**. Heschmeyer builds a case from **Reformation-era admissions of consensus**, **Old and New Testament** texts (especially **Malachi 1** and **1 Corinthians 10–11**), **sub–AD 200** Christian practice, and **Passover-style** logic (slaughter plus **eating** applies the sacrifice). White centers **Hebrews**: **one offering** that **perfects**, Christ’s **finished** work and **heavenly** presentation, and the distinction between **remembrance** of the sin-bearer and a **repeated** propitiatory rite mediated by a **sacerdotal** priesthood. Cross-examination drills **Hebrews 9–10**, **covenant blood** typology (**Exodus 24** / Last Supper), **John O’Brien**, **Eastern Orthodox** parallels, **development of doctrine**, and closing rounds connect the liturgical question to **justification**, **peace with God**, and **indulgences** in Q&A.

# Core Question

Is the Eucharist, as Rome teaches it through **ordained priests**, a **true and proper propitiatory sacrifice** for the living and the dead—**perpetuating** Christ’s cross in an **unbloody** mode—or is propitiation **complete** on Calvary and **applied** by the Spirit through **faith**, with the Supper as **non-propitiatory memorial**?

# Key Concepts

- **Propitiation** versus other kinds of “sacrifice” (praise, thanks, dedication).
- **Once for all** in **Hebrews** versus **ongoing participation** models.
- **In persona Christi** / **alter Christus** and the **ministerial** priesthood.
- **Anamnesis** (memorial) read either as **memorial sacrifice** language or as **remembrance** of a finished offering.
- **Patristic and liturgical** witness (**Didache**, **Justin**, **Cyril of Jerusalem**, **Gregory Dix**) versus **sola Scriptura** exegesis and worries about **reading Trent back** into the fathers.

# Why It Matters

How one answers ties directly to **what the gospel is**: whether Christ’s **bearing of wrath** is **complete** for the believer, what grounds **assurance** and **peace with God**, and how **worship** relates to **forgiveness** across time—including care for **Catholic–Protestant** understanding in families, parishes, and public apologetics.
