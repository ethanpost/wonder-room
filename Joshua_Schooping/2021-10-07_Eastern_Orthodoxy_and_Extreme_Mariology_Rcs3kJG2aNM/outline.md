# Eastern Orthodoxy and Extreme Mariology

## Metadata

- Author: Pastor Joshua Schooping
- Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Rcs3kJG2aNM
- Date: 2021-10-07
- Folder: Joshua_Schooping

## Detailed Outline

### Aims and audience

- The talk continues a series on **Eastern Orthodoxy for evangelicals**, framed as **edifying and clarifying**.
- The speaker acknowledges that Eastern Orthodox viewers may find some topics **tender**; he distinguishes what **may not be known** to some Orthodox, what may be **explained differently**, and what apologists sometimes **defer or ignore**—calling some issues an **elephant in the room**.
- **Cradle Orthodox** are often described as **uncatechized**, exposed to **impressions** in church life that are **not always tied to formal Orthodox theology**; sometimes the discussion concerns **popular impressions** versus **canonical** positions, and sometimes **canonical** teaching that is **not clearly known** to many English-speaking Orthodox.

### Context: post-liturgy thanksgiving and the Theotokos prayer

- Focus: a **formal, official prayer to the Most Holy Theotokos** that appears among **thanksgiving prayers after the Divine Liturgy**, taken from a standard **Orthodox service book** (any liturgy book from Orthodox publishers).
- After the liturgy, **thanksgiving prayers** are often read **aloud** in church (by a reader or a pious member); in **more observant** parishes many stay to hear them; some leave and read at home or not at all.
- The speaker encourages Orthodox hearers to **read and reflect** on these prayers; the Theotokos prayer is **penultimate or last** in that set.

### Theotokos, Christotokos, and “most holy”

- For evangelicals: **Theotokos** means **God-bearer**—Mary bore not only a human nature but the **divine person**, Jesus the Word/Son; one may also say **Christotokos** (Christ-bearer) and **Theotokos** (God-bearer); **Mother of God** reflects this Greek/Latin background (e.g. “Holy Mary, Mother of God”).
- **Most holy** is explained as tied to **God’s action and design**, not Mary’s **independent** holiness: no creature or angel **bore God’s person**; angels delivered **word**, not **person**.
- Mary is **full of grace** (Scripture); grace **accounts righteous**—she is a **special vessel set aside** in God’s foreknowledge of all history **without wavering**.
- **Holiness** here is about **purpose** (bearing the Son), not claiming she is **all holiness in herself**; all holiness is **by grace**, as with **Jeremiah** set apart from the womb and **John the Baptist** leaping in the womb—**holy purpose**, especially emphasized in **ancient churches** (East and Rome).

### Where Mariology becomes problematic (speaker’s thesis)

- Trouble arises when reflection on Mary **blurs creaturehood** with **divine attributes**; the speaker predicts Orthodox theologians would **deny** that blur, but he argues **liturgical wording** and **how it lands on hearers** (“**liturgical phenomenology**”) still **sound** as if divine prerogatives are ascribed to Mary.
- Many Orthodox say the **liturgy** is sufficient to learn Christianity; therefore **what the liturgy sounds like** matters.
- Examples of **confusing vocabulary**: **mediatrix** alongside Scripture’s **one mediator, Christ**; uncatechized hearers may not grasp nuanced distinctions. **“Mary saves”** appears in liturgy; theologians may qualify this as **situational** deliverance, not **soul-salvation** like Christ’s—but the average hearer may not ask **which sense** is meant.

### Line-by-line concerns in the thanksgiving prayer

- **“Light of my darkened soul”**: soul **darkened by sin**; the prayer names **Mary** as **light**, not (in this line) Christ as the one who enlightens—risk of **ascribing divine/light-giving roles** to Mary.
- **Hope, protection, refuge, consolation, joy**: the speaker ties **joy** to **Holy Spirit fruit** (“righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit”); **consolation** evokes the **Comforter**; **refuge/protection** language he associates with **looking to God**, not a creature—**at best confusing**, at worst **replacing** God as protector.
- **“I thank thee… that thou hast vouchsafed me… to be a partaker of… thy Son’s body and blood”**: Mary is thanked as **vouchsafing** communion; the speaker argues **only God** directly **gives divine life**; instruments may be used, but here Mary is portrayed as **presently enabling** access in a way that **parallels** divine action—he calls this a **first-commandment** concern (**no other** beside God’s face; **no created power** shares **God’s unique attributes**).
- **“Gave us birth to the true light”** (Christ): fairly stated; but **“do thou… enlighten the spiritual eyes of my heart”** asks Mary to **enlighten**—not framed as mere “save from fire” but **interior illumination**, **after** communion, which can sound as if **Christ’s gift were insufficient**.
- **“Gave us birth to the source of immortality”** (again fairly about Christ’s birth); then **“revive me who am dead in sin”**—**spiritual** death/sin; only **God** revives the spiritually dead (contrast with **Elijah** and natural life, **Holy Spirit** through prophets).
- **“Lovingly compassionate mother of the merciful God, have mercy on me”**: **mercy** sought **from Mary**; in modern English **prayer** often implies **God-directed adoration**; older English **“pray”** could mean **request** generally—the speaker notes **theological** usage today ties **prayer** to **God alone** and **adoration**.
- **Orthodox veneration vs worship**: apologists distinguish **dulia**/**hyperdulia** from **latria**, but the speaker says the distinction is **not clearly communicated** to the uncatechized and **not formally marked** in the liturgy—so **phenomenologically** Mary can **sound** **confused with God**.
- **Grant compunction, contrition, humility, recall of thoughts from captivity**: these are graces the speaker says Christians should seek **from God alone**; **renewal of mind** is **in Christ**, not by seeking a **“third person”** besides Christ for what is **already ours in him** by faith.
- **“Vouchsafe me until my last breath… without condemnation… healing… grant me tears of repentance and confession”**—again **Mary** as **granter** of repentance-related gifts.
- **“Glorify thee all the days of my life”** / **“blessed and most glorified art thou unto the ages”**: **glory** may have wider lexical range in original languages, but **in context** the speaker sees **hyper-veneration** bordering on treating Mary as **share** in what belongs to **God alone**—he ends by naming **hyper-adoration**, **glorification**, and **deification of Mary** as the recurring problem.

### Closing: honoring Mary without excess

- The speaker affirms **appropriate honor**: **blessed among women**, **full of grace**, **not derogatory** speech; fellow Christians in grace may be spoken of similarly, with Mary’s **added** promise that **all generations** will call her **blessed**.
- **Calling her blessed** is **far from** asking her to **grant mercy, compunction, contrition, humility,** and **release from spiritual captivity**—those require **Christ** and the **Spirit** (“breathe on these dead bones”).

## Core Ideas

- **Liturgical phenomenology**: how liturgical language **sounds and forms** the faithful, especially when theology assumes **catechesis** many do not have.
- **Theotokos** as **God-bearer** is **Chalcedonian-compatible** in intent; **“most holy”** can be read as **vocation and grace**, not creaturely **autonomous** holiness.
- Tension between **official qualifiers** (e.g. **situational** “salvation” by Mary’s intercession) and **prayers** that use **divine-sounding** verbs and titles for Mary.
- **Protestant concern**: **first commandment** and **unique divine prerogatives**—**life, light, mercy, repentance, glory**—should not be **ascribed** to creatures in ways that **parallel** God.
- **Balance**: **honor Mary** per Scripture **without** requests that imply **she** **bestows** what only **Trinity/Christ/Spirit** **give**.
