# Summary

Joshua Schooping walks through a standard **post-Liturgy thanksgiving prayer to the Most Holy Theotokos**, explaining **Theotokos** in a Chalcedonian-friendly way (Mary bore the **divine person**), then arguing that many lines **sound** as if **divine prerogatives**—light, hope, refuge, **vouchsafing** communion, **enlightening** the heart after communion, **reviving** the **dead in sin**, **mercy**, **granting** repentance, and **glory**—are **ascribed to Mary**. He grants that theologians may qualify phrases like **“Mary saves,”** but stresses **liturgical phenomenology** for **uncatechized** hearers and ends by affirming **honor** for Mary as **blessed** and **full of grace** without those **petitions**.

# Core Question

When Orthodox apologists distinguish **veneration** from **worship** and **situational** help from **Christ’s unique salvation**, do the **actual words** of widely used **liturgical** prayers **communicate** those distinctions to ordinary believers—or do they **blur** **creature** and **Creator** in practice?

# Key Concepts

- **Theotokos / Christotokos** and **Mother of God** as titles about **who** was born, not Mary’s **independent** deity
- **Liturgical phenomenology**: how **fixed prayers** **shape** hearers beyond technical footnotes
- **Mediatrix**, **“Mary saves,”** and other terms whose **nuanced** senses may not reach the **pew**
- **First-commandment** logic: **God alone** **gives** **divine life** and **spiritual** resurrection from **sin**
- **Appropriate honor** (**blessed among women**) versus **requests** that mirror **God-only** **gifts**

# Why It Matters

For **evangelicals** dialoguing with **Eastern Orthodoxy**, the talk names a **concrete** **flashpoint**: not abstract Mariology alone, but **authorized** **texts** repeated after **every** Liturgy. For **pastoral** and **ecumenical** clarity, it raises how **traditions** **teach** what **worship**, **prayer**, and **salvation** language **mean** when **Mary** receives **verbs** parallel to those addressed to **God**—and how **both** sides might **speak** of Mary **faithfully** without **talking past** each other.
