## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and stated aim

- Schooping introduces the video as a Protestant evangelical reflection on Eastern Orthodox iconology (theory and religious practice of icons).
- He aims to be measured rather than rude; he disagrees with icon theory and will give reasons, while welcoming Orthodox viewers and noting appreciation for Eastern Orthodoxy and the Fathers from his own Orthodox background.
- Intended audience: evangelical and Protestant Christians.

### Orthodox defenses of icons (as he summarizes them)

- **Incarnation and matter:** A common argument ties icon use to the Incarnation: the Son took flesh; matter can now represent God—not the divine nature in abstraction, but the person who assumed human nature. Phrases used include God made visible, Christ having purified/restored matter, and “matter matters.”
- **John of Damascus (8th c.) and Theodore the Studite (early 9th c.):** Cited as classic defenders. Schooping quotes Damascene (second treatise, p. 76 in his edition): Moses forbade images because of Israel’s hardness of heart and tendency toward idolatry, but now believers stand securely on faith. Schooping objects that ascribing the image prohibition mainly to Moses and hardness of heart conflicts with God writing the second commandment with his own finger—not merely a concession to Israel’s heart.
- **Seventh Ecumenical Council and tradition:** He notes claims (e.g., in NPNF2 vol. 14 on the seven councils) that Nicaea II confirmed an ancient, venerable tradition of images.
- **Semiotics / “honor passes to the prototype”:** A principal defense: veneration given to the image passes to the one depicted—bowing or kissing an image of Christ is treated as truly directed to Christ.

### Critical distinction: religious art vs. Orthodox iconology

- He distinguishes **religiously themed visual art** (illustrations, educational pictures, coloring books) from **Orthodox iconology**, which for him necessarily includes **religious usage**: bowing, saluting, kissing icons, decorating them, processions, etc.
- His critique targets veneration of icons as religious acts toward images, not mere possession of Christian artwork.

### Origen, Celsus, and ancient awareness of “representation” arguments

- He engages *Contra Celsum* (Chadwick translation) to show pagans already argued they did not think stone or wood *were* gods but offered votive worship that passed to the deity—i.e., a semiotic theory of images.
- Origen rejects praying to images and “pretending” to accommodate the multitude (criticizing Peripatetics, Epicureans, etc.); piety should not be insincere; honoring images risks treating them as other gods.
- Schooping uses this to argue that ancient people were not “too dumb” to understand representation; the semiotic defense was known and rejected by Origen.

### The Philocalia (Gregory of Nazianzus and Basil)

- The Philocalia collects selected Origen, compiled by Gregory Nazianzen and Basil—two of the three hierarchs (with John Chrysostom).
- Schooping quotes Philocalia material (p. 83 in his reference) selected by Basil and Gregory: corrupting teaching implants belief that images are gods or that gold/silver/ivory/stone are worthy of worship; common sense forbids thinking God is corruptible matter or honored when fashioned in dead matter; pictorial/symbolic representation of God is foolish; images are not gods; such art is not to be compared to the Creator.
- He argues this shows **aniconism** (rejection of religious image use), not merely iconoclasm (destroying images), in early centuries and undercuts Damascene/Theodore and claims that icon veneration was the early patristic consensus.

### Historical narrative: Jay Duma and later development of image worship

- Cites Jay Duma, *Writing on the Ten Commandments* (P&R), p. 59: early church vehemently opposed image worship in struggle with paganism; pagans said they worshiped not the image but what it represented—Augustine found that unacceptable; Synod of Elvira prohibited images and murals in churches.
- Duma’s narrative: after Christianity became state religion, pagan patterns persisted among common people; **religious use** of images appears after the 4th century (parallel shifts: Minerva to Mary, Apollo to Sebastian, etc.); miracles attributed to images as with pagan cult images; “not made by hands” themes paralleled.
- Pope Gregory I (late 6th / early 7th c.) praised Bishop Serenus of Marseilles for zeal against image **worship** but said destruction of images was not required—distinction between forbidding worship vs. destroying art.
- Dura-Europos and OT examples: religious imagery can exist, but when veneration is directed at the physical object (even with semiotic intent), it becomes inappropriate.
- Conclusion from this strand: claiming uninterrupted early “consensus” for icon **veneration** is false.

### Excursus: eschatology and “consensus of the Fathers”

- Schooping argues that appeals to unchanging patristic consensus are problematic for icons; he adds **premillennialism** as another area where pre-/Nicene consensus differed from later Orthodoxy.
- Summarizes Paul King’s essay in *Essays on Premillennialism* and other scholars (McGiffert on Eusebius, Daniélou, Papias, Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, etc.) to claim widespread early chiliasm through the 3rd century and possibly reflected at Nicaea in “come again to judge.”
- If “church unity” is tied strictly to Nicene authors’ intent, he suggests inconsistency when modern Orthodoxy does not adopt premillennialism.

### Sects, unity, and Origen again (Philocalia ch. 16)

- Origen compares Christian “sects” to medical schools—serious teachers, different remedies—not necessarily schismatic factiousness; cites 1 Cor 11:19 (“heresies”/factions) as manifesting the approved.
- Philocalia ch. 16 repeats this: sects arise when a matter is grave; Judaism had sects from varied interpretation of Moses/prophets; Christianity drew learned Greeks; different interpretations of divine utterances produced named schools—not always mere contentiousness.
- Application: Orthodox denial of any legitimate diversity within Christianity vs. Basil/Gregory’s inclusion of this Origen passage; Protestant Reformers as re-emergence of serious disagreement on non-core points.

### Seventh Ecumenical Council: anathemas and stakes

- Schooping reads Nicaea II’s letter to emperor/empress (p. 571 in his edition): those who do not believe in venerating, kissing, bowing to icons, who debate further or are ill-disposed toward such veneration, are **anathematized** by the Spirit-authorized council.
- **Definition quoted:** “Anathema is nothing less than complete separation from God.”
- Further phrases: driven from the church; iconoclasts/aniconists as revilers; salute (kiss/bow to) images—those who do not are anathematized; calling sacred images idols draws anathema; “fires of future Gehenna” (p. 32); “god-hated heretics” (session 6 extracts, p. 542).
- Canon 13: excommunication as condemned by Father, Son, and Spirit, assigned where worm dies not and fire not quenched.
- He argues this shows maximalist ecclesiology: refusal to venerate images is treated as damning, not a secondary opinion.

### Revelation 22:8–9 and proskynesis

- John falls to proskynēsai (KJV “worship”; Greek proskyneō) before the angel; the angel forbids it—John is fellow servant with prophets and those who keep the book; **worship God**.
- Schooping notes the council admits proskyneō can mean worship or veneration; he argues the angel still refuses the action John intended toward a creature he knew was not God.
- Implication: no proskynesis toward angels → by extension not toward saints; Mary as doule (servant) should not be elevated beyond Scripture; doubly wrong to venerate **images** of them.
- Contrasts latria vs. proskynesis distinction as finer than Scripture warrants in light of Rev 22.

### Second commandment, matter, and Old Testament theophanies

- Duma cited: Yahweh appeared in human form to Abraham, Jacob at Peniel, elders saw God, prophets saw human-like visions—yet Scripture never uses that to justify making and bowing to images of God.
- Schooping: matter was created good; Incarnation does not retroactively rewrite the second commandment; bowing to created matter is universal “language” of worship and enslaves; God gave matter to man to rule, not man to matter.

### Council text on kissing and “strong desire” toward icons

- He quotes council material defining reverence of cross, spear, images as salutation shown by **touching with lips** (kissing); proskynesis as word of many significations; kinēin + pros implying strong desire/affection toward the object—icons are to receive salutation and strong love.

### Basil’s “honor to image passes to archetype” (closing caveat)

- Schooping claims the common appeal to Basil’s argument about honor to an image of the emperor passing to the emperor is **misapplied** to created cult icons of Christ; Basil’s work in the Philocalia from Origen shows he would not endorse Damascene-style icon veneration as usually argued.

### Concluding pastoral note

- Mentions the Synodikon’s anathemas against those who do not worship Christ in icons “according to his humanity.”
- Closes with evangelical soteriology: Christ alone, substitutionary atonement, free gift—no need to venerate/bow/kiss objects to receive union with God.

## Core Ideas

- Orthodox iconology is not reducible to Christian art; it involves prescribed **religious acts** toward holy images.
- Main Orthodox rationales surveyed: Incarnation/matter, patristic/council authority, and semiotic veneration (honor to prototype).
- Schooping’s historical-theological thesis: early Fathers and texts he cites (Origen via Philocalia, pre-4th-century practice, Elvira, patristic objections to “we worship the prototype”) undermine the claim that icon **veneration** was the ancient consensus.
- Nicaea II’s language ties refusal of icon veneration to **anathema** defined as separation from God and Gehenna—high ecclesiological and soteriological stakes.
- Rev 22 is used to argue that creature-directed proskynesis is forbidden even when the creature is not confused with God.
- Matter/theophanies do not, in his reading, overturn the simplicity of the second commandment’s prohibition.

## Key Terms

- **Iconology:** Theory and (for Orthodoxy) liturgical/religious practice regarding holy icons.
- **Aniconism / iconoclasm:** Rejection of religious image use vs. destruction of images (he stresses aniconism in early sources).
- **Proskynesis / proskyneō:** Greek term for bowing/veneration or worship; central to council discourse and Rev 22.
- **Latria vs. dulia/proskynesis:** Distinction between worship due God alone and honor to creatures/images (he critiques the distinction’s sufficiency).
- **Semiotics (honor passes to prototype):** Defense that acts toward the image refer to the person depicted.
- **Seventh Ecumenical Council (Nicaea II, 787):** Council defending icons; source of the anathema material he quotes.
- **Philocalia:** Anthology of Origen edited by Basil and Gregory Nazianzen.

## Expected Outcome

After taking notes from this outline and watching or listening to the source material, an **A+ student** would be capable of:

### Knowing

- State Schooping’s distinction between religious-themed art and Orthodox **icon veneration** (bowing, kissing, processions).
- Summarize the standard Orthodox arguments he lists: Incarnation and matter, Damascene on Moses and images, council/tradition claims, and semiotic “honor to the prototype.”
- Explain his objection to Damascene’s reading of the second commandment as mainly Mosaic accommodation to hardness of heart.
- Describe Origen’s and Celsus’s exchange as evidence that ancient pagans already used a **representation** defense of image worship.
- Identify the **Philocalia** and why Gregory and Basil’s inclusion of Origen’s anti-image material matters for his argument.
- Outline Jay Duma’s narrative: early opposition to image worship, Elvira, shift after Constantinian era, parallel pagan-Christian devotional patterns.
- Recall how Pope Gregory I’s letter to Serenus distinguishes opposing **worship** of images from destroying them.
- Summarize Schooping’s use of **Paul King** and others on early **premillennialism** as a parallel “consensus” problem.
- Reproduce his reading of **Nicaea II** texts: anathema defined as complete separation from God, Gehenna language, canon 13.
- Explain the **Revelation 22:8–9** argument: angel refuses proskynesis though John knew the angel was not God.
- State his view on matter, OT theophanies, and why Incarnation does not, for him, negate the second commandment’s simplicity.
- Note his critique of applying Basil’s imperial-image analogy to icons of Christ.

### Conversing

- Debate whether Philocalia selections prove Basil/Gregory’s **personal** later views on icons or only their editorial judgment on Origen.

- Discuss historical causation: does post-4th-century image piety show continuity with paganism, legitimate development, or something else?
- Evaluate whether maximalist anathema language at Nicaea II is normative for contemporary Orthodox pastoral practice or requires contextual qualification.
