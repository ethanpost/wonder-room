## Detailed Outline

### Introduction and audience

- Schooping returns after a hiatus to Eastern Orthodoxy, drawing on his experience as a former Eastern Orthodox priest.
- He frames Eastern Orthodoxy as a “false system” and “false gospel” in his view, while acknowledging converts may encounter well-meaning people who gradually move evangelicals toward full acceptance.
- He warns that individual priests may say they would never “make” anyone bow or kiss an icon, but argues that formal church teaching—not casual pastoral rhetoric—is what matters.

### Thesis: three-way distinction (aniconism vs. iconoclasm vs. iconodulia)

- **Iconoclasm (not his position):** Destroying or forbidding imagery in all forms—e.g., no illustrated Bibles or coloring books. He explicitly disclaims arguing for this in the present talk.
- **Aniconism (his position):** Rejection of the *religious use* of images in worship (without necessarily demanding destruction of all art).
- **Iconodulia (as defined by the Seventh Ecumenical Council):** Formal teaching that refusal to worship/venerate icons—bowing, kissing, strong affection—places one under anathema. He plans to cite Nicaea II’s language.

### Three main argumentative strands (preview)

1. **Innovation vs. early consensus:** Seventh Ecumenical Council iconodulia breaks with what was “believed everywhere, always, by all” in the sense Vincent of Lérins describes (lowercase-o orthodoxy as descriptive consensus).
2. **Scripture:** Brief pointer to God/Christ in heaven and Psalm 115’s critique of idols (mouths that do not speak, etc.)—religious orientation through such images distorts the soul’s ordering toward God.
3. **Gospel confusion:** The council’s anathemas, in his reading, make salvation depend on venerating material images—contrary to sola fide / grace alone / Christ alone as he states at the close.

### Primary historical source: Moshe Barasch, *History of Icons* (NYU Press)

- Barasch is presented as relatively neutral scholarship surveying icon history.
- **Porphyry (late 3rd c.):** In material preserved in fragments (*On Images*, p. 60 in Barasch), only the “uneducated” identify gods with images—educated pagans already used a referential/semiotic theory: veneration passes through the image to the deity, not crude identification of statue with god.
- **Image as “text” for the illiterate:** Around p. 81 in Barasch, resemblance and comparison of statues/paintings to written texts—another line Orthodox apologists use, which Schooping traces to pagan argument Christians had long rejected.
- **Porphyry, *Against the Christians*:** Proper worshipers do not believe the god is in wood/stone/bronze; temples/images serve as reminders; honor shown through a portrait parallels honor to a friend—again, sophisticated referential veneration, not naive idol-as-deity.

### Iamblichus, Proclus, and Neoplatonic matter

- **Iamblichus (early 4th c.):** Matter is not evil but creation; material divine images need not lack validity/power. Schooping connects this to later Christian arguments that incarnation “purified” matter so Christ may be depicted.
- Ambiguity in Iamblichus: alongside semiotic language, a “real link” between image and god remains—raises questions for iconodulia about *how* honor passes and why “dead” saints receive parallel veneration (mentioned briefly).
- **Proclus:** Barasch (as cited) suggests the internal development of “resemblance” peaks with Proclus; icon-defenders’ arguments in the iconoclastic controversy are “unthinkable” without that heritage—dependence on Neoplatonic semiotic theory.

### Early Christian apologists: unified rejection (Barasch, pp. 96, 100)

- First Christian encounter with divine images described as rejection.
- Barasch lists at least five: Aristides of Athens (~AD 140), Justin Martyr, Tatian, the *Letter to Diognetus*, Athenagoras of Athens.
- Claim: between AD 140–180, no document Barasch surveys deviates from basic rejection; reading apologists offers no statement obliging us to qualify “Christians of the time rejected sacred images.”
- Schooping warns against mislabeling this as “iconoclasm” in the destructive sense—it is aniconism; misclassification yields false positives for iconodulia.

### Third century: Tertullian, Syriac *Didascalia*, pseudo-Clementine orders

- **Tertullian:** Barasch finds hard to maintain a sharp split between his total condemnation of idols and tacit permission of other images; absence of explicit condemnation of non-idol images should not imply approval—witness to non-use of religious imagery. Tertullian may tilt toward extreme iconoclasm, but still fits a broader early pattern unsupportive of icons.
- **Syriac *Didascalia* (3rd c.):** “No oblations may be received from those who paint with colors… who make idols or workers in gold, silver and bronze”—regional church discipline excluding such artisans from offering, implying no place for painted iconic worship in those communities.
- **Pseudo-Clementine church order:** Painter grouped with harlot, brothel-keeper, drunkard, actor, athlete—if icon painting were legitimate Christian practice, this list would be incoherent; attitude likely widespread, with Tertullian giving theoretical formulation.
- **Heretic example (transcript unclear):** A heretic is cited as condemned both for false doctrine and for practicing the “forbidden” art of painting—painting itself widely known as forbidden.

### Tertullian on art’s social effect

- Statue’s intrinsic meaning aside, its effect on spectators is essential; art tends toward idolatry given fallen human nature—observed, he claims, in dubious Orthodox church practices.
- Etymology chain (Greek *ethos* / *eidolon*): idolatry as attendance and service around every “form”—bowing, kissing, worship through images, including toward angels/saints (worse in his view).
- Heart as “idol factory”; prohibition extends to *making* images, not only using them—making already breaches the second commandment.

### Eusebius and the letter to Constantia

- Eusebius mentions artistic motifs (e.g., Good Shepherd) but, in the famous reply to Empress Constantia, refuses to send an image of Christ.
- Argument summarized: Christ’s glory cannot be captured in dead colors; disciples fell on their faces at the Transfiguration; to paint would imitate pagans depicting what bears no true resemblance—practices “not lawful to us.”
- Schooping reads a line from second-century through fourth-century (Athenagoras to Eusebius) resisting acceptance of the image as religiously normative.

### Paul Alexander on Patriarch Nicephorus and symbolic cult

- Aristides alludes to philosophical theory: cult statues are images of gods, not the gods—again, educated pagan semiotic veneration.
- Justin Martyr echoes arguments against soulless idols; opponents counter that cult statues imitate honor due to gods.
- **Athenagoras:** Pagans reply that statues are mere images and worship is referred to the gods—referential argument known and rejected by Christians in the second century.
- **Celsus** and **Origen** (via Basil/Gregory Nazianzen’s compilation): Origen states common sense forbids thinking God is honored when fashioned in dead matter or honored through symbolic representation; images are not gods; such art is insignificant compared to God—no opening for “true” vs. “false” semiotic iconology in this passage (ch. 29, para. 3, p. 83 in edition cited).
- Arguments for/against images predate full Neoplatonic systematization; Porphyry wrote on images before studying under Plotinus (as Barasch is said to date it).

### Julian the Apostate and John of Damasus (as presented)

- Julian’s *Contra Christianos* fragments: no apology for image worship in what survives, but a letter to Archpriest Theodorus gives a theory of images as symbols/mediators of divine presence—“worship through them.” Schooping: Christ alone mediator; icons become “mediators of the mediator.”
- Julian: offerings to images persuade gods to help; when viewing images, neither reduce them to mere wood/stone nor equate them with gods—complex symbolic view.
- Claim: John of Damascus essentially adopts Julian’s thought patterns as Christian.
- **Gap thesis (Barasch/Alexander line):** Between Porphyry and ~7th c. (Johannes of Thessalonica, Leontius of Neapolis), critical gap bridged only by hypothesis; sometime between 3rd–7th c., Christians appropriated pagan defenses of cult statues, later cited against Jews/pagans—not building from Athenagoras or Irenaeus. Basil’s argument about worship to Christ passing to the Father is called a misapplication to iconology; bronze serpent as type of Christ, not of icons, is likewise rejected as prooftexting.

### Byzantine metamorphosis (post-529)

- Change placed after 529 and before certain 7th-c. writers—church “Byzantinized,” “Byzantine captivity”; much good retained (e.g., Nicene creedal tradition) but departure from earlier patristic consensus on images.
- Early church hostility to religious art aligned with prophetic critique of pagan cult statues: matter made/unmade by hands; ears that cannot hear—representation habituates superficiality; God desires direct personal relationship through incarnation, not mediation through matter or saints (saints topic deferred to another video).

### Seventh Ecumenical Council texts (as read from *Divine Sacra* and canons)

- Imperial sacra speaks of confirming “ancient tradition of venerable images”—Schooping argues this is false if “ancient” reaches back to first centuries; short institutional memory can make recent practice sound ancient.
- **Anathema language:** Bishops fear carrying anathema of the whole catholic church into “outer darkness” prepared for devil and angels—he takes this as damnation, not mild excommunication.
- **Innovation metaphor:** Peter striking the slave’s ear; cut down trees bearing fruit of “newly imported innovation” with canonical censure and send to Gehenna’s fires—severity tied to icon refusal.
- Council text: through representations, memory returns to prototypes and worshipers share holiness of saints—mediated holiness through objects.
- Heretics who charge the church with setting up images are “god-hated.”
- Distinction between “true worship of faith” (divine nature alone) vs. salutation, reverence, incense, lights to images, cross, Gospels—honor to image passes to prototype; “he who reveres the image reveres the subject represented.”
- **Anathemas (samples cited):** Against those who do not salute holy images; who call sacred images idols; who say Christians resort to images as to gods; who deny only Christ delivered from idols; who claim the catholic church ever received idols.
- **Soteriological claim (his gloss):** Salvation made to depend on bowing before images; refusal means one is not truly Christian in this system.
- **Canon 13 aside:** Monasteries as public houses—tangential; other text: excommunication as condemnation by Trinity and assignment where worm dies not and fire is not quenched.
- **Proskynesis:** Council interprets term to include kissing with lips, strong desire toward the object—mandatory affection toward physical icons; debate about icons alone draws anathema.
- Closing: salvation by Christ’s blood alone, faith alone, grace alone; faithful works through love; avoid “spiritual materialism” and arguments that move one incrementally toward full Orthodox acceptance.

## Core Ideas

- Formal **iconodulia** (Nicaea II) is the target, distinguished from **aniconism** and from blanket **iconoclasm**.
- Pagan philosophers (Porphyry, Iamblichus, Julian, etc.) already defended **referential veneration** of images; early Christians **uniformly rejected** sacred images in the apologetic period Barasch surveys.
- **Neoplatonism** (Proclus, etc.) supplies conceptual background later icon defenders presuppose.
- **Eusebius to Constantia** exemplifies fourth-century episcopal refusal to commission/portray Christ for veneration.
- The speaker reads **Nicaea II anathemas** as condemning non-venerators to **hell/complete separation from God**, which he judges a **different gospel** (Galatians) versus sola gratia / sola fide.

## Key Terms

- **Aniconism** — Non-use of images in religious worship (speaker’s position).
- **Iconoclasm** — Destruction/forbidding of religious images broadly (speaker disclaims arguing for this here).
- **Iconodulia** — Veneration of icons; here tied specifically to Seventh Ecumenical Council requirements and anathemas.
- **Semiosis / referential veneration** — Honor directed through the image to the prototype (traced to pagan sources).
- **Anathema** — In council rhetoric cited, consignment to outer darkness / Gehenna (speaker’s reading).
- **Proskynesis** — Greek term parsed in council material as including salutation and kissing.
- **Vincentian canon** — “Quod ubique, quod semper, quod ab omnibus”—used descriptively to claim Nicaea II innovates.
