# Summary

Joshua Schooping argues that **aniconism** (rejecting religious use of images in worship) must be distinguished from **iconoclasm** (destroying or forbidding religious art tout court). His target is **iconodulia** as defined by the **Seventh Ecumenical Council**: refusal to venerate icons—bowing, kissing, showing strong affection—subjects one to **anathema**. Using Moshe Barasch’s *History of Icons* and related scholarship (Paul Alexander on Nicephorus), he traces **referential, semiotic defenses of cult images** from **Porphyry**, **Iamblichus**, **Julian**, and **Proclus**, then claims **second- through fourth-century Christian writers** uniformly **rejected sacred images** (apologists, Tertullian, Syriac *Didascalia*, pseudo-Clementine discipline, **Eusebius’s refusal to Constantia**). He reads **Nicaea II** texts as binding **salvation-level consequences** on icon veneration and as picking up **pagan argument patterns** Julian used—hence a **Byzantine “metamorphosis”** away from early consensus and, in his view, a distortion of the **Protestant gospel** (Christ alone, faith alone, grace alone).

# Core Question

Does mandatory **icon veneration** under conciliar **anathema** represent **continuous apostolic tradition**, or a **late innovation** that reuses **pagan semiotic theories** and conflicts with **early Christian sources** and **evangelical soteriology**?

# Key Concepts

- **Aniconism vs. iconoclasm vs. iconodulia** — Three distinct positions; the speaker adopts aniconism and attacks council-defined iconodulia.
- **Semiotic / referential veneration** — Honor passes through the image to the prototype; traced to late antique philosophy and compared to Orthodox defenses.
- **Vincentian “everywhere, always, all”** — Used as a heuristic to argue Nicaea II breaks early unanimity on sacred images.
- **Anathema and Gehenna** — Selected conciliar rhetoric is read as **damnation**, not mild discipline, for non-venerators.
- **Byzantine metamorphosis** — Narrative of Christian uptake of pagan image-apologetics between roughly the **3rd and 7th centuries**, with a **documentary gap** filled by hypothesis.

# Why It Matters

The talk matters for anyone comparing **Eastern Orthodox** and **evangelical** claims: it frames **icons** not as incidental aesthetics but as **dogmatic obligations** tied to **communion and curse** in conciliar texts (as Schooping reads them), and it offers a **historical-philosophical genealogy** (Barasch, Alexander) for **why** that obligation might be **novel** relative to selected **pre-Nicene and fourth-century** voices. It also clarifies a common **ecumenical friction point**: **pastoral softening** (“I would never make you kiss an icon”) versus **formal conciliar teaching**.
