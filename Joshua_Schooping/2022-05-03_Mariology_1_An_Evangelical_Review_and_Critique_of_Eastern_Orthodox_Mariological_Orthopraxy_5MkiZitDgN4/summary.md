# Summary

Joshua Schooping walks through a Marian prayer book from New Rome Press (2018), whose texts come from the Greek corpus once mislabeled as St. Ephrem. He treats these ancient, widely used prayers as **popular Orthodox piety**—not a niche academic source—and quotes lines where Mary receives titles and powers he associates with **Christ alone** in Protestant teaching: sole purity, propitiation, salvation, mediation for the world, and even language about **containing** divine essence. He argues the prayers portray **God and Christ as dangerously inaccessible** while Mary is the proximate hope, and he ends with **St. Theodore the Studite**’s penitential **canon** as a dialogue in which Christ is **slow to forgive** until Mary intercedes. The talk closes with **sola** appeals (Christ, faith, grace, Scripture, God’s glory).

# Core Question

When devotional poetry addresses Mary with **soteriological and mediatorial** language, how should **evangelicals** read that practice—and how far can **honor** extend before it **reorders** the gospel as Protestants understand it?

# Key Concepts

- **Ephraem Graecus** vs authentic Syriac Ephrem; **Photios** and **ninth-century** horizons for some texts.
- **Hyperdulia** and **icon veneration** as parallel **created mediation** (speaker’s framing).
- **Typological transfer**: Gospel titles (**true vine**, **mystery revealed**, **propitiation**) applied to Mary.
- **Theodore the Studite**’s **Marian canon** as **dramatic dialogue** (sinner–Mary–Christ).
- **Liturgical phenomenology**: what **average readers** absorb versus what theologians **qualify**.

# Why It Matters

The video is a case study in **how source texts shape intuition** for converts and inquirers: praise that sounds **honorific** in one tradition can read as **functional displacement of Christ** in another. Whether one agrees with Schooping’s readings or not, the talk forces clarity about **poetry, dogma, and pastoral formation**—and about how each side explains **intercession**, **grace**, and **access to God**.
