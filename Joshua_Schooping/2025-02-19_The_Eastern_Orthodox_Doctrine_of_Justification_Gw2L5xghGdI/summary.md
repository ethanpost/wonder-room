# Summary

Pastor Joshua Schooping argues that Eastern Orthodox teaching on **justification** should be read from **binding conciliar** texts—especially **Jerusalem 1672**, which answered **Cyril Lucaris’s** **1629** confession. He ties modern **pan-Orthodox** language (e.g., **Crete 2016**) to the claim that **universal authority** did not end with the **seventh ecumenical council**. **Decree 9** defines faith as the **right notion** about God plus **works of love/commandments** that **justify with Christ**; **Decree 13** rejects **faith alone** and explicitly denies that faith functions like a **hand** grasping **Christ’s righteousness** for salvation, affirming instead **faith in us** **with works** and **meritorious** **fruits**. From a **Lutheran/Protestant** standpoint he contends this excludes **fiducial** trust in **Christ’s object** alone, undermines **assurance**, and shifts the verdict to **self-referential** **faithing and working**—contrasting **Chrysostom**, **Cyril of Alexandria**, and **Athanasius** with these **later** formulations.

# Core Question

Do the **canonical Orthodox** decrees on **faith** and **works** (especially **Jerusalem 1672**) cohere with **Reformation** accounts of **justifying faith** as **receiving Christ’s righteousness**, or do they formally commit to a different **structure** of **merit**, **works**, and **final appeal**?

# Key Concepts

- **Jerusalem Council (1672)** vs. **Lucaris (1629)** as the framing dispute
- **Decree 9**: **right notion** + **love/commandments** **justifying with Christ**
- **Decree 13**: rejection of **faith alone** and of **faith-as-hand** on **Christ’s righteousness**
- **Notitia / assensus / fiducia** (and **desiderium**) as analytic tools
- **Assurance**, **weak faith / strong Savior**, and **objective** **atonement**
- **Christus Victor** and **theosis** as the speaker reads their interaction with **justification** language

# Why It Matters

The video is a compressed **primary-source-driven** entry into **East–West** debates on **grace**, **works**, and **assurance**: it shows how **conciliar sentences** are invoked as **official** and how **Protestant** readers may **parse** them against **confessional** definitions of **faith** and **Christ’s righteousness**—with stakes for **ecumenical** understanding, **catechesis**, and whether **popular** Orthodox piety matches **documented** **synodal** claims.
