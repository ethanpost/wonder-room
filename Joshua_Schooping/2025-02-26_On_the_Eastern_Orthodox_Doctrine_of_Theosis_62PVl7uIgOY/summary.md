# Summary

Pastor Joshua Schooping surveys the Eastern Orthodox doctrine of theosis with two aims: to show how much of what is popularly assumed about its history is misleading, and to connect Palamite theology, Hesychasm, the Philokalia’s diffusion, monastic spiritual teaching, and Gregory Palamas’s Marian homilies into a single argumentative arc. He leans heavily on Norman Russell’s historical work to argue that “theosis” as a household word is a recent phenomenon, while also citing Athonite monastic sources and academic summaries of Palamas to describe cleansing, illumination, theoria, and the vision of the uncreated light. The lecture culminates in extended quotations from Palamas on Mary as boundary between created and uncreated, mediator of divine light, and model of hesychia, framed from a confessional Lutheran perspective as a distortion of the gospel’s means of assurance and grace.

# Core Question

How much of contemporary Eastern Orthodox talk about theosis reflects ancient universal piety versus a modern reappropriation of Palamas, Philokalia spirituality, and Russian religious philosophy—and what difference does that make when Marian and Hesychast themes are treated as normative for salvation and assurance?

# Key Concepts

- **Theosis / deification** and **participation in uncreated divine energies** (Palamite councils and the 2016 Crete encyclical as framing documents).
- **Norman Russell’s** narrative of theosis’s move from technical obscurity to popular slogan, with four causal factors (Palamas, Russian philosophy, Philokalia, patristic re-engagement).
- **Philokalia** (1782 Venice; later Russian, Romanian, English reception) and **Hesychasm** / **Jesus prayer**.
- Monastic progression: **cleansing** of the psyche, **theoria**, **uncreated light**, and experiential themes around forgiveness and **synergy**.
- **Palamas’s Marian homilies**: Mary as **boundary**, **lamp**, **exclusive mediation language**, and **hesychia** as a “new way,” plus liturgical **“Most Holy Theotokos, save us.”**

# Why It Matters

The lecture matters for anyone comparing Christian traditions on **sanctification, assurance, and union with Christ**: it presses the question whether similar vocabulary (“deification,” “theosis”) maps onto the same soteriology, and whether hymnographic and Palamite claims about Mary and light function as rhetorical flourish or binding doctrine. It also offers a concrete historical argument—via Russell and publication dates—against treating current Orthodox emphasis on theosis as timeless default Christianity, which shapes fair ecumenical reading of sources and modern apologetics.
