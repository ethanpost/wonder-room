## Detailed Outline

### Why justification was the center of the Reformation

- Many people who call themselves Protestants, in the speaker’s experience, cannot explain what they are protesting; answers often focus on confession to a priest, papal infallibility, Marian doctrines, and similar issues.
- When Erasmus wrote a diatribe against Luther and Luther replied, Luther thanked Erasmus for not attacking him on matters Luther considered trivial and for addressing the heart of the Reformation: **how a sinner finds salvation in Christ**.
- Luther treated **justification by faith alone** as the article on which the church stands or falls—not a side issue but the core of biblical teaching on salvation.
- The aim is not to get lost in peripheral disputes that might have been negotiable, but to focus on the issue that fractured Christendom.

### Competing meanings of “justification” (Latin vs Greek)

- English **justification** derives from Latin **justificare**, which originally meant **“to make righteous.”**
- Early Latin fathers worked especially from the **Latin Vulgate** (not the Greek New Testament) and framed justification with imagery from the **legal system of the Roman Empire**, where **justificare** carried that “make righteous” sense.
- In much Roman Catholic development, justification addresses how an unrighteous fallen sinner can **be made righteous**—with the ordering idea that **justification follows sanctification**: one must be sanctified enough to exhibit a righteousness acceptable to God.
- The Reformation, influenced by renewed study of antiquity, emphasized the Greek background (the speaker cites **dikaiosune** / justification language in Greek) as **to declare righteous** rather than primarily “to make righteous.”
- For Protestantism, **justification comes before sanctification**—a reversal of sequence relative to much Roman Catholic articulation at the time of the debate.
- Result: a **different order of salvation** between the communions, not merely different vocabulary.

### Justification as tied to the church’s sacramental ministry (Roman perspective)

- From a Roman Catholic perspective in this lecture, **justification is a function of the sacerdotal operations of the church**—especially the **sacraments**, beginning with **baptism**.
- **Baptism** is described as operating **ex opere operato** (“through the working of the work”): Protestants have often heard this as meaning baptism works **automatically** in the sense that the baptized person is placed **ex opere operato** into a state of grace or justification.
- Roman Catholic responders object to the word **automatic**, insisting there must be some **predisposition** in the recipient—at least **no hostility** to receiving the sacrament—for it to function as intended.
- Still, the lecture stresses a **high view of baptism’s efficacy** in changing a person’s status into being placed in a state of grace.

### Infused grace vs Protestant language about grace

- In baptism, grace is said to be **infused**—a keyword in Reformation-era controversy: grace (and Christ’s righteousness in this scheme) is **poured into** the soul.
- Roman Catholic theologians are careful not to reduce grace to a crude “substance,” yet sacramental theology often uses **quantitative** language: grace can **increase** or **diminish**; it can be spoken of as **inhabiting** the soul.
- Protestants more commonly describe grace as **God’s unmerited benevolence** toward sinners—an action of God rather than a “stuff” measured in the soul.
- Protestants affirm being **filled by the Holy Spirit**, but the speaker distinguishes that from this **infused righteousness / justifying grace** model tied to baptismal status.

### Cooperation, assent, and the instability of initial justification

- At the **Council of Trent**, Rome defined her position over against the reformers using **cooperare et assentare**: to **cooperate with** and **assent to** the grace bestowed in baptism.
- If one possesses infused baptismal grace and **assents** and **cooperates**, one is in a state of grace and justification—but that justification is **not immutable**; it can change.
- The infused justifying grace received in baptism may be **lost entirely**, removing a person from justification and under **damnation’s threat**.
- That loss is tied to a particular category of sin: **mortal sin** (contrasted with **venial sin**).

### Mortal vs venial sin; Calvin’s critique of the framework

- **Venial sin** is real sin but less serious than mortal sin; **mortal sin** is more egregious.
- Examples from Roman moral theology (as cited): **getting tipsy** as venial vs **getting drunk** as mortal; some moralists taught **missing Mass on Sunday** as mortal—**not universal agreement** on every catalog of mortal sins, but many historical lists.
- Mortal sin is “mortal” because it is serious enough to cause the **death of justifying grace** infused at baptism.
- **Calvin** rejected Rome’s mortal/venial scheme **as Rome articulated it** for justification’s security: reformers could affirm **gradations of sin** from Jesus’ teaching, and Calvin could say **all sin is mortal** in the sense that **all sin deserves death** (creation threat: the soul that sins shall die; even small sins as treason).
- Yet Calvin also argued: **no sin is mortal** in the sense of **destroying saving grace** received at justification—contrasting Rome’s claim that certain sins remove justifying grace.

### Penance as restoration: “second plank” and the real Protestant flashpoint

- If mortal sin destroys justifying grace, Rome provides an **antidote**: the **sacrament of penance**, described (16th-century definition cited) as **“the second plank of justification for those who have made shipwreck of their faith”**—a nautical image for those who lost justification through mortal sin.
- Penance includes **confession**, **contrition** (not merely fear of punishment but sorrow for offending God), and **priestly absolution** (“**Te absolvo**”—I absolve you).
- Protestant caricatures target needing a priest, confession to a priest, mediation, etc.; **not all reformers opposed confession**: **Lutherans** retained confession **one to another** as salutary, with protected disclosure and a minister declaring **assurance of pardon** to the genuinely penitent.
- The speaker’s congregation example: **corporate confession** followed by **assurance of pardon** from Scripture—so confession/absolution language itself was not necessarily the core divide.
- The deeper divide: to be restored, penance required **works of satisfaction**—where **works** enter prominently.

### “Faith alone” vs Rome’s “plus” formulas; merit (condign vs congruous)

- Popular Protestant slogans misrepresent Rome if they reduce the difference to “Protestants: faith/grace/Christ; Catholics: works/merit/self-righteousness.” Rome insisted justification requires **faith**, **grace**, and **Christ**.
- The battle turned on **“alone”**: Rome’s formula was pictured as **faith plus works**, **grace plus merit**, **Christ plus inherent righteousness in yourself**—the **pluses** were the problem—especially in penance’s **works of satisfaction** (e.g., set prayers, restitution, pilgrimage, alms—linked to the **indulgence** controversy context).
- **Condign merit**: so meritorious that God would be **unjust** not to reward it.
- Works of satisfaction in penance **do not** reach condign merit; they are **congruous merit** (**meritum de congruo**): real merit, dependent on prior grace, making it **fitting** for God to restore justification—**incongruous** not to restore if the penitent completed the prescribed satisfaction.
- **Luther** treated **justification by faith alone** as a thunderbolt against **any** human merit adding to **Christ’s sole satisfaction** for sin.

## Core Ideas

- The Reformation’s center was **sola fide justification** as the answer to **how sinners are saved in Christ**, not only debates about church practice or Marian dogma.
- **Lexical and legal background** (Latin “make righteous” vs Greek “declare righteous”) shaped divergent **orders of salvation** (justification relative to sanctification).
- Roman Catholic justification in this lecture is presented as **sacramentally mediated**, beginning in **baptismal infusion** of grace, conditioned on **cooperation and assent**, and **losable** through **mortal sin**, with **penance** as restoration—especially controversial for **works of satisfaction** and **merit** language.

## Key Terms

- **Justificare** — Latin root behind English “justification”; etymologically “to make righteous.”
- **Dikaiosune** — Greek term family tied to righteousness/justification; used to support Protestant emphasis on **declaration** rather than **making** righteous in the forensic sense stressed here.
- **Ex opere operato** — “through the working of the work”; efficacy language for sacraments (especially baptism in this segment).
- **Infused grace** — grace poured into the soul; quantitative increase/diminution language in controversy.
- **Cooperare et assentare** — cooperate with and assent to grace (Trent-era formulation cited).
- **Mortal sin / venial sin** — serious sin that “kills” justifying grace vs lesser sin (examples and lists vary).
- **Sacrament of penance** — restoration after mortal sin; “second plank of justification” (phrase cited from 16th-century Roman definition).
- **Works of satisfaction** — prescribed acts in penance (prayers, restitution, pilgrimage, alms, etc.).
- **Condign merit** — merit demanding reward on justice grounds.
- **Congruous merit (meritum de congruo)** — merit that makes restoration **fitting**, not strictly **condign**.

## People

- **Martin Luther** — justification by faith alone as the church’s pivotal article.
- **Desiderius Erasmus** — wrote against Luther; Luther appreciated engagement at the central issue.
- **John Calvin** — critique of Rome’s mortal/venial scheme relative to security of grace.

## Dates / Periods

## Expected Outcome

After taking notes from this outline and watching or listening to the source material, an **A+ student** would be capable of:

### Knowing

- Explain why the speaker thinks many Protestants answer “why not Catholic?” with **peripheral issues** rather than the Reformation’s core concern.
- State Luther’s claim that **justification by faith alone** is the **article on which the church stands or falls**.
- Trace how **justificare** (“make righteous”) connects Latin patristic/Vulgate reading to Roman legal imagery and a **sanctification-then-justification** ordering in the Roman account presented.
- Contrast that ordering with the Protestant emphasis (via Greek background) on **declaration of righteousness** and **justification before sanctification**.
- Describe **baptism** as the starting point of justification in the Roman system presented, including **ex opere operato** and Roman qualifiers about **non-automatic** reception.
- Define **infused grace** and contrast Protestant vs Roman **grace** vocabulary as presented (unmerited favor vs quantitative indwelling grace).
- Explain **cooperate and assent** language and why baptismal justification can be **lost** in this framework.
- Distinguish **mortal** and **venial** sin with the lecture’s examples and note variability in **mortal sin lists**.
- Summarize **Calvin’s two senses** of “mortal”: all sin deserves death; no sin removes **justifying grace** in Calvin’s Protestant claim.
- Reconstruct the **penance** pathway: confession, contrition, absolution, then **works of satisfaction**.
- Quote or paraphrase the **“second plank of justification”** image and what “shipwreck” means in that metaphor.
- Explain **condign** vs **congruous** merit and which applies to penitential works of satisfaction in the lecture’s Roman taxonomy.
- Recognize the speaker’s correction of slogans: Rome affirms **faith, grace, Christ**; the fight was about **“alone”** and the **pluses** (works, merit, inherent righteousness).

### Reciting

- Give a 30–60 second version of: **Latin “make righteous” vs Greek “declare righteous” → different ordo salutis**.
- Recite the chain: **baptism → infused grace → cooperate/assent → losable justification → mortal sin → penance → works of satisfaction → congruous merit**.

### Sharing

- Teach a peer why **confession to a minister** in some Reformed/Lutheran practice is not the same dispute as Rome’s **works of satisfaction** requirement for restoration.
- Explain, without caricature, why **faith alone** was controversial if Rome still affirmed faith—because the argument targeted **faith-plus** structures.

### Conversing

- Debate whether **ex opere operato** is best described as “automatic” given Roman qualifications about disposition and hostility.
- Compare **mortal/venial** as Rome presented it with **Calvin’s** reframing about deserved death vs loss of saving grace.
- Discuss whether **congruous merit** language avoids or retains problems Luther associated with **merit** in justification.
