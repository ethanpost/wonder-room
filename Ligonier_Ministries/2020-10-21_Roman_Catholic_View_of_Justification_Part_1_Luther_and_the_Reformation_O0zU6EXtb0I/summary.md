# Summary

R. C. Sproul frames the Reformation’s center as **how sinners are saved in Christ**, not miscellaneous Protestant peeves about priests or Mary. Luther’s **justification by faith alone** is the article on which the church stands or falls. The lecture then maps a Roman Catholic account (as contested by Protestants): **Latin “make righteous”** and sacramental **infusion** in **baptism** (**ex opere operato**), **cooperation and assent**, **losable** justification through **mortal sin**, restoration through **penance**, and especially **works of satisfaction** analyzed with **condign** vs **congruous** merit—contrasted with Protestant emphases on **declaration**, **grace as God’s favor**, and **Christ’s sole satisfaction**.

# Core Question

What actually divided Rome and the Reformation on **justification** if both sides could speak of **faith, grace, and Christ**—and why did **order** (justification vs sanctification), **sacramental infusion**, **mortal sin**, **penance**, and **merit** language matter?

# Key Concepts

- **Justificare** (“make righteous”) vs Greek-tuned **declaration** of righteousness
- **Infused grace** at baptism and **cooperare et assentare**
- **Mortal vs venial sin** and **Calvin’s** reframing
- **Penance** as the **“second plank”**; **works of satisfaction**
- **Condign** vs **congruous** merit; **sola fide** as ruling out human merit **added** to Christ

# Why It Matters

Without clarity here, people rehearse **caricatures** (“Catholics deny faith”) or fight **symptoms** while missing the historical **breaking point** of Western Christianity—and they remain unprepared for serious **ecumenical** or **devotional** conversations about **assurance**, **sacraments**, and **the gospel**.
