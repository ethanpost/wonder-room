## Detailed Outline

### Continuity from Part 1 and the Council of Trent as Rome’s formal response

- This session continues the Roman Catholic account of **justification** in the **16th-century** crisis; later lectures will contrast this with the **Reformation** view.
- The Roman Catholic Church responded mid–**16th century** with the **Council of Trent** (held at **Trento**, **Italy**); teaching tied to that council is often called **Tridentine** (commonly pronounced with stress like “trid-in-teen,” not “tri-dentine”).
- **Tridentine** decrees are presented as the **official** Roman Catholic articulation of **justification** for ecumenical comparison.

### “The Reformation is over” vs Trent’s ongoing authority (as argued in the lecture)

- Many today treat **Trent** as irrelevant to modern Rome–Protestant dialogue; the speaker rejects that as out of step with Rome’s own claims.
- **Trent** is described as an **ecumenical council** carrying the weight of **infallibility** for Rome—therefore not something Rome can casually **repeal** without undermining its picture of **church authority** and **tradition** (the speaker uses a vivid metaphor: Rome suffers from a kind of **“theological hemophilia”**—scratch her theologically and she bleeds to death—meaning Rome cannot simply walk back conciliar decrees without systemic cost to its authority claims).
- The **Catechism of the Catholic Church** (late **20th century**) is cited as a **clear reaffirmation** of **Trent’s** authority—so ignoring Tridentine justification is ignoring what Roman authorities teach.
- Local priests in places like **America**, **Holland**, or **Germany** may dispute points, but relative to the **Roman curia** and authorities in **that city** (Rome), **Trent** stands as **immutable** teaching on justification in the speaker’s framing.

### Sixth session: doctrine first, then anathemas

- **Trent** met across multiple sessions addressing different topics (e.g., **sacraments**, **Scripture** in the **fourth session**, clergy corruption and reform efforts such as opposing **simony**).
- For **justification**, the **sixth session** is said to matter most.
- That session has two major parts: (1) **formal teaching** defining Rome’s doctrine of justification; (2) **canons** framed as repudiating **error** and **heresy**.
- The canon formula (paraphrased): **If anyone says** such-and-such, **let him be anathema**.

### Which anathemas target whom—and why that matters for “misunderstanding” theories

- The **first three canons** are said to be aimed against **Pelagianism** (already condemned earlier) and **not** particularly targeted at the **Protestant Reformation**.
- The speaker grants that **some** Trent canons, aimed at reformers, **missed** the reformers due to **misunderstandings**—supporting a partial “talking past each other” narrative.
- But **not entirely**: some canons are “**right on the mark**” and clearly **anathematize** the **Reformation doctrine of justification by faith** (as the speaker reads them).
- If the **Reformation articulation** is the **biblical gospel**, then to **anathematize** it is to **anathematize the gospel**—and a body that condemns an **essential** Christian truth becomes **apostate** and not a **true/valid church** in this line of reasoning.
- Ecumenism: many Protestant bodies may not care about doctrinal difference, but if **biblical justification** is taken seriously, there can be **no rapprochement** without **surrender** by one side—the positions are **incompatible**; **somebody is wrong** and has distorted the NT gospel.
- The speaker ties this to **Galatians**: “**another gospel**” / even an **angel**—**let him be anathema**—so, on this reading, **someone** stands under **God’s anathema**, and that issue “**has not been resolved**.”

### Rome on faith: necessary but not sufficient (sixth session)

- The speaker rejects the caricature that Protestants believe **faith alone** while Rome believes **works alone** with **no faith**; Rome’s **sixth session** is said to treat **faith** as a **necessary condition** for justification.
- Trent language (Latin, as cited in speech): faith is the **initium**, **fundamentum**, and **radix** of justification (the transcript spells the third term as “rodex,” but the standard Trent triad is **initium / fundamentum / radix**).
  - **Initium**: faith **initiates** / is the **starting place** of justification.
  - **Fundamentum**: faith is the **foundation** / fundamental structure **without which** there is no justification.
  - **Radix**: faith is the **root** / radical **core** of justification—**not** a mere appendage next to sacramental power.
- **Necessary condition**: without it, justification does not follow (like **oxygen** and **fire**: no oxygen, no fire).
- **Sufficient condition**: meeting it **surely** yields the result; oxygen alone doesn’t guarantee fire.
- Rome teaches faith is **not sufficient** **in and of itself** to yield justification.

### Faith “intact” yet justification lost: Trent on mortal sin

- In Rome’s treatment of losing justification through **mortal sin**, Trent is said to allow that a person can retain **real faith** while committing **mortal sin**—faith not lost, but **justification** lost.
- Implication stressed: one can have what the speaker calls **“saving faith”** (in a problematic sense for Protestant categories) **without** being **justified**—faith retained, justification gone.

### Instrumental cause: Aristotle’s causes and the baptism vs faith clash

- Rome distinguishes kinds of **causes** (medieval synthesis with **Aristotle**).
- Aristotle’s statue illustration (as used in the lecture):
  - **Material cause**: what it’s made **out of** (e.g., **stone / marble**).
  - **Formal cause**: the **blueprint / format** the sculptor follows.
  - **Final cause**: the **purpose** (e.g., beautify a garden).
  - **Efficient cause**: the agent bringing it about (the **sculptor**).
  - **Instrumental cause**: the **tools** (chisel, hammer) that actually shape the stone—**not** merely wishing the marble into a statue.
- Rome applies this pattern: the **instrumental cause** of justification—what the church uses as the **means/tool** to bring a person into **justified grace**—is the **sacrament of baptism**.
- Reformers (preview for next session): the **sole** instrumental cause is **faith in the heart**, not the sacrament—faith links the believer to **Christ’s work**.

### Rome’s justification as an “analytical” scheme (declaring vs being just)

- The Roman view is labeled **analytical** here: like an **analytical** / **tautological** truth (e.g., **2 + 2 = 4**, or “**a bachelor is an unmarried man**”) where the predicate adds nothing genuinely new beyond analysis of the subject.
- Both sides agree that, finally, **no one is justified** unless **God declares** the person **just**—a **legal declaration** by God’s judgment.
- The analytical Roman claim, as presented: God **will not** declare a person legally just **unless/until**, **under analysis**, the person **actually is just**—God does not “**just count**” unjust people as just.
- Therefore Rome (16th-century line cited) says **justice/righteousness must inhere** in the soul (**Latin** **inherens**): inherent righteousness before the declaration matches reality.
- Moral outcomes in this framework:
  - Die in **mortal sin** → **hell**.
  - Die with **any sin / imperfection** on the soul → not fit for heaven; must pass through **purgatory’s** purging until righteousness is **truly inherent**.
- Pastoral consequence emphasized: if salvation required arriving at **pure righteousness with no imperfections**, the speaker says that would be **despair-inducing “horrible news,”** not good news—for him personally.
- Closing preview (next session): the Reformation affirms that the moment one possesses **saving faith**, one is transferred from darkness to light, **declared just** on the basis of **Christ’s righteousness**, adopted, sins remitted forever—**no purgatory**, **no second plank** of justification.

## Core Ideas

- **Council of Trent** (sixth session) is the decisive Roman Catholic formalization on **justification**, with **Tridentine** authority claimed as **ongoing** and **non-dismissible** in Rome’s system.
- **Necessary vs sufficient** conditions clarify Rome’s insistence that **faith** is essential yet **not enough**—especially illustrated by **faith intact + mortal sin = loss of justification**.
- **Instrumental causality** frames a deep divide: **baptism** (Roman instrumental cause) vs **faith** (Reformation claim previewed).
- Rome’s scheme is characterized as **analytical justification**: divine **declaration** tracks **actual inherent** righteousness, with **purgatory** as the bridge for remaining imperfection.

## Key Terms

- **Council of Trent / Tridentine** — Mid-16th-century ecumenical council at Trento; adjective for its teaching.
- **Sixth session** — Trent session most directly treating justification (doctrine + canons).
- **Canon / anathema** — “If anyone says … let him be anathema” formulas condemning specified positions.
- **Pelagianism** — Early error; Trent’s first three anti-Pelagian canons are distinguished from anti-Reformation intent.
- **Necessary condition** — Required for a result (faith, in Rome’s sixth session).
- **Sufficient condition** — If met, guarantees the result (Rome denies faith alone is sufficient).
- **Initium, fundamentum, radix** — Latin triad for faith’s initiating, foundational, and root role in Rome’s justification teaching (as cited).
- **Mortal sin** — Serious sin that can remove justification while faith remains, in the Trent reading summarized here.
- **Material / formal / final / efficient / instrumental cause** — Aristotelian causal schema used to explain Rome’s **baptism-as-instrument** logic.
- **Analytical / tautology** — Rhetorical frame for Rome’s claim that God’s justifying declaration presupposes actual justice.
- **Inherens** — Inherent righteousness required within the soul before God’s declaration matches reality (as summarized from Rome’s 16th-century articulation).
