# Summary

This lecture continues R. C. Sproul’s walk through the Roman Catholic doctrine of justification by focusing on the **Council of Trent**, especially **Session 6**: first Rome’s **formal definition**, then the **canons** that end with **anathemas**. Sproul argues Trent remains **magisterially central** for Rome (reaffirmed in the late-20th-century **Catechism**), so dismissing Tridentine justification as ecumenically irrelevant ignores Roman authority claims. He grants **partial misunderstanding** between some Trent canons and Protestant targets, but insists **some canons** directly condemn **justification by faith** as the Reformation taught it—making reconciliation impossible without **surrender** on one side if the gospel is at stake (he ties the point to **Galatians**). The constructive heart contrasts **necessary** vs **sufficient** conditions: Rome treats **faith** as **initium, fundamentum, radix**—deeply integral—yet **not sufficient**, illustrated by retaining **faith** while losing **justification** through **mortal sin**. He then uses **Aristotle’s causal scheme** (especially **instrumental cause**) to explain Rome’s identification of **baptism** as the **instrument** of justification, setting up the Reformation counterclaim (previewed) that **faith** is the **sole instrumental** link to Christ. Finally, he labels Rome’s pattern **“analytical justification”**: God’s **declaring** someone just tracks **actual inherent** righteousness (**inherens**), with **purgatory** as the path for remaining imperfection—contrasted with the **good news** claim he will unpack next time.

# Core Question

If **Trent’s anathemas** still mean what Rome says they mean, and if **Protestant justification** is the **biblical gospel**, how can anyone treat the **Reformation divide** as a **resolved misunderstanding** rather than a **contradiction** about what **must** be true for a sinner to be **accepted by God**?

# Key Concepts

- **Council of Trent / Tridentine** authority in Roman teaching
- **Session 6**: doctrine + **canons** (“if anyone says … **anathema**”)
- **Necessary vs sufficient** conditions (faith **necessary**, not **sufficient**)
- **Faith intact** + **mortal sin** → **justification lost** (as summarized from Trent)
- **Aristotelian causes**, especially **instrumental cause**
- **Baptism** (Rome) vs **faith** (Reformation preview) as **instrument**
- **Analytical justification** and **inherent** righteousness (**inherens**)
- **Purgatory** as cleansing for **remaining imperfection** in this scheme

# Why It Matters

This is not a footnote in church history: it is the map people use to answer **“What must I do to be saved?”** If Rome’s **declaration** of justification waits on **inherent** righteousness and **purgatorial** cleansing, and Protestantism says **justification** is secured **through faith** in **Christ’s** righteousness **now**, then pastoral **hope**, **assurance**, and **unity** conversations hinge on which account matches **Scripture**—not on tone alone.
