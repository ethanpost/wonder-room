# Summary

This lecture examines how the Supreme Court’s decision in United States v. Virginia forced the all‑male Virginia Military Institute to admit women, using that case to critique the Court’s modern Equal Protection jurisprudence and broader anti‑discrimination regime. Scott Yenner argues that VMI’s true mission—cultivating manly honor, martial valor, and male leadership—could not be defended honestly in court, because anti‑discrimination law requires institutions to speak in the language of diversity, outcomes, and stereotypes rather than nature, sex, and vocation. He contends that Justice Ginsburg’s assumption that men and women should share identical educational goals, combined with a suspicion of sex‑based roles, helped dismantle VMI’s distinctive ethos and transform it into a bureaucratized, coeducational institution. The talk uses VMI’s trajectory to illustrate how equality, detached from its textual focus on “protection,” has become an empty standard that erodes traditional family and civic structures.

# Core Question

How has the Supreme Court’s anti‑discrimination‑focused reading of the Equal Protection Clause reshaped sex‑specific institutions like VMI, and what does that reveal about the costs of treating natural sex differences and differentiated social roles as illegitimate “stereotypes” in law and public life?

# Key Concepts

- Equal Protection Clause as anti‑discrimination engine rather than protection of the laws
- VMI’s adversative method and Code of the Gentleman as sex‑specific formation
- The “shadowland” of legal discourse that suppresses candid arguments about nature and sex
- Justice Ginsburg’s rejection of sex‑based destinies in U.S. v. Virginia
- Empirical evidence from VMI’s coeducation experience as a test of the Court’s predictions
- The need to rehabilitate legitimate forms of discrimination and reintroduce family‑supportive principles into law

# Why It Matters

The argument matters because it links a concrete institutional story—the transformation of VMI—to fundamental questions about how constitutional law governs sex, family, and citizenship in the United States. By showing how anti‑discrimination logic can hollow out long‑standing practices rooted in natural and historical understandings of male and female, the lecture challenges listeners to reconsider what kinds of distinctions a healthy society must preserve. It also suggests practical and legal strategies for states that wish to revive single‑sex institutions and re‑anchor equal protection in a more limited, text‑ and nature‑respecting framework.
