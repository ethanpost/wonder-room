## I. Framing the Conversation: Narnia and Political Imagination

### A. Opening question: How does Narnia illuminate our political world?

- Comparison: "What hath Washington, D.C. to do with Cair Paravel?"
- Aim: Use Narnia's political and moral vision to understand real-world law, kingship, and resistance to tyranny.

### B. C.S. Lewis as a political thinker

- Common misconception: Lewis as "above the culture war" or merely a children's author and apologist.
- Counter-claim: Lewis was deeply engaged in cultural and political conflicts, often "skewering" modern errors.
- Central concern: Connection Lewis saw between modern progressivism and tyranny.

## II. Law Above the King: Kingship, Legitimacy, and Constitutional Order

### A. King Lune (Lon) and Shasta: The king under the law

- Shasta's reluctance to be king and desire to "bow out."
- Lune's teaching: "You can no more leave your post than a sentry can leave his" – kingship as duty, not preference.
- Key principle: The king is under the law; "the law makes him king," not vice versa.
- Connection to Rutherford: Rejection of "divine right of kings" and absolute monarchy.

### B. Political "Easter eggs" in Narnia

- Miraz as "Lord Protector of Narnia" – echo of Oliver Cromwell's title and English constitutional history.
- Characters like Trumpkin at the Parliament of Owls: loyal "king's men" who reject conspiracy and sedition.
- Merlin in *That Hideous Strength*: the king cannot simply be overthrown because he is weak; loyalty to lawful authority matters.

### C. Legitimacy, usurpation, and the messiness of history

- The White Witch: An evil usurper ruling for a hundred years: longevity does not confer moral legitimacy.
- The White Witch: Her secret police and totalitarian "always winter, never Christmas" regime as a picture of tyranny.
- The Telmarines: Began as invading pirates and oppressors of Narnian beasts.
- The Telmarines: Over time, their rule becomes "legitimized" as dynasties form and society stabilizes.
- The Telmarines: Caspian IX and X: legitimate Narnian kings despite disreputable beginnings of Telmarine presence.
- Historical parallel: Human political orders are always "messy"; we cannot constantly "redo" Hastings or Gettysburg.
- Historical parallel: There is a real point where de facto rule hardens into de jure legitimacy.

## III. The American Revolution, Early Resistance, and the "Unwritten Constitution"

### A. Why early resistance matters

- If tyranny is not resisted at the beginning, later resistance becomes much harder to justify.
- Example: Parliamentary taxation of the American colonies.
- The issue was not "a penny tax," but jurisdiction and constitutional authority.
- If tolerated for 40–100 years, it becomes part of the "unwritten constitution."

### B. Written and unwritten constitutions

- Every nation has an unwritten constitution—customs, expectations, and norms.
- The American written Constitution is only part of the system; party structures and procedures are also "unwritten."
- U.S. two-party system and pre-election "horse trading."
- Parliamentary coalitions and post-election bargaining.

### C. Legality of the American War for Independence

- Colonial charters: King as executive, colonial legislatures as taxing bodies.
- Parliament's taxation as unconstitutional: akin to a random U.S. state taxing an out-of-state resident.
- Edmund Burke's view: Opposed the French Revolution as lawless and totalitarian.
- Edmund Burke's view: Defended the American cause as a fight for the old English constitution.
- Americans as constitutional conservatives: They claimed to uphold law and charters against parliamentary usurpation.
- Americans as constitutional conservatives: King George's failure: He should have defended the colonies from Parliament's overreach.

## IV. When to Rise Up: Resistance, Lesser Magistrates, and Romans 13

### A. Biblical and Narnian timelines of deliverance

- Israel in Egypt for 400 years; ultimate deliverance is clearly God-initiated.
- Narnia under the White Witch for 100 years; Aslan's coming with the Pevensies marks the turning point.
- Question: Must political change always be directly and obviously prompted by God?

### B. Doctrine of the lesser magistrate

- Reformation teaching: Individuals may resist defensively (e.g., protect family, refuse unjust conscription).
- Reformation teaching: But initiating full-scale overthrow of government is not for private persons.
- Legitimate resistance should be led by existing authorities within the system (lesser magistrates).
- Example: The shah's son or a mayor in Iran; legitimacy through position inside the structure.

### C. Reformation pattern for resistance

- First: Prophesy and preach against tyranny.
- Second: Flee (e.g., emigration to America).
- Third: Take up arms defensively as a last resort.
- American Revolution: Colonial legislatures and conventions acting corporately, not mobs.
- Patrick Henry's "Liberty or Death" speech after a decade of failed petitions and clear military threat.

### D. Romans 13 and legal appeals

- Misuse: "Do whatever the guy behind the desk says" as a simplistic reading.
- Biblical model: Paul's appeal to Caesar.
- Modern analogy: Challenging unconstitutional laws: Standing requires harm; thus one must sometimes break the law to gain a hearing.
- Civil disobedience, followed by legal appeal, can be faithful obedience to Romans 13.

## V. Law, Story, and World War II: Why Lewis Wrote Narnia Then

### A. Narnia and the Ransom Trilogy in wartime context

- Both series are shaped by the experience and atmosphere of World War II.
- Repeated references to the war ground the stories in contemporary reality.

### B. Stories vs. pamphlets

- Pamphlets can matter, but stories endure and shape imaginations.
- Lewis wrote for children like Lucy Barfield who had just endured bombings, evacuations, and fear.
- Aim: Provide "soul food" and anchor truths in child-accessible form.

### C. The fairy tale structure updated

- Traditional fairy tale: a cottage on the edge of fairyland; mundane world gives way to magic.
- Mid-20th-century equivalent: children evacuated from London air raids to stay with an odd professor.
- The wardrobe as portal: from familiar wartime England to Narnia's moral and cosmic drama.

### D. Heroism then and now

- The Battle of Britain as background of staggering heroism in the skies above London.
- Parallel: Children leave a real theater of heroism and then learn true heroism in Narnia.

## VI. The Founding of Narnia: Law, Calling, and Humble Royalty

### A. Coronation of King Frank and Queen Helen

- Frank as ordinary cabbie; Helen a washerwoman: commoners elevated to royalty.
- Aslan's charge: the king must be first in every desperate attack and last in every desperate retreat; laugh loudest over a scanty meal in times of hunger.
- Standard of kingship: sacrificial leadership, courage, and joy under hardship.

### B. Continuity from Frank to later kings

- King Lune echoes Aslan's standard centuries later.
- True kingship is measured by willingness to bear hardship and risk for others.

### C. Hierarchy and equality in Lewis's vision

- All humans are "sons of Adam and daughters of Eve" before Aslan: fundamental equality.
- Yet hierarchy is inevitable: some must rule, some must follow.
- Lewis's argument: If awe for rightful rulers is suppressed, people will idolize celebrities, athletes, gangsters, or "poisonous" alternatives.

### D. The sacrificial king as foundation

- Aslan's self-giving death for Edmund (and by implication all Narnia).
- Tyrion's statement: It is through Aslan's blood that all Narnia is saved.
- Pattern: Foundational kingship is sacrificial; all human rulers are to imitate Aslan.

## VII. Lessons in Statesmanship from Narnian Kings

### A. King Lune as model king

- Definition of kingship (headship applied broadly): First in every desperate attack, last in every desperate retreat.
- Definition of kingship: Laugh loudest over the scantiest meal; cheerful courage under pressure.
- Shakespearean background: Henry V – "Once more unto the breach" and leading from the front.
- Shakespearean background: Comforting common soldiers (a "little touch of Harry in the night").
- Shakespearean background: St. Crispin's Day speech: honor, trust in God, and camaraderie.
- Application: Leaders as stabilizing, joyful presences in hardship—home, church, and state.

### B. Courtliness, restraint, and chivalry

- Aravis's impression of Lune: personally rough (just from kennels) yet unmistakably courtly and aristocratic.
- Lune's rebuke about taunting enemies: "Never taunt an enemy unless he is stronger than you; then as you please."
- Lune's rebuke: Teaches children a principle of magnanimity and self-control.
- Tyrion's rebuke to Eustace: After dwarfs shoot horses, Eustace rages and "cusses them out."
- Tyrion: "Either courteous words or hard knocks are a warrior's only language."
- Parallel: Lewis's chivalry essay—knights should be mild and gentle in the hall, fierce on the battlefield.

### C. The four Pevensies' reign

- Summary of their rule in *The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe*: They made good laws and kept the peace.
- Summary: Saved good trees from unnecessary cutting.
- Summary: Liberated young dwarfs and satyrs from being sent to (bad) schools.
- Summary: Stopped "busy-bodies and interferers" and encouraged ordinary people who wanted to live and let live.
- Political philosophy: Limited government, protection of property, and local freedom.
- Political philosophy: State's role: protect from aggression, not manage every detail of life.

## VIII. Lewis on Democracy, Progressivism, and the Total State

### A. Democracy rightly and wrongly understood

- Lewis's small-d democracy: Democratic element as check on human sin, not celebration of universal wisdom.
- Lewis's small-d democracy: Spread power thinly because no one is fully trustworthy.
- Lewis's critique of ideological democracy: Rousseau as "father of the totalitarians."
- Lewis's critique: Idolatrous view: "Everyone must be consulted; consensus is law."
- Lewis's critique: Fear of unbridled majority rule; Founders also dreaded pure democracy.
- Balanced systems: British monarchy, House of Lords, and Commons as mixed constitution.
- Balanced systems: U.S. system: democratic House, initially state-appointed Senate, and President as rotating "king."

### B. Narnia's critique of bureaucracy and progressivism

- The Scrub family and Experiment House (*Voyage of the Dawn Treader* and *The Silver Chair*): "Up-to-date" progressive parents: first names, vegetarianism, teetotaling, "health" fads.
- Progressive schooling at Experiment House: child-centered theory leads to bullying and chaos.
- Progressive headmistress: favors manipulative bullies; discipline is absent.
- Ending: investigation exposes failures; headmistress drifts into higher administration and then Parliament.
- Governor Gumpas and the slave trade: Bureaucrat who has normalized slavery in his domain.
- When Caspian ends the trade, Gumpas accuses him of being "anti-progress."
- Link between bureaucratic mentality and tolerance of human exploitation.
- *That Hideous Strength* and NICE: National Institute of Coordinated Experiments as "peaceful" total state.
- NICE: Uses crises and technocratic rhetoric to justify comprehensive control.
- NICE: Parallels to "never let a crisis go to waste" politics.
- Lewis's hatred of bureaucracy: Prefers bats to bureaucrats in *The Screwtape Letters*.
- Lewis's hatred: Essays on petty regulations (e.g., needing permits to cut down one's own tree).
- Lewis's hatred: Warning: Those who fuss over bureaucratic details often aim to enslave.

### C. Crises, conscription, and the "willing slaves"

- Essay "A Dream" (war-time conscription and parades): Wartime emergency: all hands to the pumps, temporary powers.
- Post-war bureaucrats want to keep apparatus and compulsion, even without real danger.
- Owners of the "ship" eventually rebel when they realize they have lost freedom without lasting security.
- "The Willing Slaves of the Welfare State": Lewis's fear that citizens will trade liberty for mild security and benefits.
- Result: Soft despotism, extensive control by benevolent-sounding institutions.

## IX. Religion, Civilization, and Government: Narnia vs. Calormen

### A. The triad: God, culture, and government

- Lewis insists: Who you worship shapes your culture, which shapes your political order.
- Narnia and Archenland: Worship Aslan, the true king.
- Narnia and Archenland: Result: Free cultures that value live-and-let-live and limited government.
- Narnia and Archenland: Kings' primary tasks: keep peace, fight giants, defend from invasion.
- Calormen (and Tashban): Worship Tash, a demonic bird-god.
- Calormen: Society of hierarchy without mercy: slaves, oppressed poor, and flattery of the Tisroc ("may he live forever").
- Calormen: Government: absolute dictatorship, crushing the weak.

### B. Eustace's misplaced admiration

- Raised in progressive but nominally Christian England, he prefers Calormen as "least phony."
- Lewis's point: Progressives often gravitate toward overtly tyrannical regimes.
- Visual cues: veils, oriental luxury, minarets—clear parallels to the Ottoman Empire and Islamic civilization.

### C. Slavery and overlapping tyrannies

- Narnian slave trade tied to Calormen as destination.
- Shasta as quasi-slave to Arsheesh despite being his "son."
- Tyranny as both cultural and economic system.

## X. Women, War, and Political Order in Narnia

### A. Father Christmas, Lucy, and the ugliness of women in war

- Lucy receives dagger and cordial; she wants more active combat role.
- Father Christmas's line: "Battles are ugly when women fight."
- Principle: Women's involvement in direct combat is a sign of extremity and crisis, not a norm.

### B. Female courage within proper order

- Susan and Lucy as queens: They occupy thrones and wield real influence.
- Susan as archer; Lucy as healer and occasional fighter.
- Aravis and Jill: Aravis: valiant, clearly from a warrior culture but not a soldier herself.
- Jill fighting in *The Last Battle* only when pushed to the wall as last line of defense.
- Default assumption: Men die defending women; women are not the primary combatants.

### C. Application to political and cultural conflict

- Normal, healthy society: godly women generally not drawn into front-line political combat.
- In times of crisis: If choice is between a progressive politician and a principled Christian woman, support for the latter can be justified.
- But, where there is a faithful man available, he is preferable for the magistrate's office.
- Analogy with wartime stories (e.g., Molly Pitcher at the cannon): Heroic exceptions are admirable in emergencies.
- They do not overturn the overall creation order.

## XI. Aristocracy, Excellence, and Education

### A. Aristocracy vs. egalitarianism

- Aristocracy properly understood: rule by the excellent (virtue, wisdom, capacity).
- Modern aversion to aristocracy as a product of modernity, envy, and radical egalitarianism.
- Lewis's argument in *A Preface to Paradise Lost*: Rejecting hierarchies is like rejecting gravity; you only displace, not abolish, rank.
- Lewis's argument: If citizens cannot imagine kneeling or receiving a genuflection, they are "trousered apes" or "prosaic barbarians."

### B. Envy as spiritual acid

- Healthy posture: delight in those above you while serving those below you (noblesse oblige).
- Unhealthy posture: chafing at superiors and gloating over inferiors, producing dangerous leaders.
- Screwtape's analysis ("Screwtape Proposes a Toast"): "I'm as good as you" as demonic slogan of egalitarian resentment.

### C. Education for democracy vs. democratic education

- Lewis's key distinction: Education that preserves democracy must be non-democratic (aimed at excellence).
- Lewis's key distinction: Education that democracy "likes" tends toward leveling, grade inflation, and participation trophies.
- Consequences of leveling education: Excellence becomes impossible; best students are shackled to the slowest.
- Long-term effect: collapse of civic infrastructure and capacity.
- Aristocratic education as safeguard: Train a true elite of virtuous, skilled leaders.
- Such citizens can then sustain a healthy, limited democracy.

### D. Narnian images of aristocratic formation

- Bree training Shasta to "ride like a nobleman" in *The Horse and His Boy*: raising him into a warrior's bearing.
- Parallel to modern classical education (e.g., New Saint Andrews): Intent: form cultural leaders (aristoi) who serve others rather than themselves.
- Leadership as service for scattered "sheep without a shepherd."

## XII. Nations, Borders, and Distinct Civilizations in Narnia

### A. Narnian geopolitical map

- Narnia and Archenland: small, free kingdoms with Aslan-centered worship.
- Calormen: large empire to the south, expansionist and tyrannical.
- Giants in the north: foreign threat requiring constant vigilance.

### B. Kingship and borders

- King's duty: protect borders from giants and hostile neighbors.
- Provide peace and security, not cradle-to-grave welfare.
- Hostility to "busy-bodies and interferers": let marsh-wiggles, coastal folk, and others live their own local cultures.

### C. Nations before God

- Analogy to Old Testament Israel among the nations: Israel: knows the true God but coexists with other nations.
- Friendly neighbors (Hiram of Tyre, Queen of Sheba) vs. hostile empires (Assyria, Babylon, Egypt).
- Narnia as Old Testament-like setting: No Great Commission; no command to conquer the world for Aslan.
- Each nation has a potential "genius" to be sanctified.
- *That Hideous Strength* and national "genius": Dimble's reflection: each people has its own form of the Logos and calling.
- Ransom warns against British exceptionalism: England, France, China, and others each must become obedient while remaining themselves.

### D. Intermarriage and cultural mixing

- Shasta and Aravis's marriage: union of Narnian/Archenlander and Calormene noble.
- Suggests that distinct civilizations can interrelate and blend under Aslan's providence without erasing their differences.

## XIII. Summary: Narnia as a Political Mirror

- Narnia shows law above the king, sacrificial kingship, and the danger of usurpation.
- It illustrates the necessity of early resistance to tyranny, under lawful authority and within constitutional structures.
- It warns against progressivism, bureaucracy, and the total state using vivid characters and stories.
- It affirms hierarchy, aristocracy, and excellence as necessary for freedom, while upholding fundamental human equality before Aslan.
- It offers a rich picture of nations, borders, and civilizations under God, resisting both imperial homogenization and rootless cosmopolitanism.

## Core Ideas

- Law is above rulers: kings, parliaments, and executives derive authority from an overarching moral and constitutional order and must themselves be under it.
- Legitimate resistance must be led by lesser magistrates and framed as obedience to law and to God, not as a raw grab for power.
- Modern progressivism, when wedded to bureaucracy and perpetual emergency, naturally tends toward soft (and sometimes hard) tyranny.
- True kingship and leadership are sacrificial: first into danger, last in retreat, and joyful under hardship, patterned on Aslan's self-giving.
- Hierarchy and aristocracy are inescapable; the question is whether they will be virtuous and service-oriented or envious, leveling, and abusive.
- Education that obliterates excellence and hierarchy destroys the foundations of a free society; aristocratic education protects genuine democracy.
- Religious allegiance shapes culture and government; worshiping the true God tends toward liberty, while false worship tends toward slavery and despotism.

## Key Terms

- **Rule of Law** — The principle that law stands above any ruler and constitutes their authority, binding king and commoner alike.
- **Divine Right of Kings** — The doctrine that a king's authority is absolute and unquestionable; Lewis and Rutherford explicitly reject this.
- **Lesser Magistrate** — A lower-ranking official (e.g., local ruler, legislature) who may lawfully lead resistance against a tyrannical higher authority.
- **Unwritten Constitution** — The set of customs, norms, and practices that, alongside written documents, shape a political order's functioning.
- **Progressivism** — Modern ideological movement that often expands state power in the name of progress, equality, and expertise; in Lewis's view, closely linked to tyranny.
- **Total State** — A political regime aiming to organize and control every aspect of life, often under technocratic or humanitarian rhetoric.
- **Aristocracy** — Governance by the "best" (in virtue and competence), contrasted with egalitarian leveling and mediocrity.
- **Noblesse Oblige** — The moral duty of those in higher positions to serve and protect those below them, not exploit them.
- **Bureaucracy** — Administrative system of offices and rules which, when dominant, can become a dehumanizing instrument of control.
- **Welfare State** — Political order in which the state takes extensive responsibility for citizens' welfare, which Lewis feared could turn them into "willing slaves."

## People

- **C.S. Lewis** — Author of the Narnia books and many essays, whose Christian and political thought intertwines in a critique of tyranny, progressivism, and egalitarianism.
- **King Lune (Lon)** — Archenland's king in *The Horse and His Boy*, model of sacrificial, cheerful, and courtly kingship under law.
- **Shasta/Cor** — Lost prince raised as a quasi-slave; learns true nobility and responsibility and becomes future king of Archenland.
- **Aslan** — Christ-figure and true king of Narnia whose sacrificial death and ultimate authority ground all just kingship.
- **Miraz** — Usurping Telmarine ruler whose title "Lord Protector" echoes Cromwell and raises questions of legitimacy.
- **Edmund Burke** — Statesman who opposed the French Revolution but defended the American cause as lawful resistance, aligning with Lewis's constitutional concerns.
- **Oliver Cromwell** — English leader whose "Lord Protector" role and complex legacy appear as a political "Easter egg" in Narnia.
- **The White Witch (Jadis)** — Tyrannical usurper who rules Narnia for a hundred years with secret police and perpetual winter.
- **Eustace Scrubb** — Progressive English boy whose upbringing and sympathies (e.g., for Calormen) embody Lewis's critique of modern progressivism.
- **Governor Gumpas** — Bureaucrat who normalizes the slave trade in his island domain and cloaks injustice in language of "progress."

## Dates / Periods

- **Seventeenth Century England** — Era of conflicts between crown and Parliament, execution of Charles I, Cromwell's Protectorate, and the Glorious Revolution; background for Lewis's views on law above kings.
- **American Revolutionary Era (c. 1760s–1780s)** — Period when colonists resisted parliamentary overreach, appealing to charters and the English constitution.
- **World War II (1939–1945)** — Context for Lewis's fiction and essays; bombings, conscription, and wartime emergency powers shape his fears about permanent state expansion.
- **Battle of Britain (1940)** — Air battle over London; emblem of courage and heroism, paralleling moral heroism in Narnia.

## Geography

- **Narnia** — Fantasy kingdom that models a Christian, free, and limited political order under Aslan's kingship.
- **Archenland** — Neighboring human kingdom, allied to Narnia, representing a free but more human-centered polity.
- **Calormen (Tashban)** — Southern empire with Islamic/Ottoman characteristics; depicts a tyrannical, hierarchical, and slave-based political order.
- **England (mid-20th century)** — Real-world setting of evacuated children and wartime Britain that frames the entry into Narnia and Lewis's reflections on law and state power.
- **Experiment House** — Progressive school in Narnia's world that illustrates educational and disciplinary failures of modern egalitarian ideology.

## Expected Outcome

After taking notes from this outline and watching or listening to the source material, an **A+ student** would be capable of:

### Knowing

- The central Narnian principle that the king is under the law and that law itself stands above all rulers.
- How Lewis connects Narnian narratives to real historical debates about monarchy, Parliament, and constitutional order in England and America.
- The Reformation doctrine of the lesser magistrate and how it structures legitimate resistance to tyranny.
- Lewis's critique of progressivism, bureaucracy, and the "total state," including how crises are used to expand and cement state power.
- The role of hierarchy, aristocracy, and excellence in sustaining a free and stable society, and the dangers of egalitarian leveling in education.
- How worship, culture, and government interlock in Lewis's picture of Narnia, Archenland, and Calormen, and how that parallels Christian vs. non-Christian civilizations.

### Reciting

- A clear summary (30–60 seconds) of King Lune's definition of kingship and how it models sacrificial leadership.
- A concise explanation of why the American colonists, in Lewis's and Burke's view, were defending the English constitution rather than rebelling lawlessly.
- A brief account of how Narnia's political "Easter eggs" (e.g., Miraz, Experiment House, Gumpas, NICE) embody Lewis's worries about modern progressivism and bureaucracy.
- A thumbnail sketch of the difference between democratic safeguards (checks and balances) and ideological democracy (Rousseau-style egalitarianism).

### Sharing

- Teaching others how stories like Narnia can shape political imagination, making constitutional principles and moral truths vivid and memorable.
- Explaining to peers how Lewis's doctrine of law above the king undercuts both absolute monarchy and unconstrained majoritarian democracy.
- Guiding a discussion group through the connections between worship (Aslan vs. Tash), culture (free vs. slavish), and government (limited vs. total state).
- Presenting a structured overview of Lewis's arguments for aristocratic education and why pursuing excellence is essential for sustaining liberty.

### Conversing

- Engaging in a thoughtful debate on when resistance to government is legitimate, drawing on Narnia, the American founding, and Reformation political theology.
- Comparing Lewis's concerns about bureaucracy and perpetual emergency powers with contemporary debates about public health, security, and surveillance.
- Discussing the proper roles of men and women in war and politics, using Narnian examples to nuance both complementarian and egalitarian claims.
- Analyzing modern political movements in light of Lewis's insights into envy, egalitarianism, and the temptation to trade freedom for security, and connecting these insights to current policy and cultural trends.
