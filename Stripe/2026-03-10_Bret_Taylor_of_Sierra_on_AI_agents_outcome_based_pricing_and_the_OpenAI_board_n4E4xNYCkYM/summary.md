# Summary

Bret Taylor (Sierra CEO, OpenAI board chair) discusses how AI agents are changing established companies: agents need rich, shared context (not starved orchestrators), and the “agent harness” (skills, docs, rules) may matter as much as APIs for future SaaS. Sierra powers customer-experience agents (phone, chat) with outcome-based pricing (pay per resolved case; escalation free), shifting service from cost center to scalable, often top-line-focused investment. Applied AI has enduring value because buyers want solutions and purpose-built product, not raw models. AI productivity is about processes and ownership—the atomic unit is the process, not the person—and organizing by process with clear accountability lets companies capture value instead of “shipping org charts.” High-agency, customer-caring generalists gain an “exoskeleton”; systems of record that are ledgers stay durable, while “systems of engagement” may be challenged when agents do the work.

# Core Question

Where does value live when AI agents perform labor—in the database (system of record) or in the process and outcome? And how should companies organize (by department vs. by process) and price (usage vs. outcome) to align incentives and capture that value?

# Key Concepts

- **Agent harness** — Context, skills, documentation, and rules given to an agent so it can act effectively; distinct from raw API or UI; future SaaS may expose harnesses alongside APIs.
- **Outcome-based pricing** — Charge for business results (e.g., resolved case, sale) not usage (tokens); aligns vendor and client and pulls vendors into the last mile of implementation.
- **Process, not person** — The atomic unit of productivity for AI is the process; organizing around processes with owners and narrowing domains turns “science into engineering.”
- **Applied AI** — Solutions for specific buyers and processes have enduring value; companies buy solutions, not models; product, GTM, and ecosystem matter even as models improve.
- **Systems of record** — When agents perform labor, ledger/ERP-style systems stay more durable; “systems of engagement” (CRM, marketing) may be weakened as value shifts to process and outcome.
- **Multi-agent vs. context** — Multi-agent designs that give subagents context but starve the orchestrator produce robotic experiences; single (or few) agents with rich, shared context (e.g., OpenClaw-style) can feel “right.”

# Why It Matters

Leaders are deciding how to adopt AI (by department vs. by process), how to price and partner (outcome-based vs. usage-based), and where durable value sits (systems of record vs. process/agent). Getting this right affects competitive positioning, vendor accountability, and who benefits from AI productivity—and whether “adoption” is an imperative or a real advantage.
