When it comes to the opinion of the people who "matter", the Shakers are hip: they had all the correct views—sustainable agriculture, gender equality, and they even reduced their carbon footprint to almost zero. Today, Shaker villages appear to be populated mostly by supporters of National Public Radio. Their appeal to the NPR crowd isn’t just about eco-friendliness and gender parity, though. The Shakers’ clear rejection of tradition—especially their turning away from inherited family structure, procreation, and sacramental or patriarchal religious norms—is central to the fascination. In contrast to the Amish, who uphold strong family ties and continuity with tradition, the Shakers’ radical breaks with religious and social convention allow progressive audiences to see them as proto-modern: open to remaking society and faith along lines compatible with contemporary values. Amish communities do not seem to have the same appeal; the NPR crowd often gravitates to the Shakers’ egalitarianism and their willingness to question core assumptions about the value of children and whether we should have them.

The Shakers emerged from 18th-century English Quakerism, sharing its emphasis on the inward work of the Spirit and its rejection of traditional sacraments such as baptism and the Lord’s Supper. Like the Quakers, they did not maintain a formal preaching office, instead allowing Spirit-led exhortation and testimony within the community. Over time, however, Shaker worship developed into a highly structured and communal form, marked by coordinated singing, dancing, and ritualized movement rather than open-ended silence.

Shaker communities were also notable for their commitment to gender-balanced leadership. Men and women served in parallel roles as elders and eldresses, and women exercised real authority within an organized and clearly defined leadership structure.

While these features arose in a distinct historical setting, some observers note parallels in certain modern church contexts: a tendency to de-emphasize formal preaching or sacramental life, a preference for more participatory or experience-driven gatherings, and leadership dynamics in which formal titles do not always reflect functional authority. 

In this light, the comparison highlights a broader interpretive question about the relationship between structured forms of worship—such as ordained ministry and sacramental practice—and more fluid, Spirit-centered expressions of Christian life.
