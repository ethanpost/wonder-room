# The Obvious Problem That No One Can Agree On

## What is Newcomb's Paradox?

### Setup

- You enter a room with a supercomputer and two boxes: one open with $1,000, one closed (mystery box).
- The supercomputer is highly accurate; it has correctly predicted the choices of thousands of people in this same scenario.
- You may take both boxes (mystery + $1,000) or only the mystery box.

### How the mystery box is filled

- Before you entered, the supercomputer predicted your choice.
- If it predicted you would take only the mystery box → it put $1 million in the mystery box.
- If it predicted you would take both boxes → it put nothing in the mystery box.
- The prediction is already made; the boxes are set. The predictor's only goal is accuracy.

### The paradox

- People divide almost evenly: one-boxers (take only mystery box) vs. two-boxers (take both).
- Each side finds the opposite choice "silly" (Robert Nozick).
- Named for William Newcomb. Guardian poll (2016): 31,000+ respondents — 53.5% one-boxers, 46.5% two-boxers.

## The one-boxer argument (Pick one box)

### Expected utility reasoning

- Let C = probability the computer predicted correctly; (1 − C) = probability it was wrong.
- Two-boxing: expected value = C × $1,000 + (1 − C) × $1,001,000.
- One-boxing: expected value = C × $1,000,000 + (1 − C) × $0 = $1,000,000 × C.
- Equating the two gives a break-even C ≈ 0.5005 (50.05%).

### Conclusion

- If the predictor is better than chance, one-boxing has higher expected utility.
- Given prior evidence (thousands of accurate predictions), one-boxing is the rational choice.
- One-boxers rely on evidential decision theory: use probabilities based on prior evidence of predictor accuracy.

## The two-boxer argument (Pick both)

### Strategic dominance

- The boxes are already set; your choice now cannot change whether the mystery box contains $0 or $1 million.
- Four outcomes: ($0 in mystery → one-box $0, two-box $1,000); ($1M in mystery → one-box $1M, two-box $1,001,000).
- In every case, two-boxing yields $1,000 more (or avoids $0 when mystery is empty).
- Dominant strategy: always two-box.

### Causal decision theory

- Only factor in things you can cause; your decision cannot influence the past.
- Let P = probability (before prediction) that the predictor thought you would one-box.
- Expected utility one-box = $1,000,000 × P; two-box = $1,000 + $1,000,000 × P.
- Two-boxing always adds $1,000; therefore two-boxing is rational.

### One-boxing framed as "wishful thinking"

- Believing your present choice changes the past.

## Decision theory: why two valid answers?

### Two true statements that pull in opposite directions

- Evidence: almost everyone who one-boxed became a millionaire; two-boxers left poor.
- Causal fact: the prediction and box contents were fixed before you decided; your choice cannot change them.

### Hidden assumptions

- One-boxers: use evidential decision theory (prior evidence of predictor accuracy).
- Two-boxers: use causal decision theory (only consider what you can cause).

### Veritasium audience poll

- >24,000 responses; about two-thirds one-boxers.

## Newcomb's paradox and free will

### Winning the maximum ($1,001,000)

- You would need to be the type who would one-box (so predictor puts $1M in) but then two-box at the last moment.
- If the predictor is perfect (100% accurate), that is impossible.

### Implication for free will

- If a perfect predictor exists, nothing you do between entering and choosing changes what was predetermined.
- One view: free will is an illusion, but the world operates as if it is real — so we must act as though it exists.

### Practical constraint

- You cannot redesign society (e.g., punishment) on the assumption that free will is illusory without changing incentives (e.g., crime).
- Regardless of the metaphysics, you still must choose one box or two.

## What does it mean to be rational?

### The "Why Ain'cha Rich?" argument

- If the goal is more money, one-boxers do better; two-boxers end up with $1,000.
- But "rational" might refer to the act, not the outcome — Gibbard and Harper (1978): the rational act is to two-box, though two-boxers fare worse.
- They say the game is "rigged": if a good predictor rewards predicted irrationality, irrationality is richly rewarded.

### Tension between rational person and rational act

- Sometimes to be rational as a person you must perform an act that looks irrational in the moment.
- Analogy: prisoner's dilemma — defection dominates in one shot; cooperation pays in repeated play.
- Rational society can be full of cooperators (one-boxers); rational person in isolation might defect (two-box).

### When a two-boxer would one-box

- If your choice could causally change the past (e.g., predictor uses a "wormhole" to the future).
- If there are multiple trials — reputation matters; one-boxing gets you predicted as a one-boxer and $1M in this or later rounds.
- If you can pre-commit (e.g., tell the predictor before it predicts) — then commit to one box and keep your word.

## Mutually assured destruction (MAD)

### Historical context

- USSR first nuclear test (RDS-1): August 29, 1949.
- Arms race: by mid-1960s, US had 30,000+ warheads; USSR 6,000+.
- Robert McNamara (US Secretary of Defense): "assured destruction" — deter attack by maintaining ability to inflict unacceptable damage.

### MAD as pre-commitment

- If either side attacks first, the other retaliates → mutual annihilation.
- Commitment to retaliate is beneficial because it deters the attack.

### Cold War president dilemma

- You have publicly committed to retaliate. Soviets have launched (real attack). Launch → best case both countries die; worst case nuclear winter.
- "Rational" in-the-moment choice: do not launch (avoid more death). But you pre-committed to launch.
- Voters may prefer a leader who credibly commits to pressing the button, while hoping they would not actually do it — risk if that becomes known.

### Game of chicken and Dr. Strangelove

- Chicken: best for you if the other swerves; worst if neither swerves. Optimal strategy: visibly remove the steering wheel (pre-commit to not swerving).
- Dr. Strangelove (1963): USSR's "doomsday device" — triggers automatically on attack or tampering, kills everyone. Tampering switch prevents the Russians themselves from backing out.
- Device works only if its existence is known; that is the point of the film.

## Precommitment as the ultimate strategy

### Common thread

- In Newcomb and MAD, the best outcome follows from pre-committing to a "worse" option: one-boxing gets $1M+; MAD yields tense but stable peace.
- Rationality may be less about the single choice in the moment and more about which rules to live by.

### Rule-based framing

- Question: what rules ought one follow? If you could set your own "program," what rules would you wire yourself to obey?
- Answer: become the kind of agent that acts in line with the commitments it would have been good to make had you known about the problem.
- In Newcomb, the good pre-commitment would have been one-boxing; so if you are already wired to honor such commitments, you one-box "as if" you had pre-committed.

### Iterated / reputational view

- Treat it like repeated interaction: for every future predictor or situation, you want to live up to ideal pre-commitments.
- Building a reputation as someone who keeps commitments is beneficial beyond this single game.
- Reframing in this way can move some two-boxers to one-boxing.

### Core takeaway

- Newcomb's paradox turns on whether a strong correlation (predictor's accuracy) that you know is not causal should influence your decision.
- Distinguishing causation from correlation is hard and applies beyond the paradox (e.g., drug trials, inference from data).

## Core Ideas

- Newcomb's paradox splits people into one-boxers and two-boxers because two valid decision frameworks (evidential vs. causal) yield opposite recommendations while both use true premises.
- Evidential decision theory uses prior evidence (predictor accuracy) and favors one-boxing; causal decision theory only considers what you can cause and favors two-boxing.
- Rationality can conflict across levels: the rational act in isolation (two-box) can be worse for the agent than the "irrational" act (one-box) when the world rewards the latter, and a rational society may consist of one-boxers.
- Pre-commitment to a seemingly worse option can produce better outcomes in both Newcomb (one-box → $1M) and real-world strategy (MAD → deterrence).
- The paradox touches free will (perfect prediction implies no last-moment change), but we must still act as if we have free will for practical and moral reasons.
- Choosing which rules to live by—and wiring oneself to honor good pre-commitments—can be more important than optimizing the single choice in the moment.
- Distinguishing causation from correlation is central to the paradox and to broader problems of inference (e.g., medicine, policy).

## Key Terms

- **Newcomb's paradox** — Thought experiment: predictor fills a mystery box based on your future choice; you choose one or two boxes; evidential and causal reasoning give opposite answers.
- **One-boxer** — Someone who takes only the mystery box, relying on evidence of predictor accuracy (evidential decision theory).
- **Two-boxer** — Someone who takes both boxes, relying on dominance and the fact that the contents are already set (causal decision theory).
- **Expected utility (EU)** — Weighted sum of outcomes by probability; used by both camps with different probability inputs.
- **Evidential decision theory** — Uses probabilities that reflect evidence (e.g., accuracy of predictor); one-boxing follows.
- **Causal decision theory** — Uses only probabilities for outcomes you can cause; two-boxing follows.
- **Strategic dominance** — A strategy that is better in every possible state of the world; two-boxing is dominant in the causal reading.
- **"Why Ain'cha Rich?" argument** — If one-boxers get rich and two-boxers get $1,000, why call two-boxing rational? Highlights tension between rational act and best outcome.
- **Prisoner's dilemma** — One-shot: defection dominates; repeated: cooperation can be better; analogy for one-boxer vs. two-boxer at individual vs. societal level.
- **Pre-commitment** — Binding yourself in advance to a course of action (e.g., one-box, retaliate) to improve outcomes.
- **Mutually assured destruction (MAD)** — Deterrence strategy: each side can destroy the other; commitment to retaliate prevents attack.
- **Assured destruction** — McNamara's idea: maintain reliable ability to inflict unacceptable damage on an aggressor to deter attack.
- **Game of chicken** — Neither wants to yield; visibly removing the option to swerve (pre-commitment) can force the other to yield.

## People

- **William Newcomb** — Originator of Newcomb's paradox.
- **Robert Nozick** — American philosopher; noted that people find the "correct" choice obvious but split evenly and think the other side is silly.
- **Gibbard and Harper** — Philosophers; 1978 paper arguing the rational act is to two-box while acknowledging two-boxers fare worse and calling the setup "rigged."
- **Robert McNamara** — US Secretary of Defense; advocated "assured destruction" (later MAD) rather than disarmament.
- **Dr. Strangelove** — 1963 film; Russians build an automatic doomsday device; tampering switch prevents them from having second thoughts; device must be known to deter.
