# Summary

A supercomputer has already predicted your choice and filled a mystery box ($0 or $1 million) accordingly; you can take one box or both (the other has $1,000). One-boxers use evidential decision theory and prior evidence of the predictor’s accuracy to choose the single box and typically get rich; two-boxers use causal decision theory and dominance (your choice can’t change the past) and take both, but usually get only $1,000. Both sides rely on true premises but reach opposite conclusions. The paradox turns on whether a strong non-causal correlation should guide your decision, and it connects to rationality, pre-commitment (e.g., MAD), and the tension between the “rational act” and the best outcome.

# Core Question

Should you take one box or both in Newcomb’s paradox—and more broadly, should a correlation you know is not causal (the predictor’s accuracy) influence your decision?

# Key Concepts

- **Evidential vs. causal decision theory** — One uses evidence-based probabilities (favors one-boxing); the other only what you can cause (favors two-boxing).
- **Strategic dominance** — Two-boxing is better in every state of the world if the contents are already fixed; one-boxing can still yield better outcomes when the predictor is accurate.
- **Pre-commitment** — Committing in advance to a “worse” option (one box, or retaliate) can produce better results, as in Newcomb and MAD.
- **Rational act vs. rational outcome** — The “rational” move in the moment (two-box) can leave you worse off; the “irrational” move (one-box) can be rewarded.
- **Correlation vs. causation** — The paradox centers on whether to act on a correlation (predictor accuracy) that you know is not causal.

# Why It Matters

Newcomb’s paradox clarifies how we ought to decide when evidence and causality pull in different directions—relevant to policy, medicine (e.g., drug trials), and strategy. It shows that “rationality” can depend on whether we optimize the single act or the long-run outcome, and that pre-commitment and reputation matter in repeated or high-stakes situations (deterrence, cooperation, trust).
